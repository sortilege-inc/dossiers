Celenneth of the Dúnedain

Daughter of the North · Messenger of Gandalf the Grey · Heir of Arnor · Bearer of Nurhael's Warden Blade · She Who Ran at Nine and Stopped Running at Thirty-Eight

Rangers of the North Calling: Messenger Patron: Gandalf the Grey Safe Haven: The Angle Standard of Living: Common

A woman past her first youth, tall and lean in the Dúnedain manner, auburn-haired beneath a plain Númenórean helm. The mithril shirt beneath her travel-stained leathers is Balin's gift, made in Moria; it has taken wounds meant for her body more than once. The Warden Blade at her hip is Nurhael's, recovered from the long-dead heir of Arnor whose crown she did not honour with reverence.

She speaks little. When she does, the room attends.

I. Identity & Standing

Celenneth is a Ranger of the North in the service of no lord but the watch itself, sworn obliquely to Gandalf the Grey, who calls her by name and trusts her with sealed parchment whose symbols faintly shimmer. She has the blood of Arnor in her — not as a claim or a banner, but as a fact carried in her body and her blade. Her standing is Common; her work is uncommon. The Dúnedain do not announce themselves.

Bearing and Manner

She is composed to the point of opacity. The world reads her as severe; those who travel with her learn the difference between severity and care economised. She does not perform feeling, but does not deny it either when honestly asked. She moves with a ranger's economy — no gesture wasted, no posture aimed at being seen. The Dúnedain reflex of act, do not explain is so ingrained that she had to consciously teach herself the words I love you, I missed you both, I don't know how to do this. Each was earned across years.

The First Sentence Anyone Says of Her

That she is reliable. That she is alone too much, or used to be. That she once rode through Moria with a brazen head in her pack and a wound in her side, and came out with mithril and a sword of Arnor and more Shadow than she went in carrying. The Dúnedain count her among their own without ceremony; the Rohirrim named her wife Linnea of Bree in the hall of a marshal who became Brynja's father by acclamation; the elves of Rivendell and Lindon receive her as a kinswoman whose lineage they do not need to ask about; Bree treats her as a stranger who passes through with coin and a sharp eye.

Current Errand & Inner Weight

To carry the messages the Grey Pilgrim entrusts to her, and to bring the Free Peoples toward the alliance the Shadow forbids. Current calling — Messenger
To sail west with the family she did not believe she could have, and to open with her blood whatever has waited in the warded ruin for an heir of Arnor to come. Current heart — Book XIII outset

Personal Maxims

Cultural Blessing, Calling, Features

Cultural Blessing Kings of Men, Allegiance of the Dúnedain. The inheritance of Númenor and the duty of Arnor in a single granted line. It is why Celenneth reads weathered runes without being taught and why the Herald's Boots accept her stride. The blessing is also why the Warden Blade answered her grip when she lifted it from Nurhael's bones.
Calling Messenger. She carries word where word must be carried. Gandalf chose her for this; Elrond's summonses arrive for her; Bain heard her plea at Dale, Thalion sealed his alliance, the Steward of Gondor heard her out with considered it shall be. The mission to Rohan, the mission to Dale, the mission to Minas Tirith — each is the Calling at work.
Distinctive Features Swift. Secretive. Folk-Lore (Messenger granted). The first carries her across miles; the second carries her past patrols; the third lets her read the old stones, recognise what a Númenórean breastplate is when she puts one on, and chant the binding curse in the Frost Maw from ranger archives studied in childhood.
Shadow Path Wandering Madness. The Witch-king's curse, set on her by the cave at the very first session, has named the shape of her vulnerability. Her dreams are not idle. Her road is not a metaphor.

IV. Operating Doctrine

The Action Speaks

She inherited from Brandor and Orlec the conviction that loyalty is demonstrated and not declared. The clean tactical solution is preferred over the dramatic one: circle the camp, cut the ropes, whisper stay quiet, and be gone before the bandits know. Her finest acts in the chronicle have all been quiet — the spear stolen from an orc camp in silence, the binding curse chanted from memory in a frozen chamber, the healing draught carried for years and then offered without speech. The Dúnedain do not announce themselves; she does not announce herself even to those who love her, and they have learned to read the silence.

The Single Watch

She holds the watch until relieved. This is the structural fact of her character, more fundamental than skill ratings or virtues. She set her own broken leg in Fangorn because the watch could not be paused. She rode home through a Rohirrim winter early because Linnea and Brynja were the watch she now stood. She walked into Moria alone because no one else was available, and she came out only because the work was done. Across thirteen books she has never failed to complete what she undertook, and never been able to say plainly that any of it has cost her.

Hidden in Plain Visibility

The Secretive feature is not stealth. It is the discipline of carrying what she carries — the chain scars, the iron eye, the cursed dagger, the dead sister's name, the pregnancy of a relationship and a child that have permanently changed the shape of her — without disclosing any of it to the world. She is read as severe because what she contains would, if shown, change every conversation. Linnea is the one person allowed inside that economy, and the right to be there was earned across two books before it was granted. The Garin conversation in Book VII — hold me while you tell me everything — is the operating manual: full honesty inside the inner circle, full opacity outside it, and the two are not in tension.

The Body as Instrument

She does not display her body and she does not preserve it. It is the means by which the watch is held, the family carried, the road completed. Wounds are noted, tended, and moved past. Pregnancy, when it came, removed her pragmatic control and she had to learn to receive the body's transformation as something other than failure of capability. Linnea taught her this with patience and specificity over thirteen books, and the learning is one of the chronicle's central arcs. The body that crossed Moria alone and the body that nursed Hallas by the fire in the Angle are the same body, transformed.

Giving What Was Given

She does not hoard. The chronicle has built a moral architecture around divestiture — the chalice given to the Dúnedain holy spring rather than kept, the lorebook placed with Almedra, the brooch left on Martha and Luthan's mantel without announcement, the sword of her younger years passed to Eadric. Each gift is significant precisely because the receiving was significant. The circuit of gift and giving is what binds her to the world; accumulation would have been a kind of severance.

V. Interior Architecture

Sources of Formation

The Sister Seven years old. Brave. Foolish. Taken from a wood when Celenneth was nine, and never seen again. Named once in the chronicle and never again. The wound the entire architecture is built around — every road, every refusal to stay, every watch held alone. I ran. The chronicle is honest that this wound is permanent.
Brandor & Orlec The two rangers who found her in the woods when her legs gave out, and raised her into the watch. Two men whose partnership across decades was the language of partnership without the language of declaration — the model from which Celenneth learned that loyalty is action and that love does not need conventional categories. Found dead in the Frost Maw in Book X; Orlec dying mid-warning to her, Brandor choosing to follow him with I can't leave him alone in this cold earth. Buried together, hands clasped, in the northern hills.
The Ranger Archives The scrolls and codices and stone-rubbings she studied as a child while the elder rangers watched her without comment. The reason she reads Númenórean runes by instinct, recalls the binding curse for the wraiths in the Frost Maw, and recognises the make of weathered armour. Brandor's archive in her body.
Gandalf the Grey Her Patron. Met her once when she was a child studying old stones and weathered runes near Fornost; remembered her name. Found her again in Bree and trusted her with sealed parchment and a name. He commands nothing; he trusts. The distinction is structural.
Linnea of Bree The wagon prisoner she cut free without asking thanks. The companion who was supposed to be dropped at the next village. The wife by the Binding of Aegis. The mother of Brynja by adoption and of Hallas by birth. Her Fellowship focus, unchanged across thirteen books. The person who taught her to be held.

Inherited Doctrine

The single-watch endurance is paternal pedagogy from Brandor — taught and modelled by example, never explained. The unorthodox model of love is Brandor and Orlec made operational across a generation. The reading of ruins is the archive made internal. The refusal to declare is the Dúnedain register itself, which she received as a child and has carried as her register for the world. Her doctrine is not original synthesis but inherited instrumentation, refined in her own practice across years.

Courage and Fear

Her Valour is real and operational. Her fear is for what she has now built. The Shadow's argument against her early in the chronicle was the loneliness of the road; the Shadow's argument against her now is loss — the specific terror that Linnea or Brynja or Hallas might be taken from her, that she might be made again into the girl in the woods. The dream of Linnea drowning while she stood frozen on the shore was the Shadow's clearest move. She woke from it shaking, and Linnea held her, and said you won't lose me. It's not allowed. The fear is permanent. The vow against it is also permanent.

Action vs Word

She is fluent in action and slow in words. The phrase I love you first arrived under stars in the Woodland Realm, after a feast that had freed her from the human anxieties she carried; it took the elves' suspension of ordinary time for her to say what she had already been doing for two books. I missed you both arrived after a two-day ride home through a Rohirrim winter. I don't know how to do this arrived in Linnea's arms after months of refusal. Each of these sentences is a small revolution. She speaks them rarely. She means each of them absolutely.

VI. Fellowship & Bonds

Linnea — wife, bound by the AegisBree-folk
Cut free from a bandit's wagon at the beginning of Book II; rode east beside her ever since. The woman who was meant to be a waypoint and became the centre. The Binding of Aegis sealed at Yule in Rohan made warrior's vow what marriage would have made ornament: I swear to stand as your shield, to face the darkness at your side, and to guard your life with my own. Her instinct for people is the necessary complement to Celenneth's instinct for terrain. She has spoken before Bain, Thalion, the Steward of Gondor, and Eofred; she has fought shadow wolves; she has spotted overhangs in two blizzards and bitterroot for Brynja's fever; she has held Celenneth through the dream of her own drowning. She is the chronicle's other protagonist, and the chronicle knows it.
Brynja — elder daughter, of Rohan and the DúnedainFoundling, Riddermark
Infant found near the Anduin in the Riddermark. Eofred said Rohan shall be her father; Celenneth answered her name will be Brynja, and we will raise her to protect others. The adoption was made the morning after the confession of the sister — running once, refused twice. Brynja is now five and a half, has watched her mothers fight cultists and corrupted men, has been carried through a rockfall, has met Círdan the Shipwright at Mithlond, has set her hand on Celenneth's pregnant belly and said I'll make sure he's never scared at night. She is becoming a specific child and the chronicle has been scrupulous to render her that way.
Hallas — son, infantDúnedain, born in the Angle
Celenneth's first and only biological child, born after a five-hour labour in the Angle with Linnea acting as midwife from Freowyn's lessons in Rohan. Named for Hallas the ranger who fought beside them at the Frost Maw, who sat by a fire wondering aloud what connection felt like, and who spoke Celenneth's name with reverence at the moment she shattered. Our little Hallas. Welcome to our family. Brynja met him with I'm your big sister and a careful steadiness no one had taught her.
Gandalf the Grey — PatronIstari
He came to her in Bree at the end of Book I — sealed parchment, faint shimmering symbols, the line about her being a child when they last met. He does not command her; he trusts her, and the distinction lands hard every time he uses it. Reappeared in Book IX on the road, telling Linnea stories of young Celenneth studying weathered runes near Fornost. All who walk under these skies have stories to tell. Including yours, if I may say so.
Lord Elrond — summoner, healerRivendell
Received the brazen head of Dero from Balin via Celenneth. Sent her Nimroch the morning Orlen's letter arrived. Sent her the healing draught she carried unused for years until Linnea's broken arm on the plains of Rhovanion required it. Sent her the summons that broke open her question to Linnea about Bree. He sends, and she goes; this has been true across thirteen books, and Linnea has learned to release the pattern rather than resent it.
Brandor & Orlec — foster fathersRangers of the North
The two rangers who found her at nine and raised her. Their partnership was the unspoken architecture of her understanding of love. Found at last in the Frost Maw in Book X — Orlec dying mid-warning, Brandor choosing not to outlive him. Celenneth buried them together with their hands clasped, in the hills above the watchtower, and finished what they had begun. Book X's answer to the question the chronicle has asked since she was nine: was there anyone who came back for you? The answer is yes — they came back for her, and she came back for them.
Damrod & Hallas — ranger allies, parallel partnershipRangers of the North
Met in the snowfields above the Frost Maw, arrived as equals with their own mission rather than as rescuers. We'll help you root out whatever curses these lands; you help us into the Frost Maw. Hallas recognised Celenneth as Brandor and Orlec's protégé — the detail that made the alliance real. Hallas spoke her name with reverence at the moment she broke; she gave his name to her son. After the Frost Maw and the Watchtower, they rode east tracking the Forgotten Watchers — the chronicle's Dol Guldur thread continuing offscreen, the way the chronicle has always handled darker work being done by other hands.
Eryndil — summoner from LindonElf of the Western Shores
Sea-grey eyes, shell-beads in her hair, a blue cloak that caught light like water. Arrived at the Angle in Book XIII with patience rather than urgency — camped under birch trees, waited for the family to decide. Bears the request that directs them west: to a warded Númenórean island whose threshold requires Dúnedain blood to open. Loosed an arrow at a fleeing prisoner Celenneth had chosen to release — an act of pragmatism whose ethical weight Celenneth has not yet addressed and the sea voyage will not let her avoid. One of the chronicle's most carefully ambivalent figures.
Eorlas — herbalist, of more than he saysMirkwood
The wandering herbalist with staff raised who commanded a shadow-figure begone. Knowledge of poisons and herbs and old things; poison is only poison when misunderstood; the forest preys on our hearts. Gave her the elixir she still carries. Spoke of the past like someone who remembered it. The chronicle has never opened him fully, and the elixir in her pack is the more important of his gifts.
Balin — dwarven lord, fallenDwarves of Moria
Lord of the brief reclamation of Moria. Equipped her with the mithril shirt she still wears. Entrusted her with the brazen head of Dero for Rivendell. Substituted mithril for gold from the hoard far lighter to carry. Died, as he was always going to, while she was away. The chronicle handled the grief plainly when news reached her at Rivendell in Book VIII: he was a good friend. And a better leader than most gave him credit for. The mithril armour is the gift that has continued to do his work.
Eofred — marshal of the East-mark, Brynja's father by acclamationRohan
The Rohirrim lord who hosted them through a winter, witnessed the Binding of Aegis, and granted Celenneth's petition to adopt Brynja with Rohan shall be her father. Tested her urgency over Rohan's borders and was not moved; respected nonetheless. The right kind of obstacle: not a villain, not a fool, a man who has kept Rohan strong by not being rushed.
Garin — a woman of EdorasRohan
Met after Fangorn, on the open plains, drawn efficiently and with affection. You're as sharp as an arrow when it comes to spotting danger, but when someone pays you a compliment, you're as dull as a blunted sword. The chronicle's most careful handling of intimacy as character revelation: Celenneth told Linnea everything — hold me while you tell me everything — and what was established was not infidelity but the structural honesty of their partnership. The pattern learned from Brandor and Orlec, made operational in her own marriage.
Orlen — a man left behind in BreeBree-folk
A man she had begun to love, before Moria. Wrote to her at Rivendell to say he had not waited — she does not share the fire that you carry in your heart — and the letter and her own letter to him were dated the same day. The first wound the chronicle treated as a closed door rather than an open one. The book has forgotten him; she has not.
Lady Elwin — wraith, redeemedForsaken, of the North
The cave-wraith at the very first session, who softened into a noblewoman before dissolving. The first of the chronicle's many ghosts. Has returned to Celenneth's dreams since, alongside Moria's shadows, and is one of the figures the Wandering Madness uses against her.
The Witch-king of Angmar — cursemakerOf the Shadow
Did not appear in the chronicle. Set the curse on the cave where Celenneth took the crown and the blade. The Wandering Madness on her sheet is his work. The Warden Blade's Bane against the Undead is the chronicle's promise that this debt will eventually find its payment.

On What Was Built

The watch held without anyone behind it is a different watch from the watch held for one's own.

VIII. The Shadow Carried

The Wandering Madness

Her Shadow Path. Set on her by the Witch-king's curse at the cave in the first session and never fully lifted. Operates through dreams primarily — the Dol Guldur gates, the black blade in her own hand, Linnea drowning while she stands frozen, Lady Elwin's stare returning when Moria's shadows do. Hope sits two points below maximum and two Scars are on the sheet. Strength of Will and Confidence are the structural answer; love is the actual answer; both are necessary.

The Cursed DaggerDrawn from Fangorn's earth in Book VI with a broken leg in splintsReturns every time discarded. Fangorn judged it her problem. Pulses cold at her hip in dreams. Gandalf has said dark objects call to each other. She is sailing west with this on her belt.

The Pattern of Vulnerability

The Shadow has consistently found the crack of loss and pressed there. Early it threatened her with the loneliness she had built around the sister's disappearance. Now it threatens her with the family she has built since. The argument is the same; the stakes have only increased. The dream of Linnea drowning is not random; it is the most precise possible attack on someone whose entire architecture was built on the principle that she could not afford to love. Linnea's you won't lose me. It's not allowed. is the vow that holds the line. Brynja's I know you love me, Mama is the reinforcement that came back.

The Long Reckoning

Three threads remain unanswered, and the chronicle has been patient with all of them. Gorgol son of Bolg — promised in Book I, has not arrived. The pendant. The dagger. The Warden Blade's Bane against the Undead and Men of Darkness suggests the chronicle has been preparing a confrontation since the first session, and is not done preparing it.

Removed Nurhael's Warden Blade and the crown from the mummified corpse, nailing the crown into the skull as punishment rather than honour. Released Lady Elwin's wraith into peace. The cave's curse fastened to her as the Wandering Madness; the blade became hers by inheritance. Returned to find Orlen already gone from Bree. Just the right sting.
Book I · Bree, Gandalf, the Sealed Parchment
Made messenger; given the brazen head's eventual destination.
Gandalf called her by name, called her trust rather than command, gave her the sealed parchment with its faintly shimmering symbols. She sharpened her sword and checked her provisions, as rangers do.
Book I · Moria
Travelled alone into the reclaimed mountain; served Balin's hall; left with mithril.
Equipped with the mithril mail-shirt by Balin; entrusted with the brazen head of Dero for Rivendell; substituted mithril for gold from the hoard. Exited the western gate alone under open sky. A Ranger of the North knew how to endure.
Book II · Cermië 2965 — Rivendell, Nimroch, Orlen's Letter
Delivered her message; received the horse; received the closed door.
Met Belegon at the threshold, delivered Balin's gift, received Elrond's audience. Wrote to Orlen the night before riding out; his reply dated the same day told her he had not waited. Elrond's healing draught entered her pack. Nimroch was hers from then on. The iron eye pendant was found in the undergrowth the same arc.
Book II · The Road South — Linnea
Cut a Bree-woman from a bandit's wagon.
Intended to drop her at the next village. The road did not let it happen. Across the book Linnea was rehearsing speeches, foraging, learning how to keep watch; Celenneth was learning that I don't know how to do this was a sentence she was allowed to say. By the chapter's end: I don't want to. But yes. Her arms came up slowly. The wall came down incrementally.
Book III · Mirkwood, Dale, the Lonely Mountain
First successful alliance work; Bain heard them in his hall.
The orc-stolen map laid before Bain; Linnea asking for rest before the audience and being respected by a king. Dol Guldur named for the first time in a hall of state. The inn after, ordinary stew, ordinary rest. We're stoking a fire that can dismiss the shadow.
Book IV · The Woodland Realm
Thalion's alliance sealed; I love you spoken under stars.
The feast of Mirkwood, the elven hosts Arannis and Eryndor, the suspension of human anxieties about time and consequence. The first articulation of love between Celenneth and Linnea; the structural honesty that would carry every later thread. Linnea named Linnea of Bree by Thalion in his pavilion.
Book V · Rohan, the Binding of Aegis
The warrior's vow at Yule; the foundling adopted; the sister named.
Yule celebrated with honey cakes and mismatched socks; the Binding of Aegis sworn before Rohirrim witnesses: I swear to stand as your shield, to face the darkness at your side, and to guard your life with my own. Days later, by firelight, the sister finally named — she was seven. Brave. Foolish. I ran. The next morning Eofred granted Celenneth's petition; the foundling became Brynja, and Rohan her father.
Book VI · Capture, Fangorn, the Broken Leg
Stripped to absolute survival; the cursed dagger acquired; the Ent's test passed.
Captured by orcs after Uftak's and Malech's spears; the deliberate provocation, the loosened ropes, the stone shard palmed; one orc dead, the camp behind her. Fled into Fangorn; broke her leg on a root and set it herself with a belt and a branch. Found the rusted dagger with runes she almost recognised. Tested by the Ent — escape does not cleanse what clings — and was found sufficient. Walked out alone toward Edoras. You were never my friend, but you let me live.
Book VII · Garin, the Trollshaws, the Bath
Honesty about the body as structural commitment of the marriage.
Met Garin on the road back. Honest with Linnea before anything, honest with her after — hold me while you tell me everything. The chain-scars finally explained in the bath at the Trollshaws. The marriage tested and proved by the inclusion of what would have destroyed lesser bonds. The pattern from Brandor and Orlec made operational in her own life.
Book VIII · Bree, Rivendell, the Choice
The summons answered with the family rather than alone.
News of Balin's death received at Rivendell, grief named plainly. Linnea, watching Celenneth move among the elves, finally released the pattern: there will be times we'll have to let you go. Celenneth answered with destination rather than declaration: let's go to Bree. Let's make it solid. Together. Brynja's fever broken with Linnea's bitterroot. Brynja's leg set by the same hands that set Celenneth's own in Fangorn.
Book IX · The Angle
Choice of home; Gandalf met on the road.
Linnea offered the journey north: you've spent so much time helping me reconnect with my past. I want to do that for you. Gandalf reappeared on the road, telling Linnea stories Celenneth had never shared. The Angle accepted them. The tracking lesson for Brynja in the woods — the rabbit, the deer-tracks like this big, the quiver of feathers and smooth stones.
Book X · The Frost Maw, Brandor & Orlec
Found and buried the two rangers who had raised her.
Met Damrod and Hallas in the snow; the warg, the blizzard, the runes restored from ranger archives. Descended into the Frost Maw with the frost hounds, the wraiths, the binding curse chanted from old Númenórean memory. Orlec dying mid-warning; Brandor following him with I can't leave him alone in this cold earth. Buried together, hands clasped. The book answered the question was there anyone who came back for you with yes.
Book XI · The Watchtower, the Glade, Hallas the Son
The chronicle's slow recovery and the family completed.
The chalice given to the Dúnedain holy spring rather than kept — the chronicle's moral architecture of divestiture made explicit. Damrod and Hallas split eastward, tracking what they would track. Brynja's you smell like horses at homecoming. Hallas the son named for Hallas the ranger. Linnea midwife, the labour of five hours, the thin wavering cry. Our little Hallas. Welcome to our family.
Book XII · The Domestic Arc
Pregnancy and birth as the subject rather than the background.
Morning sickness and the pragmatic body no longer pragmatic. The appreciation scene during pregnancy — the body's transformation received as itself rather than as failure. Linnea's grief: I just want to be enough. Held, not solved. Brynja's hand on the moving belly; I'll make sure he's never scared at night. The chronicle's quietest and most interior work.
Book XIII · Eryndil, the Sea, the Warded Ruin
The pivot west; the family as indivisible unit.
Eryndil arrived in the Angle with patience rather than urgency. The Eärlindë readied at Mithlond under Círdan's eye. Almedra and Berthor met; the lorebook left with her. The Eryndil arrow — loosed at a prisoner Celenneth had chosen to release — sat unresolved between them. The voyage west to the warded Númenórean ruin began, with the cursed dagger still in her pack and a Dúnedain heir's blood the key.

X. Trajectory & Endgame

The Voyage West

The chronicle's near-term arc: six to eight books at sea and at the warded island. The Eryndil arrow is now structurally unavoidable, since it sits between them in the cabin of the Eärlindë for weeks. The cursed dagger and whatever is behind the wards will likely speak to each other — Gandalf's principle that dark objects call to each other is the chronicle's promise that the dagger has been waiting for this destination. Whatever opens to her blood will require her blood to open. The Númenórean inheritance that has been seeded since the Herald's Boots in Book I will become explicit. Celenneth, in her late thirties, will be the one to open it.

The Possible Endings

The chronicle has, by its own structural admission, been considering a time-jump: Brynja and the younger Hallas returning to Eriador as young adults around 2985–2990, with Celenneth and Linnea in their late forties, after the sea arc has put on them what it will put on them. The handoff is being prepared even now — Brynja's tracking lessons, the quiver of feathers and stones, the explicit pedagogy of being parented differently from how Celenneth was parented. The chronicle has built three generations of unorthodox relationship across thirteen books — Brandor & Orlec, Celenneth & Linnea, and whatever the children will carry forward. The endgame is generational, and the chronicle knows it.

What the chronicle has answered

The chronicle began with a question: can someone who learned to survive by never stopping learn to live by staying? By the end of Book XII the answer is unambiguously yes. Celenneth's vigilance and solitude and capacity for endurance were never the problem — the problem was that she aimed them at the road rather than at a life. The solution was never to make her softer; it was to give her something worth being hard for. The watch held for one's own is a different watch.

What the chronicle has not yet answered

Whether what she opens at the warded ruin will be something the family can survive together. Whether the cursed dagger will release her, or claim her, or simply continue. Whether the Witch-king's curse can be lifted before the Witch-king himself falls. Whether Gorgol son of Bolg, promised since the first session, will arrive in time to be met by the Warden Blade or in time to take from her what she has built. The chronicle is patient with all of these. So is the Shadow.

The Probable End

She is unlikely to complete her work in this life. The Dúnedain rarely do. What she finishes will be partial; what she leaves to Brynja and Hallas will be substantial. The watch is generational. A Ranger of the North knew how to endure was the line that closed Book I; it will likely be the line that closes whatever closes her. Endurance is not victory. It is the discipline of holding the line until the line is held by someone else.

And the line, now, is held by more than her.
