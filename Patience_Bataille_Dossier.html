I. The Young Lady in Question

Born in Martinique of an English mother and a French sailor, Patience spent portions of her upbringing in all three countries. Her parents died while she was a minor, and she has been tossed between the two families ever since. She is recently returned from France — returned to the man she believes is waiting for her — and hoping, at last, to find a place she might call home.

She is currently in residence with the Harringtons, cousins on her mother’s side, in the official capacity of companion to Miss Jane Harrington. The arrangement suits everyone but is a kindness undertaken on conditional terms: she lives under another family’s roof, and a Dependant’s welcome can be withdrawn.

To the table she presents as — in the words of one fellow socialite — a charming and interesting conversationalist; a bit reckless; worldly; fascinating; could be tamed.

To be tossed between two families is to learn, very early, that neither is your own. Patience is twenty-two years old, three nations behind her, and has decided that the next address she keeps will be a roof of her choosing.
