Lineage & Formation Origines

Born around 1890 in Beaune, a Burgundian town anchored by the medieval Hospices and surrounded by the vineyards of Pommard, Volnay, and Meursault. Her father Édouard Bergeron kept a notarial practice handling vineyard inheritances on one of the streets radiating from the Place Carnot; her mother Marie-Louise ran the house above the étude. The house smelled of law books, her mother's baking, and in autumn the fermenting must carried on the wind from the vineyards that began at the edge of town. Hélène for Homer's Helen and Racine's Andromaque; Éloïse for Héloïse d'Argenteuil, the abbess and philosopher — the medieval French woman intellectual. Her parents named her to carry weight.

Her older brother Édouard — her first teacher in everything, going to be a scientist or engineer — was killed at Verdun in 1916. Her life bent around that loss. She could not ride past Pommard or Volnay for a decade. Even on her visits home she arranges her walks to avoid the roads they used to take.

She went to the Lycée Clos-Maire in Beaune; her philosophy teacher Mademoiselle Verchère pushed her toward the Concours Général and the Sorbonne. The train left Beaune in 1908 or 1910 with her trunk in the luggage car and her father pressing a small leather purse into her hand on the platform with more money than she had asked for. Her departure was the day her life started and is also the day she is still, in some small part of herself, saying goodbye to.

Sorbonne licence, agrégation, doctorate. She studied with Marcel Granet (Durkheimian sociologist of Chinese religion), Antoine Meillet (language is an eminently social fact), Paul Pelliot (the brilliant philologist who looted Dunhuang in 1908 — a complicated figure to attend the lectures of, especially given whom she married). Her dissertation — defended c. 1922 — was on the sociolinguistics of the Chinese communities in France during and after the Great War: she went to Billancourt, Le Creusot, and Montargis with notebooks, sat in Chinese Students' Association meetings, collected leaflets, cross-referenced against Protestant missionary translations and Buddhist sutras and the classical canon.

Her methodological insight: that theological semantic drift — the mechanism by which the Cappadocian Fathers turned Greek philosophical vocabulary into Trinitarian theology — operates the same way in revolutionary discourse. When Chinese Work-Study students translate Marx, the choice between 民主 (people-rule) and 民權 (people-power) is doing the work that hypostasis and ousia did in the fourth century.

She met Ch'ien Po-hsiang in Paris in 1918 and married him in 1919 or 1920. Her parents did not come; her sister took the train alone, against their wishes, and stood up for her at the civil ceremony. Her mother wrote a letter Hélène has kept in a locked drawer that says some things that cannot be unsaid. By 1923 there has been partial reconciliation — her parents met Michel on a visit to Burgundy in 1921, her father liked him to his own surprise, they shared a bottle of 1895 Gevrey-Chambertin and talked soil chemistry — but her parents have not yet visited them in Peking. Her mother says the journey is too long. Her father says he is too old. Both of them mean something else.

She is a radical in the French intellectual sense — Dreyfusard descent, anti-colonialist, feminist (the vote is still denied French women), sympathetic to but not a member of the Communists. She attended the Congress of Tours in December 1920 as an observer. She knew Zhou Enlai in Paris better than her husband did — attended his study circles, interviewed him for the dissertation, corresponds with him still. When an old Work-Study friend writes asking for a favor, she is more likely than Michel to say yes without hesitation.

In Peking she is Conférencière invitée et chercheuse associée at the Sino-French University on Prince Zailan's adapted courtyard; she lectures in French on translation, religion, and the communities she studied. The Guimet keeps her on as chargée de mission. Granet at the EPHE lobbies quietly for a Sorbonne post. She does not yet have one. French academic posts for women were vanishingly rare. She does research. She writes. She corresponds. She takes private students.

The Party Compagnons

Ch'ien Po-hsiang Michel · husband · paleobotanist · the man who knows what she has said by listening to what she has not Deep love; productive political tension lovingly maintained. She teases him for his moderation; he teases her for her enthusiasms. They argue well, because they are both articulate and both love each other, and have been having variations of this conversation for four years. He calls her Léna, or Ai-lien; in serious moments, Hélène; in teasing moments, the full deployment.
Marie Halvor Boucher Pao Su-t'ien · 包素恬 · medical officer · Vancouver A surprising depth of connection. Both women on unconventional paths; both serious. Hélène is fascinated by Marie's Michif heritage and watches herself at the line between scholarly generosity and intrusion. Their conversations about the Chinese Union Version Bible translation choices could be extraordinary. The first intact living faith Hélène has studied up close that does not flinch from her.
Subedar Gurdit Singh Bajwa Director of Expedition Security · Amritsar Complicated. Part of her is warmed by his intellectual seriousness. Part of her has watched French imperialism at work in Indochina and is wary of the colonial position. She will try to ask interesting questions about Sikh theology; he will answer with measured politeness; over time, if she proves she takes him seriously as a person and not a case study, he may warm. She knows this line exists and watches herself at it.
Prof. Wang Enlai Department of Geology · Peking University The expedition's organizer. Her presence on the team was negotiated quietly, on the strength of her language skills and Michel's stipulation. She introduced herself to him in Mandarin, careful and deliberate, in a register her seven months of language school made audible.
Langdon Warner American · Fogg Museum, Harvard · Wagons-Lits Hotel She does not wait for her husband to speak first. She is precise — which in certain registers is worse than rude. Warner has been listening with his arms crossed and his head tilted slightly, the way a man listens when he is enjoying himself and has decided not to show it yet. The quality of his attention when he looks back at her has shifted slightly, the way light shifts when a cloud moves off.

Field Journal Journal de campagne

3 September 1923 — Morning The Courtyard off Dongsi
She commandeers the study table. Both fail their History rolls; Pelliot's 1908 looting stays unknown. She marks W-7, W-8, W-11 with a small question mark — three coordinates south and west, descriptions deliberately omitted. "Cela revient au même." She lights another cigarette, and this one she actually smokes.
3 September 1923 — 3:00 P.M. Wang Enlai's Meeting Room · Geology Building
She does not preface. In careful Mandarin, she opens the meeting with the three sites and refuses to soften the question. Wang reveals Warner's manuscript source — a private collector in Shanghai. "Une source," she repeats. She caps her pen. The single question she asks aloud: "What language is the manuscript in?" Wang's answer: a Central Asian dialect. Nothing further.
5 September 1923 — Dawn The Range
Marksmanship trial with Bajwa. She asks to be shown the grip once and watches his hands the way she watches a text she is parsing. First shot missed. Second clipped the frame. Third, after a long pause — inside the frame, low and left but inside. She sets the rifle down. "Only the one," she says, confirming for herself what she has demonstrated.
7 September 1923 — Morning Hôtel des Wagons-Lits · Legation Quarter
She does not wait for Michel to speak first. She introduces herself in English that carries its French in the vowels and, within two sentences, has arrived at the telegram. The urgency. The vagueness. The fact that four people rearranged their lives on the strength of thirty words. She is not rude. She is precise — which in certain registers is worse. When she finishes, Warner files her name somewhere useful and asks the room about the Library Cave.
7 September 1923 — later Wagon-Lits sitting room · the Pelliot catalogue on the table
Something shifts. The Library Cave comes back the way suppressed things do — all at once. Wang Yuanlu in 1900. Stein in 1907, forty thousand manuscripts for two hundred and twenty pounds in silver. Pelliot the following year, three weeks selecting, the Diamond Sutra to Paris without objection. Pelliot she knows personally — the same circles as people she studied under. She also realizes she has never once discussed any of this with Michel.
7 September 1923 — afternoon The same room · a recent photograph
Warner produces a recent photograph: dense Cyrillic across a cave wall, in what is clearly not paint, looping around damaged Tang-dynasty fresco with indifference. Russian soldiers, interned 1921–22, with too much time. The repeated phrase declines to give itself up. "The script is clear enough. The language —" she tips her hand, a gesture French in its precision. "I can work with this."
October 1923 Mogao Caves · Dunhuang
They arrive at Wang Yuanlu's caves. She has been working on the Russian phrase the whole journey. She steps forward and repeats it in Mandarin: "他們來了. 末日降臨. 因為他們來了." — they have come, the end has come, because they have come. Wang Yuanlu is still for a moment that goes on slightly longer than a moment should. He looks at her with an attention different in quality from how he looked at her before. "你在哪裡看到這個的?" — where did you see this?
That night A Tang-era painted chamber, deeper in
The priest leads her and Michel inward without looking back. A white bodhisattva in equanimity. He tells her the phrase has existed here for very much longer than the Russians. "你是怎麼知道的?" — how do you know it? She asks who they are. "有些門, 不應該被打開. '他們'是那些不同意的人." — there are gates that should not be opened. They are those who disagree.
After lamps out Their painted quarters · ground level
Michel lies awake beside her thinking about some do not know they are guarding. She sleeps — or pretends to — with the photograph and the phrase still working in her head. The painted eyes on the ceiling watch the room. She has done what she came to the Sorbonne to do, what she went to Billancourt and Le Creusot to do: she has stepped inside a religious vocabulary from the outside. The vocabulary is stepping back.
