義 · 仁情 Duty & Desire Giri · Ninjō

Giri Duty

Her lord is Nasu Tadaaki, her own father and current head of the declining Nasu family. Her duty is to restore the family's status and influence with the Imperial families. Kogo must prove the Nasu worthy of their former position.

Ninjō Desire

Kogo yearns for power that cannot be taken away by political whims or clan hierarchies. Deep down, she wants to transcend the petty limitations of clan politics entirely - to become something more than a samurai bound by duty and status.

Paramount tenet Duty and Loyalty Less significant Compassion

Kogo believes that the ends justify the means. Bushido matters mostly as a matter of what is observed; what is unobserved is not beholden.

Heritage

Kogo was named after her aunt, Nasu Kogo the Elder, a talented performer and courtier who was considered the family's brightest star a generation ago. Two years ago, Aunt Kogo was invited to perform in an imperial theatrical production—a tremendous honor and exactly the kind of recognition the Nasu desperately needed. Instead, she delivered a catastrophically bad performance. Whether from nerves, illness, or simple misfortune, she forgot lines, missed cues, and finally froze entirely on stage. The Emperor himself left the theater in discomfort. The shame was so profound that Aunt Kogo took the tonsure and entered a monastery, but the damage was done—rumors spread that the Nasu family is cursed by Benten-no-kami for some forgotten slight against the arts. Young Kogo now carries not just her aunt's name, but the burden of redeeming it.

Heritage modifiers: Honor +0 · Glory +0 · Status -3

人物像 The Person Within Jinbutsu-zō

What sets them apart

Kogo is experimental and creative in her work.

What they had to learn

Shiba Atsutane was assigned as Kogo's sponsor when she first arrived at Otosan Uchi—a well-meaning Phoenix courtier who was supposed to guide her entry into Imperial politics. Instead, she watched him destroy his own career within six months. Atsutane's fatal flaw was that he actually cared about the people affected by court decisions; he advocated too openly for the suffering, showed too much genuine emotion, and completely failed to read when powerful figures wanted problems ignored rather than solved. His compassion made him predictable, exploitable, and ultimately disposable. When he was quietly recalled to Phoenix lands in disgrace, Kogo stepped smoothly into his position—having learned that court is performance, that sincerity is a liability, and that compassion is a luxury for those who can afford to lose. She feels no sympathy for his fall; he had it coming, and she's more than happy to replace him.

A defining success

During her final year at the Shosuro Shadowweaver School, Kogo uncovered a Lion Clan conspiracy against Scorpion trade interests in the capital. Rather than simply reporting it to her sensei, she manipulated the situation—feeding the conspirators false information while ensuring the Scorpion's countermove appeared as superior planning rather than intelligence work. In the process, she obtained compromising evidence against Akodo Nobuhiko, a Lion courtier stationed at Otosan Uchi, which she quietly kept for herself.

A defining difficulty

During her Scorpion training, Kogo discovered a hidden scroll tucked away in her sensei's private collection—a fragmentary document on forbidden techniques that she believes the Scorpion was saving for leverage. She copied it in secret, certain she was the first to read it in years, and the text spoke of power beyond the kami's limitations. The knowledge was intoxicating, and when she attempted the first technique described within, she succeeded—but something dark answered her call. She gained power, but also corruption she can never fully cleanse. The Shadowlands Taint now marks her spiritual essence, a constant reminder that she has stepped onto a path from which there may be no return.

What calms them

Despite her ambitions and resentments, Kogo finds genuine tranquility in the traditional tea ceremony. In those quiet, ritualized moments—the precise movements, the meditative focus, the temporary suspension of scheming and striving—she can almost forget her burdens.

What worries them

Kogo lives in constant fear that her maho practice will be discovered before she achieves her goals, which would mean execution and her family's destruction. Worse still, she has become convinced that conspiracies surround her—that rival Phoenix families work to keep the Nasu marginalized, that the Scorpion suspect what she stole, that someone is always watching and waiting for her to slip. She sees patterns and plots in innocent coincidences, reads threat into casual questions, and trusts almost no one. Whether these conspiracies are real or products of her guilty conscience hardly matters; the paranoia shapes every decision she makes.

At first sight

Where many would have their own scribes, she takes her own copious notes at all times, in an elegant personal cipher.

Under stress

She organizes her spaces, demanding a complete sense of control over her environment.

Notable relations

Kogo maintains a careful relationship with Bayushi Toshiro, a mid-ranking Scorpion operative in Otosan Uchi who served as one of her instructors. He occasionally feeds her intelligence about court intrigues—partly because the Scorpion value having a Phoenix ally, partly because he enjoys keeping her in his debt. Kogo has cultivated a relationship with the proprietress of the Plum Blossom Tea House, a neutral meeting ground for courtiers in Otosan Uchi. The woman, Usagi Hanako (a Hare Clan vassal family member), runs the establishment as a place where "all are welcome to find peace"—but she's also remarkably well-informed about court gossip and has helped Kogo navigate the capital's social waters.

Standing with their clan

Publicly, Kogo is a dutiful daughter of the Phoenix, respectful of tradition and the Elemental Council. Privately, she seethes at how the clan's rigid hierarchies and spiritual purity have constrained her family's potential. She sees the Phoenix as hidebound, more concerned with ancient texts than real power, more worried about propriety than results.

In their parents' eyes

To Nasu Tadaaki, Kogo is "the future of our family, the one who will reclaim our rightful place." He describes her with a mixture of pride and barely concealed desperation—she is his only child, his last chance to restore the Nasu name before he dies. He speaks of her accomplishments with fierce pride to anyone who will listen, perhaps too loudly, too insistently, as if he can will the world to recognize her worth through sheer force of repetition. Other nobles find his advocacy unseemly; Kogo finds it suffocating. The weight of being everything to him is crushing.

How they will die

When the Nasu name has finally been restored to its rightful place among the Phoenix—when her father has seen his family honored once more, when the whispers of curses have been replaced with respect, when she has proven that will and ability matter more than bloodline—then and only then should Kogo die. She imagines herself at the pinnacle of her power, having mastered both legitimate magic and the forbidden arts, having achieved everything she set out to accomplish. In that moment of triumph, she would take her own life in a final act of control, silencing the kansen that whisper in her mind, severing the corruption before it can spread to others or taint her family's restored glory.
