I. Biography & Antecedents

E. Burgess — Pastel, ca. 1834

Ernest Burgess is the second son of an earl, which is to say he has the manners of money, the income of an allowance, and the prospects of nothing in particular. The eldest, Thomas, will inherit the Onslow viscountcy and the seat at Clandon Park in due course. Ernest will inherit, by his own father’s decree, a quarterly remittance and the firm understanding that he is to remain decorative, dissipated, and out of the newspapers.

He very nearly managed two of the three.

What his family failed to anticipate — what Ernest himself failed to anticipate, for some time — was that the demimonde he had taken up as cover (occultism being, in the 1830s, an extraordinarily permissive social circle for a young man of his tastes) was not, in fact, the elaborate fashionable nonsense it pretended to be. Several of the séances were real. Some of the bargains were binding. And his own latent talent — the high concept on his sheet reads simply Inadvertent Adept — began to manifest with an enthusiasm that quite outran his understanding of it.

Early Life & Disposition

Educated as befits the second son of a Surrey peer — tutors, a brief and undistinguished interval at one of the older universities, the Grand Tour curtailed by some unspecified embarrassment. He returned to London with sufficient French to be useful, sufficient Italian to be charming, and a recently acquired enthusiasm for the more rarified end of art collecting. His ostensible occupation, insofar as he has one, is that of a connoisseur and minor patron; his correspondence with Continental dealers is voluminous, his actual purchases somewhat more modest.

The trouble on his sheet — What goes around comes around — was at first read by Mr. Burgess as a charming aphorism about the karmic rewards of generosity. By the close of his time with the Club, he had come to read it differently.

Admission to the Kerberos Club

Mr. Burgess came to the Club’s attention in the winter of 1830, by way of an incident at a London soirée — an angry female revenant, fragmented like a broken mirror, addressing herself with some specificity to a gentleman named Simon. The shade in question had been called up by Mr. Burgess at Simon’s coercion several days prior, and now declined to depart without satisfaction. The matter was resolved, at length, with the assistance of Madame LaFonte (whose Limehouse establishment Mr. Burgess had been frequenting for unrelated reasons), the widow Eugénie, and three persons who would shortly become his colleagues: Natalie Brighton, Myron MacCurrach (then trading as “Albert Tremblay”), and the gargoyle Louis.

The Duchess Mary Somerville of Fairfax extended membership the following morning, on Madame LaFonte’s sponsorship. Mr. Burgess accepted.

It is quite odd indeed to have five new in such a short time. — The Duchess of Fairfax, on the admission

A Note on the Magic, as Played

The Profane Sorcery stunt was, in this campaign, run without a fixed spell list. Mr. Burgess’ player pitched effects as they were imagined — the larger the conceptual swing, the greater the chance of an extraordinary initial success, and equally the greater the cost on a failure.

An unusual pattern emerged: his magic works best the first time he attempts a given effect, ignorantly and on instinct. The vase-into-moonstone simulacrum, the binding inkwell exorcism, the swap of souls with Spencer — all were tried “blind” and all succeeded extravagantly. The reading the archivists have come to favour is that the conscious mind is, in the first attempt, not yet narrowing the act down to a humanly-shaped channel — he reaches into a pool he does not know is there and pulls up what the situation requires. Afterwards he sits down to understand what just happened, and the understanding under-describes the capacity. The valley that follows is the cost of the model being smaller than the thing it is trying to hold.

Only with time and study does the working come back to reliability. Astral Body is the lone working that climbed out of the valley to dependable use, and the reason is consistent with this reading: it is, in its nature, a working expansive of self. The conscious frame that needed to grow toward the underlying capacity could grow large enough only on the one working whose direction was outward into more space. Every other working remained stuck in the gap between what he could do and what he could understand he had done.

III. Profane Sorcery — A Catalogue

What follows is, so far as the Club’s archivists can reconstruct it, the substance of Mr. Burgess’ magical practice over the course of his association. The list is not exhaustive — trances of less consequence are omitted — but the named workings below are those that proved consequential, instructive, or catastrophic.

A note on nomenclature: the eighteen working-names below are not names Mr. Burgess pitched. He pitched effects. The names are descriptions assigned after the fact by the recording secretary, drawn where possible from recognised theological vocabulary — though “Exorcism by Inkwell” belongs to no tradition known to the Club, and “Transmutation — Scandal” names the fuel rather than the working. Mr. Burgess, asked what school he works in, would not be able to tell you. The taxonomy here is the archivist’s; the practitioner has none of his own.

A Note on Frequency

Astral Body is the working Ernest attempted most often by an order of magnitude — perhaps a dozen times in the campaign, and routinely as a matter of course by the end. Summon Entity ran a distant second, with five named callings (Katherine, James Grayson, Balaam, the petition to Kinsataan, and an attempted contact with a river-spirit that hid from him). Every other working in the catalogue above was, in this campaign, performed essentially once.

A Note on the Shape of His Blowback

The recording secretary observes, reading down the Disposition column, that the costs Mr. Burgess pays are not distributed at random. Every entry marked Blowback, Costly, or Cascade is a working performed at distance — a magic stretched thin across a gap. Sympathetic resonance with the missing Serafina opened a link a vampyre fed back along. The projection along the Carolina coast extended his self until the ocean noticed there was something extended-and-self-shaped near its surface. The soul-exchange with Spencer opened three vacancies simultaneously and three things filled them. None of these workings failed because they were ambitious. They failed because they thinned him across a span, and something on the far side had room to reach back along the line.

The workings that succeeded extravagantly — the moonstone transmutation, the inkwell exorcism, the cellar warding, the blood-substitution at the Clockwork God’s feed — are without exception local workings, however large in effect. He stood in a room and did a thing in that room. He does not, on the evidence, pay for ambition. He pays for attenuation. The Club’s archivists record this for future reference; whether Mr. Burgess himself has noticed it, the file does not say.

IV. Entities Whose Attention He Has Drawn

The following powers, persons, or things took notice of Mr. Burgess in the course of his work. The Club’s archivists wish to be plain: these are not his patrons. He is not held by any of them. Some merely watched. Some intervened. One is owed payment and has not yet collected.

Balaam
A daemon, summoned by name. Latterly self-styled “Eddie.”

Called up in Ernest’s cellar one night in 1833, in trade for information on how to kill the vampiir. The price agreed was a year and a life — the latter being Miss Hayes’ man, the former being drawn from Ernest’s own.

Balaam has been a steady presence since. It rode along during the asylum committal in Nice and used Ernest’s sleeping body to escape; it walked Ernest’s flesh into Thibault’s townhouse to retrieve the moonstone simulacrum and the books, and bargained briefly with Myron in a dream (offering “true invisibility” for “making sure the mage holds the stone”). In the Holler, when the body-swap left Ernest’s flesh vacant, it moved in openly, introducing itself to the dig-team as Eddie with one of Myron’s small mannerisms borrowed for verisimilitude.

In the cave-chamber, with Kelly Keating’s knife in its borrowed ribs and the ritual trying to fix it in place, Ernest fought his way back into his own body and evicted Balaam by violence to self — biting and clawing his own flesh from within. The eviction held. The bargain did not close. Of the agreed year, Balaam reckons it has used twenty-three hours and thirty-six seconds. The remainder, and the life, remain outstanding.

Balaam considers Ernest’s choices “truly amazing” and the soap opera of his life its preferred entertainment. This is not, on the whole, reassuring.

The Maker of Twisted Ones
A power of the Carolina backwoods. Fae-allied, of the Holler.

The presence behind the binding of a man inside an animal’s skull and skeleton — the “Twisted One” that nearly was Misha, in Blythewood. Mr. Burgess’ improvised Latin exorcism, broadcast through Myron’s held inkwell from astral remove, broke the binding. Pitched in absolute desperation; performed for the first and only time; it worked. Mr. Burgess himself could not believe it had.

The Holler remembered. The engagement that followed, when the company passed through the painted forest of the cave-passage on its way to the chamber, presents on the surface as retaliation, and was so taken by those present. The recording secretary submits a fuller reading. Fae powers across the traditions do not, as a rule, expend their own substance to do things they could do with substance taken from a captive practitioner. The Maker of Twisted Ones, having identified Mr. Burgess as a wandering Englishman capable of breaking a Holler binding from outside, would correctly classify him as both a threat and a resource. The brief detention in the painted forest reads, in this light, as a sample — a test of what was there, followed by a draw on whatever could be drawn before larger powers in the area objected.

Anayehu is the most plausible reason the engagement was brief rather than fuller. By that point she had declared an interest in his matter, and a fae power harvesting from a practitioner she had grading authority over would have been overstepping. The Holler took its sample and let him go. Mr. Burgess did not enjoy the experience. He has not, since, returned by foot to the Carolina interior.

The God of the Forest
A presence felt always to the west. Vast. Hungry. Bound, until it was not.

The brooding pressure of the interior of the continent — the thing called by some the Clockwork God, a god of war once bound by older powers and sealed beneath the cave. Ernest felt it from the Carolina shore and again, more keenly, on the airship. Spencer and Kelly Keating intended to wake it as the first prong of a Union war upon the peoples of the interior; Ernest, having heard arguments on both sides, agreed with Anayehu to some smaller war, then, and offered his own blood instead of the fluxonium they meant to use. The barrier to the interior fell. The god did not wake. The forest, west of where the barrier had stood, is no longer so wholly spidery as it was — though much of it remains itself.

The Presence in the Ocean
A power off the Atlantic seaboard, vast and impersonal.

Encountered first by astral projection along the Carolina coast, when Ernest stretched himself too thin and the water began, gently at first, to draw him in. He yielded; he was nearly absorbed. His friends saved him by binding him bodily into a steamer trunk, where the constriction reminded him where he ended. Nothing has been heard from the ocean since — not because it does not remember, but because the Americas are full of more pressing matters.

Kinsataan
A god of the Tik people. Trickster. The forge of the coin.

Antonia’s grandmother named him “that trickster” with the resignation of one who has known him a long time. He gave Mr. Burgess a coin — a two-headed creature stamped in metal, useful as a key to wake the bound god, or as something else entirely. Mr. Burgess carries it still.

He has called upon it twice. Both times Anayehu came instead. The Club’s archivists file three readings, none mutually exclusive:

  1. That the coin does not do what Antonia’s grandmother said it did. Possible — but Antonia’s grandmother is not the kind of woman who is wrong about gods.
  2. That Kinsataan and Anayehu are aspects of the same entity, or members of the same household, or otherwise sufficiently entangled that calls to one route to the other. Plausible, given that Anayehu came both times and apparently without effort.
  3. That there is a trickster joke being played that Mr. Burgess has not yet been let in on. The grandmother’s tone supports this. The joke may be that the coin is Anayehu’s, that the coin is shared, or that Kinsataan sends Anayehu in his stead because she has the standing claim on the matter.

The file does not adjudicate. The coin remains in Mr. Burgess’ pocket; the metaphysics of it remain unsettled.

Anayehu
A god of the deep forest. A spider, taller than the trees.

Ernest reached for Kinsataan in the river-fight and reached, instead, Anayehu. She cocooned his astral self in webbing and hung him from a branch while she made her own arrangements with his companions on the ground. Subsequently they talked — about war, about waking the Clockwork God, about whether his coin would be used or laid down. She did not eat him.

She returned, in the cave chamber, when the working was nearly done. She mended Thibault’s broken leg with a touch and accepted nothing in payment, only saying that they had given her enough already. To Ernest she said: I will see if I can find you a mentor. But I think you are farther along than you think you are.

A standing debt — for the attention of the Club’s officers

Mr. Burgess is, at the close of this dossier, in possession of working sorcery, an unpaid debt to a daemon known to him by two names, the recognised regard of two deities of the Americas, and the lingering interest of a god of the western ocean. The Club may wish to take note. He himself has, since the close of the affair, become rather more attentive to such matters.

V. Chronicle of Notable Events

London · Winter 1830
The Soirée, & the Calling of Katherine

Mr. Burgess summons Katherine Stevins under coercion. The shade attaches and returns of her own accord at a soirée. With Madame LaFonte, Eugénie, Natalie Brighton, Myron MacCurrach, and the gargoyle Louis, she is brought to peace. Simon, her widower-murderer, is collared. Sponsorship to the Kerberos Club follows.

Henley-on-Thames, Oxford, Penrith, & Dorset · 1832–1834
The Whitby Affair

The Duchess of Fairfax summons the company to the Club’s long museum room, beyond several locked galleries, and there commissions them. A painting of hers has been stolen from her London manor: a landscape by one Norman Whitby. The Duchess’ private detective Mr. Lucas Moreland has not made progress and she would like the four of them to look into it. There is no sign of forced entry; the wards on the house were in their proper places.

The matter becomes immediately stranger. A second theft, this time from a locked storeroom of the firm Stratton, Cadwell & Co., empties the Duchess’ reserve of all her remaining Whitbys; the only evidence in the chamber is a small pile of ash and a scrap of canvas. The thieves came and went through no door anyone can find.

In Henley-on-Thames they find Whitby’s rooms above the haberdashery let to a young man named Marlowe; the landlord Mr. Ainsley has not seen Whitby since the previous March. In Oxford for the Solstice Exhibition, Mr. Burgess discovers the working that resolves the mystery: a small Whitby pastoral hangs in the Bodleian, the librarian leaves him alone with it, and from a borrowed office he attempts to project himself into the canvas. The painting draws him in across a painful distance. He cannot hold himself there; he backtracks to his body. The paintings are doors.

He steals the Bodleian canvas under his coat and ships it back to the Manor at Birchwood. From there the affair unfolds across half of England: a Whitby is stolen from the Grafton estate by a steel-grey gentleman who beats Mr. Grafton with a lamp and burns the kitchen; Mr. Burgess and the company travel by Whitby through the painting they recovered, finding themselves in the countryside of a similar but slightly off-season summer; the trail to Penrith follows the artist’s lover, one Lise, who has gone missing; in Penrith the Murchisons sit at the heart of a tangle that opens into a salon, an apothecary’s collection of paintings with hidden symbols, and an evening that introduces Mr. Burgess to one Lord Dunwell, who is buying up Whitbys at exorbitant prices to resell at higher ones.

The list of paintings that the recording secretary has been able to reconstruct, from a ledger lifted out of Myron MacCurrach’s cousin’s flat:

  • A still life with fruit and vase — Lord Falwell
  • A set of four pastoral pieces — The Duchess of Fairfax (the original commission, stolen first)
  • A pastoral farm scene — Oxford Library (stolen by Mr. Burgess, in the manner described above)
  • The Uffington White Horse — Stratford-upon-Avon, latterly Lord Dunwell’s
  • A pastoral of the seaside estate — the Grafton House (stolen by the grey-haired intruder)
  • Several in London, of variable provenance, some checked
  • The Jurassic shore at Lyme — commissioned for Miss Mary Anning, displayed at the Royal Society (subsequently stolen, with the Night Witch’s intervention)

The further the affair travels, the more clearly Whitby’s paintings are themselves the structure of the case. Through the Dorset canvas the company crosses to a tropical lagoon under an unfamiliar sun; they find Lise, then Whitby himself, living at last on an island somewhere off the colonies. Whitby refuses to return. He admits, more startlingly, that the missing paintings stolen from the Duchess’ storeroom were destroyed by his own hand — he had realised, too late, that a painting of her London manor would let any practitioner of sufficient skill walk in past her wards, and he had taken them and burned them himself. He sends a letter to the Duchess attesting his safety. Mr. Burgess carries it home.

The Duchess accepts the letter. The affair is, by her satisfaction, closed. The recording secretary notes for the file that the paintings did precisely what Whitby feared they would, and that Mr. Burgess’ use of them — to travel between London, Dorset, France, the New World — established a category of working the Club had not previously held in its catalogue. Whether the paintings remaining outside Club control will be similarly used by parties less considerate of the wards on their destinations is a question the file leaves open.

Uffington Manor · 18 September 1835
The Trial of Peers

Mr. Lucas Moreland, the Duchess’ original investigator and not pleased to have been superseded by the company, presents the case to a panel of six Peers convened at the country seat of William Craven, Viscount of Uffington — Lord Dunwell, having lodged the complaint, declining to attend, which is permitted but suggestive.

The jury, as constituted:

  • Chandos Leigh — a minor poet, friend of Lord Byron, and (privately, but not as privately as he supposes) sometime fellow of Mr. Burgess’ occult cabal.
  • Viscount FitzHarris — of the art scene; generally well-disposed; expected to assume any perceived slight is imagined.
  • Lord Chatterleigh — slighted by Mr. Burgess once in the art rooms some years past, and possessed of suspicions about Leigh and Burgess for which he has context but no proof.
  • James Scarlett, Baron Abinger — a barrister recently elevated for plain dealing, and now finding the privileges of his elevation in tension with the principle that won it him.
  • William Craven, Viscount of Uffington — host of the trial; particularly concerned because the painting at the heart of the matter was of his ancestral white horse; not unsympathetic but unwilling to be made the fool.
  • (The Peerage’s sixth seat is filled out by another of FitzHarris’ cohort, of no recorded distinction.)

Moreland presents the timeline with the precision of a man who has thought about little else. The dates run as follows:

  • 15 June — the Duchess’ painting is stolen from her London manor.
  • 17 June — Mr. Burgess is engaged by the Duchess to investigate.
  • 18 June — the remainder of the Duchess’ Whitbys are taken from her locked storeroom.
  • 19 June — Mr. Burgess travels to Henley-on-Thames in the company of two unmarried women.
  • 20 June — he purchases a Whitby in Henley.
  • 20–21 June — he travels to Oxford for the Exhibition.
  • 21 June — a Whitby is stolen from the Oxford Library, during the Exhibition.
  • 23 June — he leaves Oxford in the company of a third unmarried woman.
  • 24 June — a Whitby is stolen from the Grafton Estate. Mr. Burgess arrives shortly afterwards.
  • 26 June — he leaves for Penrith.
  • 27 June — at a private viewing at Lord Dunwell’s, attended by Mr. Burgess and one other, a large Whitby of the Uffington White Horse is stolen overnight from Dunwell’s house.
  • 29 June — he returns to London.
  • 30 June — a Whitby is stolen from the Royal Society.

Moreland declines to allege the actual mechanism (which he does not understand) and confines himself to opportunity. The defence Mr. Burgess presents is the defence a peer is permitted to present, which is mostly his own bearing.

After a considerable amount of time at the sideboard and the liquor cabinet, the Peers pronounce a verdict of Guilty. Mr. Burgess pleads the Privilege of the Peerage. The company disperses. The matter is recorded; no further action is taken.

The recording secretary observes for the file that the Trial established the public record of his involvement without establishing any of the mechanisms by which it was possible — which is to say that the most important facts of the affair remained, after the Peers had finished with it, exactly where the Club preferred them.

London · 1833
The Vampyres, & the Bargain in the Cellar

Serafina Grafton, having been bitten in a manner the company did not at the time appreciate the consequences of, has been turning in Mr. Burgess’ wine-cellar for the better part of an evening — tied to a chair, Natalie Brighton sitting with her, with Mr. Burgess having warded the cellar door against the passage of anything evil or unnatural without knowing for certain whether the ward would now keep Serafina herself in.

The folklore in the household’s possession — a Baltic volume Valentín translated for him, concerning the vere-imeja — named holy water as effective but unspecific. Miss Hayes’ coven was, by all signs, preparing to come back for Serafina. The household’s margin was hours, not days.

Mr. Burgess descended to the cellar landing outside the warded door — the side his sworn friend was not behind — and called Balaam by name. He proposed a trade: the life of Miss Hayes’ man Wright, whom he had restrained in another room and intended to dispatch by hand at the conclusion of the affair, for instruction in the killing of vampiir. Balaam bargained back. The price the daemon required, in addition to the man Wright, was a year and a life of Mr. Burgess’ own — the year drawn slowly, the life at the daemon’s convenience. He agreed.

The instruction was given: holy water to slow, beheading to kill, garlic packed in the cavity to prevent return. Mr. Burgess ascended to the kitchen, took a wreath of garlic from the ceiling and an axe from beside the stove, and was holding both when Myron walked in with the new arrivals Clarice Jacobson and Jonah Steiner. The information proved true. Miss Hayes evaded the household and lives still. The bargain remains open. See § IV.

Nice · December 1834
The Moonstone, the Scandal, the Asylum

At Lascaris’ soirée, Mr. Burgess engineers the social outrage required to power a transmutation — alone with the young Grand Duchess Olga Nikolaevna in the Collections Room. A vase becomes the moonraker’s stone. He hides the original. He is ejected. The Empress is not amused.

A letter from Thibault, two days delayed, was already an answered prayer; by the time it reached his hand the writer was gone, on the Artémise for the New World. Mr. Burgess drinks himself out of LaChappelle’s credit and is collected, by paid arrangement, by orderlies of an asylum on the outskirts of the city. His brother Thomas signed the order.

The asylum did not hold him. Balaam, walking his sleeping flesh, sigil-burned the door, the cot, and the night-nurse’s composure, and took him to Thibault’s shuttered townhouse to retrieve the books and the stone. Of these events, he himself remembers nothing.

The Atlantic & the Carolinas · 1835
Savannah, the Tik, & the Coin

From Nice by way of the painting and the deal with the Siren, they cross to the Americas. The swamp. Antonia’s people. Kinsataan’s coin. The reach of the ocean from the Carolina shore. The growing certainty that something to the west is awake and watching, and that something else, ahead of him, is calling.

Capefair · 1836
The Airship, the Cave, the Clockwork God

Spencer and Kelly Keating, Union operatives by way of the office Myron once nominally worked for, mean to wake the bound war-god beneath the cave and pour him over the barrier into the interior. Thibault, who has been working the site for his own academic purposes, has been imprisoned for protesting the schedule. Ernest finds him behind stalactite bars and, in a working that flickers with horns and wings about him, melts the stone.

The body-swap with Spencer goes sideways; Balaam takes the vacancy. In the deep chamber the dig crew threads fluxonium pipes into the chamber-walls (which are not walls). Ernest transmutes the silver to his own blood and offers it instead. The thing drinks. He drops ashen.

Anayehu comes. She heals Thibault. The barrier breaks. Spencer is “handed off to the deep forest” by Antonia and does not survive. Kelly Keating is shot. Ernest lives.

After · 1836
Atlantis, & a Painting Returned

Mr. Burgess presents to Thibault a Whitby painting of the French countryside — pried from LaChappelle’s reluctant hands — that Thibault had long sought. They go to Atlantis together at Antonia’s invitation; Thibault does not stay. He keeps a house on the coast in the New World, and visits Ernest through the painting at his pleasure. Ernest, knowing now what the daemon is and what it is owed, prefers it that way.

VI. Concerning Monsieur Bélanger

If the occult was Mr. Burgess’ on-ramp, Thibault Bélanger is — by the close of the chronicle — the actual purpose of it. A French baron of central-western descent, an erudite and red-bearded scholar of antiquities, met at cards in Nice in the autumn of 1834. They were briefly happy.

Thibault’s father had died, the story is told only later, in a room that looked as if something had exploded in it — ruined by his own obsession with magic. Thibault has therefore reasons to mistrust the practice itself, separate from his fondness for the practitioner.

The misunderstanding in Nice was, in the event, a misunderstanding only of timing: Thibault had written to propose a reconciliation, his letter took two days to reach Ernest, and by the time it was opened the writer had taken passage on the Artémise for the New World. Ernest, who had stolen and read several of Thibault’s books while Balaam wore him through Thibault’s townhouse, followed under his own steam, by way of a daemon-haunted asylum, an Atlantic crossing, a swamp, and a god.

In the cave Ernest told Thibault, plainly, what he had been concealing from himself — that the powers he channelled were vaster than his discipline, that what he could do he could not always undo, and that several large and patient things now knew his name. Thibault listened. He has accepted the painting; he has accepted, with reservations, the man.

The arrangement, as it now stands: Thibault keeps a small house on the Carolina coast. The Whitby painting hangs over its mantle. Through it, at his choosing, he visits Ernest in Atlantis — or at the Burgess house in London, or wherever Ernest can be found. They share lives. They do not live together. Ernest, for the first time in a number of years, considers this the responsible thing to do.

You convinced LaChappelle to give this to you, to give to me. I have been trying to get this. I have been trying to convince him, for some time, to give me this painting. — Thibault Bélanger, on opening the box

VII. Relations & Connexions

Of the Club & the Cause

Madame LaFonte — Medium, of Limehouse. Sponsor.
Brought him in. Taught him what little he was prepared to learn.
Duchess Mary Somerville of Fairfax — Of the Club’s governing board.
His patron, more or less. Set the Whitby business in motion.
Dr. Monroe — Kerberan. Often exasperated.
Has flounced past him, been flounced past by him.
Eugénie — Widow, witch, of the older generation.
Stomp-mode only. Built the shamble that found Katherine.
Natalie Brighton — Tinker, fellow-Kerberan, sometime co-conspirator.
One of the four. Has stood beside him in most of his worse decisions.
Myron MacCurrach — Spy, hat-wearer, fellow-Kerberan.
Briefly tenanted Ernest’s body during the Holler affair. They are friends.
Louis — Gargoyle. Of the Limehouse rooftops.
Treats Ernest as a chew-toy of long acquaintance. The affection is mutual.
Misha — Russian, occasionally a pug.
Cousin to the Imperial family. Has not forgiven the moonstone scandal.

Of the Continent & the New World

Thibault Bélanger — French baron, archaeologist, scholar of Atlantis.
The point of everything since 1834. See § VI.
Monsieur LaChappelle — French art connoisseur. Survives all things.
Has paid his tab in Nice; surrendered the Whitby canvas; carries on.
Antonia Isabella Eva Chiara — Tarot reader. Of the Tik. Does not miss.
Guide, conscience, exasperation. Made the Spencer problem disappear.
Serafina Grafton — Engineer, latterly vampiir.
Survived. Lives at distance with Lise. Cannot, now, cross running water.

Of His Own Blood

Thomas Burgess, Viscount — Elder brother. Heir to the earldom.
Signed the asylum order. Sober. Disapproving. Genuinely concerned, in his way.
Sir Milton Onslow, Earl of Onslow — Father. Of Clandon Park.
Has not seen his second son in some considerable time, and is not actively looking.

VIII. Assessment & Disposition

Mr. Burgess began this chronicle a foppish dandy with a habit, a Trouble he did not yet understand, and a curiosity that exceeded his sense. He ends it — nominally — in retirement at Atlantis with a French archaeologist, a coin from a god, a daemon’s pending invoice, and the standing acknowledgment of three powers that ought, by every reasonable metric, to have eaten him.

The Trouble has paid out, in full. Several times.

The Club’s archivists note the following, by way of summary:

Standing — at the close of the chronicle

Of the Kerberos Club: Member in good standing, by Madame LaFonte’s sponsorship. Has been declared Guilty by a Trial of Peers and immediately pleaded the Privilege. Has not, otherwise, brought public disgrace upon the Club; what disgrace he has brought has been upon himself, his brother’s patience, and the diplomatic relations of the Russian Empire.

Of the law: Wanted in Nice for the unauthorised removal of priceless artefacts (the Lascaris moonraker), for the engineered scandal of a foreign Grand Duchess, and for the burning down of a private asylum. Has not returned to Nice.

Of his own father: Cut off, then quietly reinstated, then cut off, then reinstated again. The remittance continues, on the understanding that he remains abroad.

Of his magic: Considerably better than when he began, and worse than what is asked of it. Anayehu thought him farther along than he thinks he is. She may be right; she may simply have been kind.

Liability — for the file

The Balaam bargain is open and bears interest. Should Mr. Burgess be called upon for further service to the Club, the Club is advised that the engagement of one Profane Sorcerer entails, by extension, the engagement of the daemon attached to him. The daemon finds the soap opera entertaining. So far this has worked in the Club’s favour. It will not necessarily continue to.
