On the Subject

Arman's family worked iron for four generations in a Zagros mountain village before he broke the lineage. He was the eldest son, and good at the trade — by twenty he was running the forge alone on the days his father went down the mountain for stock-iron. His back gave out the spring he turned twenty-three: a stupid lift, an iron billet he should have asked a neighbor to help with, a slipped vertebra he could feel grinding when he moved. The village's only physician, a Sufi who passed through every season and a half, gave him three months of poppy-tincture and a recommendation to stop forging. He could not stop. The forge was the family's only income.

He kept working. The pain was bad. He started staying up at the forge through the night because the work distracted him from it. On a night three months after the injury, he was alone at the bellows, half-delirious from poppy and pain, and something in the fire spoke to him. It said its name was Erlik. He had heard it in steppe-traders' stories — the god of the underworld, the great smith below the mountains, the keeper of fires that do not go out. It offered him a bargain: take the fire into himself, and the fire would carry the work the body could not. He said yes. He does not remember saying yes; he remembers waking up at dawn at the forge with the fire still burning despite no one having fed it for hours, and a new heat in his hands.

That was eight years ago. He cannot lift heavy things anymore — his back is still bad, and the bargain did not cure it — but he can shape metal with a touch of his fingers, heat a blade to glowing without coals, and call fire into being where there is none. The village kept him for a while; he made better tools than anyone else, and the priests of the local mosque looked the other way. Then a Mongol-aligned raiding party came through two winters ago and burned the forge, and Arman walked out of the village with what he could carry. He has been moving along the Silk Road since, fixing what travelers need fixed, and listening — sometimes — for the next thing the fire wants of him.

He is wary of his patron. He is also grateful to it. He has not yet decided which of those is the truer feeling.

The Smith of Black Hells
Level 1 · Subclass core

He learns heat metal as a warlock spell; it does not count against his spells known, and he can cast it once per long rest without expending a slot, or normally with slots.

  • Ritual variant — imbue up to 3 melee weapons with magical heat. They count as magical and deal an extra +1d4 fire on each hit (scaling: +1 at L1, +1d4 at L3, +1d6 at L5, +1d8 at L11, +1d10 at L16). Imbue a fourth, the earliest loses its property. He may instead imbue up to 5 pieces of ammunition, which lose the property on firing.
  • Armor variant — imbue 2 sets of armor. When a wearer is hit by a melee attacker within 5 ft., the attacker takes 1d4 fire (once per round per armor piece).
  • Proficiencies granted — Smith's tools (already had), plus two martial melee weapons of choice. Selected: scimitar and shortsword — both light/finesse options his Str 7 can actually wield.
  • Spellcasting focus — Smith's tools or any metal melee weapon. He uses the small hand-hammer at his belt.

Background — Tradesman of Fars

Tradesman

Local salesmen are inclined to give him a discount, and he immediately knows if simple sellers are trying to deceive him.

Specialty · Village Smith Turned Itinerant

Once the four-generation village blacksmith; now an itinerant smith and repair-man. He sells small repaired items, fixes traveler-gear at caravanserais, and quietly works the kind of metalwork that requires more heat than most forges can produce.

Your Journey

Lifepath · rolled and chosen entries from the Silk Road book

Birthplace
The family's forge-shed, adjacent to the house — born on a winter night his mother could not be moved.
Family
Single father. His mother died of a fever when he was eight; his father raised him and his younger brother alone.
Siblings
One — a younger brother who left at sixteen for Isfahan to become a Sufi student. Eleven years now without word.
Lifestyle
Comfortable. A working smith's family — the forge paid well enough for a four-generation business; solidly above subsistence. Until the raid burned it.
Strings to Arcanum
The community had seen the supernatural — a possessed traveler thirty years before, a wood-bound charm against djinn over the inn-door, the deva-fires the older women said sometimes appeared in the high pastures during dry years. No one in the village was surprised when fire spoke to Arman.
Past Education
Apprenticed to his father at the family forge from age six (Option 3 — unorthodox). Learned enough Persian to handle accounts; no scholarly tradition. Grants kamancheh proficiency — the Persian bowed string instrument.
Event I · Arcane · d20 = 4 → d8 = 5

Drank a potion — GM chooses the contents.

Two winters ago, before the raid, a traveling Sufi physician passed through his village and offered him a black liquid in a small bone-stoppered vial "for the fire in his back." He drank it. He slept for two days. When he woke, the back pain was meaningfully less for the first time since the injury — but his hair, which had been black, grew back grey at the temples afterward, and he sometimes hears voices in the boundary-state between sleep and waking. The Sufi is gone. The GM decides what was actually in the vial.

Event II · d20 = 11

Lost something or someone important.

The forge. Burned by a Mongol-aligned raiding party two winters ago. He saved a few tools and the small hand-hammer he now carries; everything else four generations had built went with the building. The village survived; the smithy did not. He left rather than rebuild on borrowed iron.

Event III · d20 = 19

Religious faith changed completely.

Officially he is still Sunni Muslim. Privately, since the bargain at twenty-three, he addresses Erlik. He has not been to a mosque in three years. The two faiths sit uncomfortably in him; he does not know which will be the one he dies inside.

Event IV · d20 = 7

Made a friend of an adventurer.

Somewhere along the Silk Road since the raid — Isfahan, a caravanserai near Tabriz, a roadhouse where two refugees shared a fire — he made a friend of another adventurer who will come to his aid when called. GM-assigned, not yet named.

Personality

Ideal — Repair · Neutral

"Things break. The world is mostly broken things. My work — both kinds of my work — is to mend a little of it before I go."

Bond

"My father trained me at the forge before he died. The forge is gone now. The hand-hammer is the only thing left of him I still carry. If I lose it I will not have words for what I have lost."

Flaw

"I said yes to the bargain because I was in pain and the offer was real. I have not yet been asked to pay for it. I am thirty-one and afraid of being thirty-five."

Connections

  • Zerah ibn Dawud al-Isfahani — They met at a Sufi-run roadhouse in the hills above Isfahan eighteen months ago, the night a winter storm pinned six travelers under one roof. Zerah was on his way down from the Mountain House on an errand for the abbot; Arman was working an itinerant repair-route between Persianate towns. They got to talking about fire — the only subject Zerah talks about freely with strangers — and discovered, by candlelight, that they hold mirrored cosmologies: Zerah's everlasting flame is the world's first kindled light; Arman's patron-fire is the world's first buried fire. Neither managed to argue the other out of their position. They parted at dawn, civil and a little unsettled. Arman still thinks about the way Zerah held a coal in his bare palm during the conversation — not to demonstrate; just because his hands were cold.
  • Safiyya bint Tariq al-Qıpçaq — They have not met. They share a cosmological doubleness that would be visible to either of them within a single conversation: Safiyya's seer-line draws from the Blue Sky (Kök Tengri) and ancestors who walk in the upper world; Arman's patron-bargain draws from Erlik, who rules the fires beneath. The Kipchak shamans Safiyya was raised among acknowledge Erlik without worship — the under-side of the same theology her grandmother's voice speaks from. Arman's village charms against djinn were the southern Persian mirror of the same precaution. If they meet — likely at a roadhouse on the Khwarezm trade-route, where eastbound steppe-folk and westbound Persian travelers cross paths — they will recognize each other within an hour. The recognition will be wary. Each will know the other is touched by the same family of powers, from opposite faces of it. Neither will be sure whether to consider the other an ally.
