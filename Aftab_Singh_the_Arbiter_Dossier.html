On the Subject

Aftab was born in a fishing village on the lower Indus, the elder of two children of a widowed mother. His father had drowned the year before his birth; his mother kept their small boat going alone, and Aftab learned to row before he learned to read. His childhood was comfortable in the way a fisher-village is comfortable: enough fish, enough rice, a roof, neighbors who knew his name.

Slavers came when he was twelve. They were a small raiding party, three boats, working the river under the cover of a monsoon storm. They took him and four other boys from the village. He never saw his mother or sister again.

He spent the next twelve years as a ghulam — a slave-soldier — in the service of a sultanate whose name he no longer says aloud. He was good at it. He was big for his age, then big for any age; the bowyers gave him heavier draws and the armorers fitted him with mail when most of his cohort still rode in leather. He killed men in three battles and stopped counting after that. His master, an emir of moderate rank, treated him well enough — better than most ghilman had it — and in his twenty-fourth year, on the occasion of the master's son's wedding, manumitted him along with a dozen others. He was given fifty dinars, a horse, and his freedom.

Within the month he was falsely accused of theft by a merchant in a caravan-town outside Multan. The accusation was malicious; he had refused the merchant's daughter, and the merchant repaid him. There was no due process to speak of. He served sixty lashes and ten months in a dirt cell before the merchant died of a fever and the magistrate, embarrassed, released him.

He took the name Singh the day he walked out. He has not put down his sword since. He does not believe in vengeance — karma is the universe's accounting, not mine — but he believes in consequence. He follows the Oath where it leads. He has executed three men in the years since his release; each one was justly executed; he still says their names at sunset.

He has felt watched, the past few years. He does not know by whom or by what — the souls of the men he has executed, perhaps, or some older debt he has not yet paid. He does not flinch from it.

Class Features

Paladin — Base
Divine Sense
Level 1 · Action · 3/long rest (= 1 + Cha mod)

For 1 round, detect celestial, fiend, or undead creatures within 60 ft. (not behind total cover).

Lay on Hands
Level 1 · Action · 15-hp pool / long rest

Pool of healing equal to 5 × Paladin level = 15 hp. Touch a creature to restore HP from the pool (any number at once). 5 hp from the pool can instead cure one disease or one poison.

Significant emergency healing. Can be split into 1-hp chunks to stabilize multiple downed allies, or burned in one go for a 15-hp pick-up.

Channel Divinity · Vital Equality
Level 3 · Bonus action · 1/short or long rest

Choose two creatures (one may be himself) within 60 ft.. If both are willing, their hit points are combined and distributed equally (rounded down). Each chosen creature also gains temporary HP equal to Paladin level (3).

Niche but powerful at tier 1 — splitting HP between a near-dead ally and a healthy Aftab can stabilize a low-Con party-mate while he absorbs future hits more easily.

Channel Divinity · Mirroring Force
Level 3 · Reaction · 1/short or long rest

When a creature within 60 ft. applies a condition (poisoned, blinded, deafened, stunned, paralyzed, or a disease) to a creature other than himself, force the offending creature to make a Constitution save DC 12 (8 + PB 2 + Cha 2). On a failed save, the same condition is applied to it with the same duration and rules.

Hard counter to enemy casters who paralyze a party-mate. He can only mirror conditions applied to non-self allies, by rule.

Community doesn't believe in magic. The fishing village laughed at upriver tales. Magic was for the cities of north India, not for Sind river-folk. This belief did not survive Aftab's life. He has met things since.
Past Education
Institution of learning — adjacent to it, anyway. After his manumission, he traveled for a season with a Buddhist monk in the lower Indus who taught him to read Sanskrit and study the Upanishads and the Bhagavad Gita. Lifestyle qualifies; grants the Sanskrit language.
Event I · d20 = 2 → Fortune d10 = 6 · A golden ring from a lord

A small gold signet ring from his former master.

After his manumission, his former master gave him a small gold signet ring along with the fifty dinars and the horse. He wears it on his sword-hand; it is one of the few possessions he has not sold or traded in eleven years.

Event II · d20 = 18 · Felt followed throughout his life

A watcher he attributes to the men he has executed.

Since the false accusation and his release, he has felt watched. He suspects the souls of the three men he has justly executed under the Oath; he suspects his own paranoia from cell-years; he suspects an older karmic debt he has not yet paid. He does not flinch from it, but he has not resolved it.

Event III · d20 = 3 → Crime d10 = 2 · Falsely accused of theft

The defining injustice of his life. The Oath was sworn on the morning of his release.

The merchant outside Multan accused him of stealing after Aftab refused the merchant's daughter; no proof, no defense, sixty lashes, ten months in a dirt cell. The merchant died of fever; Aftab was released. He held — and still holds — that the merchant's death was karma's accounting, not his.

Personality

Trait

"I say the names of the men I have executed at sunset. None of them is a man I should not have executed. None of them is a man I will forget."

Ideal — Consequence · Lawful Neutral

"The merchant who lied about me died of fever before I could touch him. That was karma. My work is to be karma's instrument when karma needs hands — not to be karma itself."

Bond

"My sister, if she is alive, is somewhere on the lower Indus. I have not gone back. The day I have answered enough other debts, I will."

Flaw

"I served twelve years as a ghulam. I was good at it. Some days I miss the simplicity of obeying. Some days I am afraid of how good I was at it."

Connections

  • Orkhan-Tegin of the Yamak — They met at a caravanserai outside Merv four years ago, both hired to the same merchant's escort for a six-day haul east. Orkhan was the captain of the small mercenary brotherhood Aftab fell in with; Aftab was the new contracted blade. They recognized each other inside an hour — Seasoned Warrior on Aftab's side, the same gift on Orkhan's — and the unspoken acknowledgment between two career-killers from different traditions held them civil through six days of dust. They have crossed paths twice since, at other caravanserais. They drink (Orkhan) and abstain (Aftab) at the same table when the work permits, and they have never asked each other about the men they have killed. They will not. The fact that they have not is itself the friendship.
  • Yasmina al-Khorasani — They have not met in person. They share an inheritance neither knows the other carries: the feeling, every few weeks now, of being watched. Aftab's watcher he attributes to the men he has executed; Yasmina's she attributes to the silvered compass-disk she lost outside Mary. The two trails may be a single thread; they may be two unrelated coincidences in a country full of watchers. Aftab and Yasmina meeting on the road may be the moment one or both of them realize their watcher has a face.
From the Vizier's Hand

A man who served twelve years under a master he no longer names, and who still says the names of the men he has executed at sunset, is a man who has counted the cost of his own hand. I would have him at the head of any expedition through hostile country where the question is not can we kill, but should we have killed.
