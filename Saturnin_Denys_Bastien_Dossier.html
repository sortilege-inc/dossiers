I. Identity & Trade

Saturnin Denys Bastien is an Acadian cab driver working out of the 1920s, the kind of man whose name is too long for the people who hire him and who answers to whatever shortening they settle on. He has a car. The car runs. His French is the French his grandmother spoke, which is not the French anyone has printed in a long time, and his English is the English of a man who learned it from passengers. He charges by the run, and he prefers the fare in his pocket before the wheels turn.

He is not an academic. He is not an occultist. He has never read a Latin word he understood. He is, in the company he found himself keeping that night, the one person on the manifest with a working knowledge of a carburetor and no working knowledge of anything else the evening was about to require.

He is the cab. The cab is the trade. That is the whole of the offer.

Physical Description

Lean, clean-shaven, pale, with the kind of face that goes blank when it is being looked at by people who think they are better than him. Flat cap, navy three-piece, overcoat heavy enough for the weather wherever he is going. A pocket-watch chain across the waistcoat — not for the watch, which is cheap, but because the chain is what keeps the watch in the pocket when the road is bad. Hands that know the inside of an engine. Eyes that do an accounting of every room he enters and price each item in it for the trouble it represents.

Manner of Presence

Quiet, polite, transactional. He says “sir” and “ma’am” in the way a man says them when the saying costs him nothing and the not-saying might cost him the fare. He does not volunteer opinions. He does not ask after the business his passengers are about. When pressed, he gives a short answer and watches what is done with it. The party that hired him took his quiet for deference. It was not deference. It was the watchfulness of a man whose livelihood depends on knowing when to be elsewhere.

III. Operating Disposition

The disposition is the part that did not need the sheet. Anyone who sat in the cab learned it inside a mile.

The fare is paid first. Saturnin’s working rule, and the one piece of business he was clear about before the wheels turned: the run is paid in advance. The party who hired him paid in advance. They thought it was a courtesy. He thought it was the agreement. The two interpretations would diverge by the end of the evening.

The cab is the line. Whatever happens off the road is the passenger’s business. Whatever happens to the cab is his. He will wait. He will wait a long time, in fact. He will not wait through anything that puts the cab at risk he cannot recover from.

He does not ask. The cab driver who asks his fare what the business is in the strange old property out past the last lamp on the road does not get hired again. He had not asked. He has, by the evening of the one-shot, several reasons to wish he had, and none of those reasons are reasons he intends to honour by staying.

I’ve already been paid. I’m getting the fuck out of here. — Saturnin, at the cab, engine running

The sentence is the whole disposition compressed. The first half is the contract: the work was paid for, the work was done, the obligation is closed. The second half is the working man’s reading of a situation that has stopped being the work he was hired for and started being someone else’s problem. He does not negotiate the second half. He announces it.

IV. The Cab & What He Carried

The equipment list on the sheet is not recovered. What is recorded below is what the play of the one-shot makes inescapable: a working car, the clothes he showed up in, and the small honest tools of his trade. Nothing more is asserted.

  • The cab. A 1920s motor-car in working order, the engine of his livelihood. Damaged but not destroyed by the night. Will need work.
  • Driving clothes. Flat cap, three-piece suit, heavy overcoat, sturdy shoes — the working uniform of his trade and his station.
  • A pocket watch on its chain. Cheap. The chain is the point. The watch keeps the time he charges by.
  • The tools a driver carries. Implied by the occupation, not enumerated on the recovered sheet. A man does not drive in the 1920s without something to crank, something to wrench, and something to patch.
  • The fare, paid in advance. In his pocket before the engine turned over. The single most consequential object in the dossier.

V. The Fares

The party who hired Saturnin out to the property were academics and occultists — the standard Call of Cthulhu Investigator company: men and women with letters after their names, references they expected him to follow, and books they expected him to respect. He could not have given their names back to anyone afterwards. He did not need them.

Their names are not in this dossier. The player does not have them, and inventing them would be the kind of detail this dossier has agreed not to produce. They are recorded here as a category — the fares — which is the category Saturnin used for them at the time.

He drove them out. He listened, in the way a cabbie listens, which is to say: as much as he needed to know the route, and not one syllable past it. They paid him in advance, which a more careful party might have known better than to do. They got out at the property. He stayed with the cab.

VI. The Run to the Property

The destination was a strange property out past the last paved stretch of road. The hour was the hour at which a cabbie begins to charge extra and a passenger begins to pretend he hadn’t noticed. Saturnin charged extra. The fares paid in advance.

The exact nature of the property — what was kept there, what was happening on the grounds, what the academics and occultists had come to investigate — is, from Saturnin’s vantage, unknown. He waited in the cab. He saw what came out of the dark. He did not need a name for it to understand what kind of evening it was about to be.

What He Saw

The dossier records this with deliberate vagueness, both because Saturnin had no framework for what he was looking at and because the player’s notes do not require otherwise. He saw enough. He saw it move. He saw it come towards the cab. The taxonomy of the creatures is not his department.

VII. Now or Never

The scene plays as follows. Things have started to go wrong at the property — in the way that things go wrong in a Call of Cthulhu scenario, which is to say all at once and from a direction the academics did not have a contingency for. The party are still inside the situation. Saturnin is at the cab. The cab is running.

He gives them the choice. He gives them the choice once. He gives it the way a working man gives an ultimatum to people who have been treating him as decoration for the last several hours: clearly, in plain language, with the engine doing the punctuation for him.

Now or never. I’m leaving — with or without you. — Saturnin, the second offer, last offer

This is not a cowardice. The sheet does not have a cowardice line on it, and the play of the moment does not warrant one. It is the working man’s reading of a contract whose terms have changed without his consent. He was hired to drive. He drove. He was paid. The fare is closed. What is happening at the property is now an addendum to the agreement that no one offered to negotiate with him in advance, and which therefore has no force.

The mechanical instrument of the departure is the car. The moral instrument of it is the prior payment. He owes them nothing further. He has made that clear; he has made it clear once; he is making it clear with the engine. He goes.

VIII. After the Headlights

He gets away. The creatures reach the cab before the road can carry it fully clear — bodywork raked, glass cracked, something taken off the side of the chassis that the chassis was not built without — but the engine holds, the wheels hold, and the cab carries him out. The headlights bounce off the road back towards town. He does not look in the mirror. He has no professional reason to.

What he tells the next person who asks — the man at the garage, the woman at the kitchen window when he gets back, the constable if one ever puts the question to him — is not in the record. Whatever he tells them, it is a working man’s account of damage to his car, and it does not mention what was on the lawn at the property he had been paid to drive his passengers to.

The cab will need work. The work will get done. Saturnin will be back on the road. He will not be back to that road.

Hired by the academics. Paid in advance.

Drove the party out to the strange property past the last lamp on the road. Waited at the cab.
// the turn
Things went sideways at the property. Saw enough from the cab to know it was time.
// the ultimatum
Started the engine. Made the offer once. Did not wait a second time.
// the departure
Drove out under attack. Car damaged. Driver alive. Fare honoured.

The character ended the one-shot alive and away. The cab did not end the one-shot in the same condition. Both will be repaired. Only one will be back at the same address.
