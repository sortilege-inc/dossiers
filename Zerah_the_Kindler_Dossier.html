On the Subject

Zerah was born in the Jewish quarter of Isfahan, the second of two surviving children, to a family the rolls of the city listed as beggars. His father Dawud sold rags when there were rags to sell and begged when there were not. His mother died of fever when Zerah was nine. He was a small, quick boy with a deep stillness in him — and one autumn, when he was twelve or thirteen, a wandering monk of the Fire Sages saw that stillness and asked his father if the boy would come to the mountain. His father said yes. The boy walked four days into the high country with the stranger and did not see Isfahan again for sixteen years.

The monastery sits high above a smoke-mouthed crack in the rock, where the everlasting flame burns and the rain never quite extinguishes it. The monks of the order say the flame is older than the prophets — older than Abraham, older than Zoroaster, older than naming. Zerah learned to fight and to sit and to hold a coal in his palm without flinching. He learned to read by candle and to read smoke by the same patience. He learned that fire, properly understood, is a kind of memory.

He has been on the road for the past five years. The order sends its kindlers down into the cities to do what work the world requires of them — heal where they can, cauterize where they must, watch the flames in others' hearts. He carries little. He has a friend in the abbot who took him in; he has another friend in the wealthy Isfahani merchant who endows the order's mountain house. He has felt eyes on him since he was a child — eyes that do not belong to either friend, that follow him in the dark even in the monastery's deepest courtyards. He has not asked the abbot about it. He suspects the abbot knows.

He once loved a woman in the lowlands — a baker's daughter in a town two valleys from the monastery — and parted from her badly. He has not seen her in three years. He thinks of her sometimes when he kindles a hearth.

Background — Outcast

Guiding Winds

Zerah can find fresh water sources, fruitful hunting grounds, and safe camping spots away from natural hazards.

Specialty · Two Worlds Outside

Born of the Jewish quarter; taken to the mountain monastery before adolescence. He carries the outsider status of both worlds — neither fully of the city he was born in nor of the lowlands he now walks. The monastery is the only place that has ever felt like home.

Profession

Silk Road PEX system · Apprentice rank

Tinker
Apprentice · Rank 1 PEX 0

A winter a few years ago, an itinerant tinker took shelter at the monastery during a storm. He stayed three months, fixing what was broken in the mountain house in exchange for board, and Zerah — drawn to the man's quiet, focused work — became his apprentice in his free hours. The tinker moved on at the thaw. Zerah is now formally a novice at the craft and looks for opportunities to practice. No abilities purchased yet; PEX 0.

Available to purchase at 50 PEX each — Apprentice abilities: Necessary Tools (Tinker's Tools + a skill), Searching for the Needle (+5 Passive Perception; reroll on Search), Substance Detection (understand an object's composition on touch), Streetwise (Thieves' cant + Persuasion proficiency).

Connections

  • Arman of the Black Forge — They met at a Sufi-run roadhouse in the hills above Isfahan eighteen months ago, the night a winter storm pinned six travelers under one roof. Zerah was on his way down from the Mountain House on an errand for the abbot; Arman was working an itinerant repair-route between Persianate towns. They got to talking about fire — the only subject Zerah talks about freely with strangers — and discovered, by candlelight, that they hold mirrored cosmologies. The conversation was the only conversation Zerah has had with a stranger about fire that did not bore him within five minutes. Arman is wrong, Zerah is certain, about the nature of the flame — Erlik's underworld-fire is a derivative reading, not the original — but he is wrong in interesting ways, and he is wrong because he asked the question seriously enough to get an answer at all. Zerah has thought about Arman more than he expected to in the eighteen months since. He has not sought him out. He wonders, sometimes, what he would say if he did.
  • Farideh al-Saffar — The merchant-patron who endows the Mountain House has, for the last six years, also been one of Farideh's standing customers — a high-end Isfahani man who orders specific rose-and-cedar oils for his household twice a year and pays in Baghdad silver. Zerah has carried the man's correspondence down from the mountain twice. He has never met Farideh. The merchant once mentioned her name in passing and Zerah remembered it because the merchant pronounced it with unusual care. If they meet, Zerah will know within a sentence that he holds her name on the merchant's authority, and will have the unusual problem of deciding what, if anything, to tell her about her own customer.
