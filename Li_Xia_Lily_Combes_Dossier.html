I. Case Summary

Li Xia — her legal name, though she has answered to Lily Combes since her 1947 marriage — is the daughter of Cantonese immigrants who entered the United States illegally in 1922, settling in California's agricultural interior. She was born in the United States and carries full citizenship. Her older brother, 李勇 Li Yong, was born on the mainland and grandfathered into citizenship through his service with the 407th Air Service Squadron. The family's legal standing in this country has always been contingent, borrowed, earned by proximity to American military purpose — a fact she has absorbed at a cellular level and that shapes every calculation she makes.

She is, at campaign start, a woman of twenty-eight managing three overlapping identities with the calm efficiency of someone who has never had the luxury of a single one. She is the discreet professional at the Jet Propulsion Laboratory, navigating the fraternal politics of a rocket programme still half in love with its own mystique. She is the dutiful wife of a USAF officer currently posted to Korea, maintaining a household and a reputation in Pasadena that permits her to do the former job. And she is the quiet asset of the Federal Bureau of Investigation, to whom she agreed to pass certain information concerning the work of Dr. Qian Xuesen — on the explicit condition that she be permitted to remain ignorant of what the Bureau intends to do with it.

That condition is the most important thing to understand about this file. She is not naive. She understands she is participating in something. She has simply decided that the boundary of her complicity ends at the edge of her own knowledge, and she is defending that boundary with considerable determination.

Handler's Note — Entry Point

Li Xia enters Delta Green's orbit through her posting as personal assistant to Dr. Raymond Kellerman, a JPL rocket scientist whose research interests have drifted considerably further than the Bureau appreciates. Kellerman inherited several of Jack Parsons' more esoteric organisational connections along with his desk. Lily's access to Kellerman's correspondence and filing system makes her the shortest path between Delta Green and whatever Kellerman is working toward. She does not know this. Yet.

II. Background

Origins & Family

Her parents crossed into California in 1922, one of tens of thousands of Chinese labourers and their families who continued to enter despite the Exclusion Acts, absorbing the associated legal precarity as the cost of the geography they needed. They settled in the Central Valley. Lily grew up bilingual and bicultural in the way that American children of illegal immigrants typically do: fluent in the public performance of Americanness, and equally fluent in everything that performance excluded.

Her brother Li Yong fought in the 407th Air Service Squadron and brought the family's citizenship off the contingency list. Lily carries that debt in her understanding of what military service means for people whose presence here was never fully ratified. It explains a great deal about the choices she has made since.

Wartime Service

She worked briefly as a translator after Pearl Harbor — a period she declines to discuss in detail — before enlisting with the Madame Chiang Kai-shek Air WAC in 1943 at nineteen. Her primary posting trained her in the review and interpretation of aerial surveillance photography; by the end of the war she had developed the systematic visual patience that the job required and that has served every position she has held since. She served in the Philippines, which is where she met Jeremy Combes.

Marriage & Education

Jeremy Combes was a USAAF officer. The precise nature of their courtship is outside the scope of this file; what matters is that after the war ended they moved to Hawai'i together, married in early 1947, and Lily spent the next two years completing a Bachelor's degree in East Asian languages at the University of Hawai'i. Jeremy began making extended trips to California during her final year as he established a liaison function between the USAF and the Jet Propulsion Laboratory on the subjects of experimental aircraft and future air strategies. When Lily graduated in 1949, she followed him west.

Note on Marriage

The marriage to Jeremy Combes appears functional from the outside — maintained household, maintained correspondence during his Korea posting, no separation proceedings on file. The private arithmetic is more complicated. There has been at least one affair on her part, now terminated, that produced a pregnancy that was not. The child exists. Jeremy does not know about either the affair or the child. Both facts are active leverage points, though Delta Green does not currently hold them.

JPL Career

Through her WAC contacts, she found initial work at the Space and Missile Systems Center on LORAN station synchronisation improvements — a research assistance role that was correctly matched to her aerial surveillance training. When the Korean War sent Jeremy to Korea, she transferred to a secretarial position at the JPL itself. She was good at the job within a month. Within a year she had built, through a combination of aggressive administrative networking and a brand of stern soft power that her colleagues find difficult to characterise accurately, the kind of institutional indispensability that senior researchers compete for.

Her friendship with Jiang Ying — Qian Xuesen's wife, a concert pianist and the social centre of the JPL's Chinese-American intellectual circle — became the fulcrum on which her promotion turned. She is now personal assistant to Dr. Raymond Kellerman. She is very good at this job also. She is beginning to wonder what it is, exactly, that Dr. Kellerman's job actually is.

III. Current Situation

The FBI Arrangement

The Bureau approached her approximately four months ago. They want information relating to Qian Xuesen's research activities — specifically documentation that would support the political effort to have his security clearance revoked and, ultimately, to have him deported. She agreed to cooperate. Her condition of cooperation was explicit and documented: she will pass documents. She will not be told what the Bureau intends to do with them, what case they are building, or against whom the information will be used. She has made her peace with this arrangement by refusing to follow any of the implications to their conclusions. She passes paper. What happens to the paper is not her knowledge.

This is not stupidity. It is a very specific moral architecture that she has built with considerable care. It is also structurally fragile — one correctly-placed piece of information about what the Bureau actually intends could bring the whole thing down. She knows this too. She is, among other things, betting that nobody will give her that information.

Operational Concern

Kellerman's files contain material the Bureau has not requested and does not know exists. Delta Green's interest in this file begins and ends with that material. The FBI's investigation of Xuesen is a parallel track. If Lily discovers that Delta Green is distinct from, and operating independently of, the Bureau's political operation, her cooperation calculus changes entirely. She agreed to work with one agency. She will be considerably more resistant to two.

Dr. Kellerman

Raymond Kellerman is a legitimate rocket scientist. He is also the inheritor, in both institutional and social terms, of Jack Parsons' stranger connections. Several of the fraternal and occult organisations Parsons worked through remain active in Pasadena; several of their members are regular guests at Kellerman's home. Lily has attended two of these dinner gatherings as part of her role. She found them odd. She has not yet found them alarming. The documents in Kellerman's correspondence files are further along that spectrum than the dinners suggested, and she has not yet read them with enough attention to understand what she is looking at.

This is, for Delta Green's purposes, still recoverable. She is observant, capable, and clearance-adjacent. She can be guided toward the right files by someone who knows what to look for. The question is how to accomplish this without disclosing the operational context in terms that will cause her to withdraw.

SIGINT 90 is the number that defines the sheet. It is an exceptional value — she reads signals intelligence, intercepts, and coded communications with the trained fluency of a career analyst. Combined with Search 70 and the Special Training in aerial reconnaissance, she is one of the better document-and-imagery analysts Delta Green can access without going to a formal intelligence agency. VIII. Operational Assessment

What She Is Good At

She is an exceptional analyst. SIGINT 90 is not a number that gets assigned by accident — it reflects someone who has spent the better part of a decade reading documents that meant one thing on the surface and something else underneath, and who has learned to do this quickly and without appearing to look. Search 70, Alertness 70, the aerial reconnaissance training: she finds things. She finds them before other people notice she is looking.

She is an exceptional administrative operator. Bureaucracy 70 and Persuade 70 in combination mean that she moves paper and people with equal fluency. She has built a network inside JPL that predates her current posting and will outlast it. People do things for her not because they are afraid of her but because they respect her and because she has, at some point, done something for them first. She keeps track. She is very good at keeping track.

She is composed under sustained pressure. The last three years — the affair, the child, the FBI arrangement, Jeremy in Korea, Kellerman's accelerating strangeness — represent a stress load that would have produced visible fracture in most people. She has not fractured. She is managing it. The management is exerting a cost, but that cost is not yet visible from the outside.

She does not make decisions quickly. She makes them once. — Observation, source withheld, JPL, 1951

What She Will Not Do

She will not accept operational parameters she cannot understand the surface of. Her FBI arrangement is the template: she cooperated, but only after establishing what she would and would not know. Any handler approaching her needs to offer a similarly bounded frame — something that permits her to participate without requiring her to follow the implications all the way down. This is not cowardice. She is not afraid of what is at the bottom. She is afraid of what she will be required to become if she goes there knowingly.

She will not burn Jiang Ying. The Bureau wants Xuesen's files; she can provide access to JPL documentation because the institution is abstract. She will not take any action that directly damages the woman who is, at this particular point in her life, the closest thing she has to a genuine friend. If Delta Green's operational needs intersect with the Bureau's investigation of Xuesen in a way that makes this choice unavoidable, she will choose Jiang Ying. The handler should plan around this.

She will not function as a field operative. She is not trained for it, does not want to be trained for it, and will correctly identify that she is being treated as one if the operational asks start resembling field work. She can provide intelligence. She can move within her existing access. She is not a trigger-puller, a confrontation operator, or a containment asset. The unarmed combat score reflects WAC training that she has not maintained. The firearms score reflects basic familiarity with a service pistol that she has not fired since 1945.

The Kellerman Variable

She likes Dr. Kellerman. This complicates matters. He is technically brilliant and personally peculiar in ways she has categorised as harmless eccentricity — the dinner gatherings, the correspondence with people whose letter headings she has chosen not to read carefully, the particular quality of his enthusiasm for certain research lines. She has built her professional stability partly on the assumption that he is strange but not dangerous. The documentation that will require her to revise this assessment is in his files. She has handled those files. She has not read them with the right frame. Delta Green's job, at one level, is to provide the frame.

The more important question is what she does when she reads them correctly. She will recognise them for what they are — she is too good an analyst not to. The Sanity cost is real and will need to be managed. But the more operationally significant moment is what comes after: she will want to know who knew, how long they knew, and whether they sent her into that office with that knowledge already in their possession. If Delta Green's approach has been honest, she survives that moment. If it has not, she doesn't survive it functionally. She will either withdraw entirely or, more dangerously, start looking for who else has been watching her and why.

Handler Recommendation

Approach honestly within a bounded frame. Tell her what she needs to know to participate. Do not tell her what you do not need her to know. Do not lie about what she will encounter. She will find out; she is better at finding things out than anyone she has met so far realises. She is an asset, not a pawn. Treat her accordingly and she is one of the most capable analysts Delta Green can access at a civilian level in 1952 California. Treat her as a pawn and she will know it before the second meeting, and you will have made an enemy with SIGINT 90, Bureaucracy 70, and a direct line into JPL's filing system.

IX. Possible Trajectories

The Document That Changes Everything

At some point she will read the right file in Kellerman's correspondence. This is not a contingency — it is a certainty; the question is when, and who is in the room when she does it, and whether she has been prepared for it or is experiencing it cold. A cold read, alone, with no context: Sanity test, no support, breaking point at 40 from a current value of 50. A prepared read, with someone who has told her something true about what she might find: still costs something, but she processes it rather than fragmenting. The campaign gets to decide which version this is.

The Bureau Problem

Qian Xuesen will have his security clearance revoked in September 1950 — if the campaign is running close to historical time, this has already happened or is happening now. His deportation will follow by years of legal contest. Lily does not yet know she has been participating in this. When she finds out — and she will find out, she reads newspapers and she knows the names — the moral architecture that keeps her functional will need to be rebuilt from different materials. What she does in that moment is the most interesting character question in the file: does she blame herself, the Bureau, Jeremy's world that recruited her into it, or the circumstances that made a woman in 1952 America a person whose cooperation could be assumed?

The Child

The child is not an abstraction. The child is a person who exists in 1952 and is, at current estimate, somewhere between a few months and a year old. The child will grow up. The child will eventually need to be accounted for in terms that do not include the current arrangement. If this campaign runs long enough for that accounting to arrive, it is among the most significant character events available to this sheet — not because of what it means for the plot, but because of what it requires her to decide about who she is and what she is willing to be honest about.

What She Will Not Be

She will not become a field operative. She will not become a true believer in any of the ideological frames Delta Green deploys for the benefit of its newer assets. She is too old for that and has seen too much institutional machinery. She will become someone who knows what she knows, manages what she manages, and does the work that is in front of her with the same professional competence she has applied to every job she has held since 1943. Delta Green does not get her heart. Delta Green gets her skills, her access, and her judgment. For most operations, this is considerably more useful.

She will not be unchanged. Nobody is. The question is what the campaign costs her and what she is carrying at the end of it that she was not carrying at the beginning. That is, in the end, the only question worth asking about anyone in this file.
