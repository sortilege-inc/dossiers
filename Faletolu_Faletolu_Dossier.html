III. Class & Race Features

Two class levels. The sheet records Bard 1; the multiclass into Barbarian 1 is the stated intended configuration. What both classes give at level 1 is rules-given and not invented here.

Barbarian (Level 1) — Path of the Berserker

Rage

Two uses per long rest. On his turn, as a bonus action: advantage on Strength checks and saving throws, +2 bonus to damage rolls on Strength-based weapon attacks, resistance to bludgeoning, piercing, and slashing damage. Lasts up to 1 minute; ends early if he ends his turn without attacking a hostile creature or taking damage. Cannot cast or concentrate on spells while raging. At level 1, a berserker does not yet have Frenzy — that arrives at Barbarian 3. What he has is the foundation.

Unarmored Defense

While not wearing armour, AC equals 10 + Dexterity modifier + Constitution modifier. With Dex −1 and Con 0, this yields AC 9 — marginally worse than the recorded AC 10, which suggests he may have been wearing light armour or that the sheet was recorded at a different configuration. The figure on the sheet (AC 10) is what is recorded.

Bard (Level 1) — College Unrecorded

Bardic Inspiration

A number of uses per long rest equal to his Charisma modifier (1 use). One creature he can see within 60 feet gains a Bardic Inspiration die — a d6 at this level — which the recipient can roll and add to one ability check, attack roll, or saving throw within the next 10 minutes. He cannot inspire himself. At Charisma 13 (+1), he has one use per long rest. Potent conceptually; limited mechanically at this level.

Spellcasting

Two cantrips known; two first-level spells known; two first-level spell slots per long rest. The specific selections are not recorded. Given the character concept — martial epic singer, haka-style performance — College of Valor is the mechanically and thematically natural home, but this was not confirmed and is not stated as fact here.

Jack of All Trades

Half his proficiency bonus (+1) added to any ability check that does not already use his proficiency bonus. Fills out several of the otherwise-dead skill values on the sheet — the Acrobatics +1 is this feature at work against a −1 Dexterity modifier.

Song of Rest

During a short rest, if he or any friendly creature uses hit dice to regain hit points, they can add a d6. A small sustaining function, appropriate to the role of the skald in camp as much as in battle.

IV. Concept & Character

Six Houses

The name is a statement. Faletolu means "three houses." He carries it twice — Faletolu Faletolu — so he is six houses, a doubled claim, a man who takes up enough space to need the name said again. Whether this is lineage, title, self-bestowed reputation, or all three, is not established. What it communicates is clear enough.

The Skald

He is not a bard in the lute-and-tavern sense. The model is older and louder: the warrior-singer, the man who memorises the deeds of the fallen and beats them into the living, who performs not for applause but to put iron in the blood. Think Athenian paeans before battle. Think the haka — a challenge that is also a declaration, a physical assertion that the body making the sound is not afraid of the body receiving it.

He loves epic poetry. Given the opportunity, he will hold an audience for hours with accounts of battles — his own, his forebears', anyone's — rendered with the full formal apparatus of the tradition: the boast, the catalogue of the fallen, the hero's address before the charge. The problem is that he tends to be substantially intoxicated when the battles in question occur, and his retellings blend what happened with what he hallucinated was happening, to a degree he cannot himself reliably distinguish. The result is accounts of genuine events with giants in them, or genuine giants with the dimensions slightly wrong. The epics are glorious. Their relationship to the historical record is loose.

The Trickster

His deity is a trickster — name unrecorded, nature implied. A god who enjoys a good insult, who takes the side of the man willing to speak plainly and profanely in public. Faletolu's most reliable income stream when times are lean is hiring himself out as an insulter-for-hire: he will confront a designated target in a public place and insult them. Eloquently. Elaborately. With genuine feeling and considerable volume. There is a craft to it. He is good at it. He does not consider it beneath him. His god, presumably, concurs.

The ferocious temper is real. So is the absence of grudges. He gets as good as he gives, and both are accepted as part of the same transaction. The anger burns hot and burns clean. There is no sullenness in him, no accumulation of resentment. He is the most straightforward kind of dangerous man: one who means exactly what he says at the moment he says it, including the threats.

The Body

Strength 16. The numbers confirm what the concept requires. He is physically enormous — a wall, a palisade, a geological feature that has learned to walk and developed opinions. The Wisdom of 5 is the other side of the same coin: he is not cautious, not perceptive in the careful sense, not interested in hesitation. What he lacks in situational awareness he compensates for in the sheer correctness of the assumption that he can take most of what the world is likely to throw at him.

This is not, mechanically, always accurate. He has 8 hit points.

The Fearlessness

Player-stated: fearless to a reckless extent. This is not courage in the philosophical sense — it is not the deliberate choice to act despite fear. It is the simpler and more dangerous version: he does not experience the fear that the situation is presenting. The haka is not theatrical bravado. It is an accurate report of his internal state.

VI. Assessment

The character is mechanically coherent with the concept in the ways that count. The high Strength and the Barbarian chassis give him the physical scaffolding the concept requires. The Bard gives him the mouth. The low Wisdom confirms what the concept implies: he is not a man who pauses to reconsider. The 8 hit points are the only number on the sheet that feels out of step — a man described as fearless and built like architecture should, in the intuitive sense, be harder to kill. He is not, yet. The Barbarian's hit dice will remedy this.

The mechanical tension is interesting. A Bard cannot cast spells while Raging. He is a man whose two class features are in fundamental conflict: the epic singer and the berserker cannot both be operating at full capacity at the same time. This is probably the correct dramatic design. The question the character poses — whether the song sustains the fury or the fury silences the song — is not one that level 1 can answer. It is the question the arc is built around.

The trickster deity is the wildcard. A trickster does not want a simple berserker; a trickster wants a berserker who also knows what he is doing to the room. Faletolu is both. The deity, unnamed, is presumably satisfied.

What is known

A name that doubles itself into a boast. A Strength score that justifies the boast. A Wisdom score that explains why the boast sometimes lands him in difficulties. A god who rewards the man willing to say the true and offensive thing in a public space. A past full of battles he remembers somewhat differently from how they occurred. A performance tradition that is also a threat display. The intended shape of an arc — skald becoming berserker, or berserker who was always also a skald — that this record cannot confirm was reached.
