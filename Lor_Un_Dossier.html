1.Identity (such as it is)

Lor Un is a Human Troubadour, Auteur subclass, Performer career, Level 1, Wealth 1, Renown 1 from career, no XP yet. Her ancestry traits are oriented around resistance to the supernatural — which is most things in this world. Her class is built for talking, redirecting, and rewriting scenes in her favor.

She is, in addition, Lauren, age 20, who was in the middle of an undergraduate semester before this happened. She did improv in high school. She has not done it well since.

Lauren: Marcus DM'd. He's been running this campaign with us since freshman year. Priya is Maike. Jordan is Vellpryte. I was going to be the orc fury. Lor Un was my second draft because I changed my mind about wanting to play someone who hits things. Now the orc fury is hitting things in my apartment. My apartment has an orc fury in it.

The dossier you are reading is being kept in real time. It is also, mechanically, the same character sheet PDF that exists on Lauren's laptop somewhere, which means there are two of it now — the one in her dorm and the one in her hand. Both are accurate. Neither is comforting.

3.Drama — the heroic resource

The Troubadour's heroic resource is Drama: narrative momentum, accrued from the heightened moments of an encounter. It gains aggressively — the Troubadour rewards combats that look important by the metrics a story would use.

TriggerGain
Start of your turn1d3
The first time three or more heroes use an ability on the same turn+2
The first time any hero is made winded during the encounter+2
Whenever a creature within your line of effect rolls a natural 19 or 20+3
When you or another hero dies+10

The come-back clause

From the class text, recorded verbatim: "When you are dead, you continue to gain drama during combat as long as your body is intact. If you have 30 drama during the encounter in which you died, you can come back to life with 1 Stamina and 0 drama (no action required). If you are still dead after the encounter in which you died, you can't gain drama during future encounters."

Lauren — annotation Read that paragraph again. The class I picked has a respawn timer. You die, you keep accruing the death-of-a-hero bonus while your body lies there, and at thirty drama you stand back up. None of the other characters in this cell have this on their sheet. Whatever did the swap put me in the body of the only person at the table whose class can come back from being dead. I am trying to decide whether that is a courtesy or a setup.
Lauren: Also: the line that reads "+10 drama when another hero dies." My party getting killed makes me more powerful, on a sheet that gets me back if I die. I have read enough of these to know that this is what a horror story's protagonist sheet looks like.

5.Troubadour Class Features

"The whole world's a stage, and everyone on it, an actor. No one knows this better than the troubadour."

Always active — Scene Partner

Whenever she succeeds on a test to interact with an NPC using a skill from the interpersonal group, she can form a bond with that NPC. When she enters a negotiation with a bonded NPC, their patience increases by 1 (max 5). Additionally, the first time during a negotiation that she personally makes an argument that would increase a bonded NPC's interest by 1, she instead increases their interest by 2 (max 5).

She can have 1 active bond at level 1 (= level). When she would form a new bond that exceeds the limit, she chooses which existing bond to drop.

Always active — Routines

She enters every battle with a set of Performance abilities at the ready. Performances are magical presentations (songs, dances, poems, gymnastic feats) that allies can participate in. At the start of each combat round, as long as she is not dazed, dead, or surprised, she can either choose a new performance or maintain her current one (no action required). Performances last until she's unable to maintain one or until the encounter ends.

12.Inciting Incident — Trading Places

From the Draw Steel inciting incident: "You had been playing a game of Draw Steel with your friends from college. Your character was an orc fury, when all of a sudden you felt yourself dropped into the game, your actions now driven by the orc you had played."

What's established
  • Lauren was playing Draw Steel with her friends.
  • Her character was an orc fury (not Lor Un — the orc fury was a separate, earlier character).
  • The swap took her into the game world and brought the orc fury into hers.
  • The orc fury is now driving Lauren's real-world body.
  • Lauren has the memory of being Lauren. She knows the game is a game.
A note on the sheet

The sheet says "Lor Un, Human Troubadour." Not orc fury. The Inciting Incident text says her character was an orc fury — meaning the orc fury was the player character she had been controlling when the swap occurred. Lor Un is not that character. Lor Un is the body Lauren woke up in — a Human Troubadour built to a sheet that, on every detail Lauren can check, matches a sheet she had previously rolled for a different game.

The simplest reading is: the swap put Lauren into a character she had also designed — the "second draft" Troubadour she shelved in favor of the orc fury. Whether the sheet existed in this world before Lauren arrived in it, or whether her arrival created the sheet to match a body, is — at this point — beyond what the character sheet documents.

Lauren: I made Lor Un three weeks ago. I shelved her in favor of the orc fury because Marcus was running a campaign with combat in it and I thought a Troubadour would die first. Lor Un sat in a folder called "backup characters" on my Discord. The body I am in now is the body Lor Un had on the sheet. The face is the face on my driver's license. Both of those statements are simultaneously true. I am writing this down because if I don't I will lose track of which is which.

13.Position in Collateral draft

Provisional · drawn from campaign concept

The Collateral campaign concept identifies Lauren/Lor Un's premise as the meta-horror angle: a real-world player has been swapped with her character. The orc fury is now in the real world wearing Lauren's life. Lauren is here. Details are subject to Session Zero.

The premise: the cell wakes in a holding room and is told "The debt has defaulted. You belong to the Saffron Seal now." Of the prisoners, Lauren is the only one with no in-world history — she has been here for four days. She also, alone, knows that the system she is mechanically operating under is a tabletop role-playing game.

Role on the team

Combat: support caster and turn-flipper. Cutting Sarcasm at 10 squares for psychic damage with bleeding rider, Quick Rewrite as a 3-cube area control, Flip the Script as a 5-Drama team teleport. Turnabout Is Fair Play turns enemies' edges into banes (or, for 3 Drama, double banes). The Troubadour does not deal heavy damage — she changes the math of other people's rolls.

Out of combat: face, negotiator, and interrogator. Eight Interpersonal skills, So-Tell-Me follow-up after any successful Presence test, Scene Partner bond-forming on successful interpersonal interactions. She can also identify supernatural creatures from another world within 5 squares (Detect the Supernatural). She does not yet know whether the ability rings on her own arrival.

Faction connections

None established. Lauren did not exist in this world a week ago. Whoever named her as collateral did so for reasons that do not predate her arrival — which constrains the possible authors considerably. Candidates: whoever did the swap; whoever benefits from Lauren being in this cell specifically; whoever issues debt instruments naming creatures-from-another-world. Any or all of these may overlap.

Lauren — annotation The campaign concept calls this "meta-horror." I want to note that being inside it does not feel like horror per se. It feels like having woken up in a house someone else rented for me. I do not yet have words for what it will feel like the first time I get hurt here, or the first time I have to spend a Recovery to get back up, or the first time I roll dice in front of my new teammates and watch them not understand why the roll is happening. Those are the things I am bracing for. Marcus would have called them "the actual horror beats." Marcus is not here.

14.Character Arc

Three threads.

The first is which body she gets back, if any. The orc fury is in Lauren's life. Lauren is in Lor Un's. The campaign assumes she'd want to return; the assumption is doing work. There is also the version where the orc fury is doing a competent job and Lauren, given a real choice, builds a life here instead. The mechanical sheet — Troubadour, Auteur, eight Interpersonal skills, the come-back-at-30-drama clause — supports both endings.

The second is what counts as her past. Detect the Supernatural names her as a human from this world. The sheet has cultural language slots she filled with bespoke choices. The campaign will need to decide whether the worldbuilding around her — the College Conclave, the Performer career history, the bonded NPCs she might form — refers to events that happened in this world, or to events Lauren remembers from hers, or to a confused middle category in which the answer is "yes."

The third is the fourth wall and what it costs her. Lauren knows this is Draw Steel. She knows the rules. Her teammates do not. The campaign asks whether her knowledge is an advantage — she can flip the script literally and metaphorically — or whether the cost of being the only person at the table who knows it's a table is a kind of loneliness that, eventually, the other characters will read as something else. The sardonic register is part of the protection. It is also, structurally, what wears off.
