I. Identity & Station

Squire Llwyd ap Geraint, called Paun — the Peacock — is a squire of the Wolves of Vagon, attached to Sir Eamon "the Fox" and serving his second active season in the field. He is seventeen years old, Cymric of the Belgae tribe, British Christian, and the orphaned son of Sir Geraint ap Llwyd, who fell at the Battle of Kent in 457 on the rebel side against Vortigern's tyranny. He is tall, handsome to the point of being a problem, possessed of a singing voice that stops conversations — and beneath all of that, a young man with his father's vengeance sitting unspoken under his breastbone, his uncle's blood on Kent Saxon hands, and a girl in the audience whose laugh he can pick out of a crowded hall.

Physical Description

Strapping for his age and still growing into himself — tall (SIZ 14), well-built, with the kind of natural athleticism that has not yet had to be trained for. Dark, curly hair that catches the firelight. The improbable straight white teeth that earned him a nickname from older squires before he had earned anything else. A face that does what fashionable young women's faces do in chapel windows. He wears a squire's serviceable wool and leather, a wooden cross at his throat that was his mother's, and his father's spare hunting knife when he can manage it. His armour — aketon, gambeson, haubergeon, open helm — is good, well-kept, and protects him better than it has any right to for his rank. He does not carry himself like a beautiful boy. He carries himself like a beautiful boy who has been quietly told, since he was twelve, not to.

The Voice

His most remarkable trait. Rich, melodious, well-pitched. In a hall full of Cymric storytellers and harpers, his Singing 11 and his harp at 6 do not make him the best musician in the room — but the voice itself, the instrument before any instrument, makes him the one people lean toward. He composed and performed the Lay of Squire's Hill in 465 and was awarded Glory for the performance as well as for the battle. He is aware of what the voice can do. He has not yet decided what to do with that awareness.

Manner of Presence

Humble despite his looks, in the specific Cymric Christian way that knows pride is the first deadly sin and has been told so often. His Modest 13 is dominant and his Spiritual 12 is dominant; his bearing reflects both. He speaks more carefully when noble women are within earshot, not from calculation but because his mother trained him so. His Cymric is his first language; his court Latin is functional but plain. Under stress, he gets quieter and more correct, not louder — the Sir Eamon school of presence, learned by watching the master at every feast since November.

Combat Loadout

Aketon (AP 2, padded) under Gambeson (AP 4) under Haubergeon (AP 4, mail) — the proper layered loadout of a Cymric squire of means. Open helm (AP 1) for visibility and breath. Large round shield (AP 6, −2 weapon skill when mounted).

Weapons

Cheap sword (parry 3, breaks on a fumble), one-handed spear, two-handed spear (his choice from horseback), great mace (his choice on foot — +3D6 vs mail, which is the Saxons), dagger, selfbow, wooden practice sword. He has no warhorse of his own yet; he rides what the Wolves provide.

What APP 16 means in practice

His appearance is the stat the campaign has tested most. Lady Efa propositioned him; he deflected with the line about Sir Eamon and the word "fortunate." Lady Branwen requested him to dance at Midsummer 462 when he was fifteen and she was thirteen, and remembered him well enough that his performance of the Lay of Squire's Hill in her hearing this year was registered separately for Glory. He does not perform the looks. He is simply, structurally, the most-watched young man in any room he enters that does not also contain Sir Eamon — and even then it is a near thing.

What Valorous 17 / Vengeful 13 mean together

This is the most dangerous pair of numbers on the sheet. Valorous 17 means he will fight when he should fight, and fight when he should run, and fight when he should call for help. Vengeful 13 dominant over Forgiving 7 means that when the fight is with Saxons — the people who killed his uncle at the Giant's Dance two years ago, and arguably (through Vortigern's policies) his father at Kent eight years ago — he is not balanced. His major wound in the Saxon engagement following Bramblethorn (Prophecy of the Dragons, late winter 465) is the first instance of this pattern hurting him. It will not be the last.

Aspirational Heraldry

Blazon (to be borne upon knighthood)
Per bend azure and vert, a sun or.

A golden sun on a diagonal field of blue and green — sky over earth, radiance rising from the land. He has not earned it yet. He has drawn it once on a scrap of parchment for himself and put it away. The sun on his shield has not yet risen; when it does, it will mean the darkness his father died fighting has finally begun to lift.

III. Operating Disposition

Paun has begun to develop an operating doctrine where Tiphaine has not. He has eighteen months of squireship behind him, two named battles, three categories of adversary killed (Saxons, Barghests, giants), one Glory-worthy rescue, and Sir Eamon's direct tutelage since January 465. He moves with more confidence than the eschille's youngest squires. He is also less interior about it.

Default Posture

Forward. Where Tiphaine watches, Paun does — he fetches the water Sir Rhain asked for, kicks Cináeda awake when Cináeda needs it, charges the bandit, chases the fleeing one alone into the woods. Valorous 17 and Energetic 9 do not quite agree about this, but the Valorous wins every roll that matters. He is not impulsive — Prudent 13 holds — but he closes distance more readily than the eschille's other squires.

When Asked Directly

He tells what he understands to be the truth. Honest 9 is balanced rather than dominant; he will round corners off a story to spare a friend, or to keep his Modest 13 from having to detail his own accomplishments. He does not lie when it matters. He may, occasionally, fail to volunteer.

When Pushed

He moves into the push. Valorous 17 will not allow a backward step that looks like fear; Modest 13 will not allow a forward step that looks like swagger. The result is a young man who simply does not yield and also does not escalate — he stands where he was and waits for the matter to clarify. The Wolves have not yet found the edge of this.

In the Field

Competent and seasoning. He defeated Caradoc in the autumn sparring; he was overwhelmed by Gorthyn in a different bout and absorbed the lesson. He chose the great mace for foot work and the spear for mounted work — both reasonable, both played to his strength (13) rather than his dexterity (11). In the bandit fight at Bramblethorn he charged and struck a bandit with the mace, then chased a fleeing one into the woods alone — the Valorous-Vengeful pattern showing under low stakes, the kind that will show under high stakes if it is not corrected. At the quintain he was knocked down by the counterweight; he got back up.

In Court

This is where he is most distinctive. The seven courtly skills at 11 are a real arsenal, and he deploys them — not as Tiphaine refuses to, but with the easy confidence of a young man who knows the room is paying attention. He sang at the Session 3 minor feast and gained admiration; he discussed falconry with Sir Hiawell at the same feast and again at the autumn hunt, gracefully concealing (or perhaps not concealing) that he does not actually know much about it. He held composure with Lady Efa where most squires would have either accepted or fumbled the deflection. The voice does the work the looks do not.

Toward Saxons

Conflicted in a way he has not yet fully examined. Hate Saxons 8 is no longer a hot passion — it has been worn down from the inherited 15, either by play or by other passions rising past it. But Vengeful 13 remains structural. The thing he hates the Saxons for is not the same thing his father hated them for; his father's hatred was political (Vortigern's policies, the dispossession of the Cymri), while his own is personal (the Kent Saxons murdered his uncle at the Giant's Dance when he was sixteen). He has not yet had a Saxon at his mercy. The first time he does will be a campaign event.

Toward the Fae

Wary, in the specific way of a family that has been cursed by them. His father offended the fae hunting in 450 and the family Hunting skill has carried the −5 penalty since. He killed Barghests in 465 and was Glorified for it; he killed giants in the same year. The fae and the supernatural are not abstract to him. He notices in the world more than he says.

IV. Interior Architecture

The defining structural fact about Paun's interior is a three-part tension between the boy his looks make people expect, the man his sheet says he is becoming, and the inheritance his father left him.

The Surface

What the court sees: a handsome, humble, devout Cymric squire with a beautiful voice and proper manners. Modest 13, Spiritual 12, Generous 11. The nickname Paun — Peacock — was given to him by older squires as a joke at his expense, and he wears it without complaint because resenting it would be Proud. He performs humility well because he genuinely feels it; the appearance is not the calculation, the modesty is.

The Soldier Underneath

What play has revealed: Valorous 17 and Prudent 13 sitting on a body large enough to carry them. He is the squire who closes, and closes again. The Lay of Squire's Hill was composed about a battle he fought in, not a battle he watched. The mace work at Bramblethorn was real. The major wound in the Saxon engagement that followed was the body cashing the cheque Valorous 17 had written. He is going to be a knight who fights, not a courtier who occasionally carries a sword.

The Father's Shadow

What the sheet's vices encode: Vengeful 13, Arbitrary 15, Indulgent 11, Lazy 11. These are not large dominances except Arbitrary, but together they describe a young man who has internalised certain costs of his family's recent history. Sir Geraint kept his head down through the bad years — through the Saxon ascendancy of 447–456, through the murders of Constantine and Constans, through the dissident question — and only switched to the rebels in 456, dying at Kent in 457 with the same one-eyed determination he had brought to every previous battle. Paun's Just 5 is, in part, that inheritance: his father did not believe in abstract justice as a fixed star, and Paun has not been taught to. He believes in his people, his family, his dead. He will be a partisan knight in a partisan war.

The Religious Frame

British Christian, properly so. Devotion (God) 10, Religion (Christian) 5, Modest 13 and Spiritual 12 both dominant. The faith is not performative and not cynical; it is the air he breathes. He goes to chapel without being told. He observes the fasts when the eschille keeps them. The Lay of Squire's Hill has a thread of sun-and-darkness theology running through it — the sun rising, the dragon flying, the darkness lifting. He has not yet had to choose between Christian formation and the pagan world he is actually living in (the fae curse, Merlin's prophecy, the dragon dream the eschille's squires are sharing). When he does, the religious frame will hold — but it will not necessarily hold cleanly.

The Adoration

Branwen. Already Adoration (Branwen) 9 on the sheet, which means the campaign has registered that this is more than crush-grade. He danced with her at Midsummer 462 when he was fifteen and she was thirteen, stepped on her toes, apologised, and was teased for it; she remembered him well enough that the Lay of Squire's Hill three years later was an event marked separately on his record for her presence in the audience. He notices when she enters a room. He notices when she does not. His Chaste 10 / Lustful 10 is balanced, which in the Cymric Christian frame means he is exactly where a seventeen-year-old in love is supposed to be: tempted and trying.

Pwy a wel ei llygaid, a wel y môr.
"Whoever sees her eyes has seen the sea" — a line of his own composition, not yet sung where she could hear it

Where His Face Feels Like His Own

In the practice yard at first light, alone with the wooden sword, before anyone is awake to watch him. He drills not because Sir Eamon told him to but because it is the only place his appearance becomes irrelevant. Sweat erases the Peacock. The body is just a body, doing what it is for. When he sings later in the hall he will look like the boy the court sees. At dawn he looks like the man he is becoming.

V. Family & House Llwyd

The family is a depleted thing — rebel against Vortigern through two generations, hit hard at Kent in 457 and harder at the Night of the Long Knives in 463, scattered partly to Brittany, currently held together by an uncle's stewardship of lands Paun has not yet inherited. He is the heir, but the inheritance is small, encumbered, and contested by history.

The Family Inheritance, Mechanically

  • Hate (Saxons) Passion: rolled 15. Inherited directly from the family's collective grievance. Currently at 8 on the sheet — the campaign or the player has reduced it, perhaps through failed rolls, perhaps through deliberate cooling. The drop is itself significant.
  • Fae Curse: Hunting −5. From Sir Geraint's offence to the woodland fae in 450. Mechanical, persistent, undispelled. The inheritance includes the Glory and the political position both — rebel family, on the right side of history if Aurelius rises, on the wrong side if Vortigern's heirs ever return to power.

VI. The Wolves of Vagon

The eschille that took the Sarum squires in for the winter of 464–465 and named them in turn through January. Young knights, mostly in their twenties, based at Vagon castle west of Sarum, undefeated in skirmishes against the Saxons though they have not yet fought a battle proper. Sir Elaid is castellan. Paun is squired to Sir Eamon — the assignment was made the morning Cináeda recovered at Glastonbury, after a long night of moral instruction in the abbey courtyard.

VII. Lady Branwen ferch Cadoc

Already a passion on the sheet — Adoration (Branwen) 9, the only directed love-passion on his roll. The relationship is not hypothetical; the campaign has already registered it as one of the structural facts of his life.

Who She Is

Fifteen, marriageable age approaching, Cymric, daughter of Lord Cadoc ap Rhodri (a minor lord of the Welsh marches with an advisory role at the Salisbury court) and Lady Nest ferch Gruffydd (from a slightly more prestigious family, intensely focused on her daughter's marriage prospects). She has an elder brother Hywel (19, recently knighted, household guard at court, has already noticed Paun's interest before their parents have) and a younger sister Catrin (12, idolises Branwen, unwittingly reports her movements to their mother). She lives at court because her father's advisory role requires it.

Physical Description

Ethereally beautiful in the specific Celtic way — dark auburn hair that catches firelight, sea-green eyes that miss little, slender but not fragile, graceful in movement, expressive with her hands when she speaks. Naturally noble in bearing, tempered by an approachable warmth that she has not yet learned to weaponise the way her mother has. The court watches her the way the court watches Paun. They have, in this, more in common than either has acknowledged.

What They Are to Each Other

They have known each other since they were children. The first recorded interaction is Midsummer 462 — Paun fifteen, Branwen thirteen — when she requested him to dance and he was so flummoxed he stepped on her toes repeatedly and apologised, and she teased him about it for the rest of the evening. He earned +57 Glory for that feast, partly for his harping and singing. The toe-stepping was not among the logged achievements, but it is among the things he remembers.

Three years later, in 465, his first public performance of the Lay of Squire's Hill was logged with a special note: in the audience was Branwen. The Glory was +10 for the performance — modest by Glory standards, significant by the standard of how Glory entries are usually annotated. The campaign has been paying attention.

The Dynamic

For her, an escape from constant evaluation — her daily reality of every gesture scrutinised by her mother, her brother, the court ladies, her potential suitors. Paun is the one young man at court who treats her as a person rather than a prize, partly because his Modest 13 will not permit him to do otherwise and partly because he genuinely sees her.

For him, the one place his looks become a useful sign rather than a confusing inheritance — she sees him, registers him, responds to him. Adoration 9 is not yet a triggering passion in the field, but it is a passion the campaign can invoke. It will, sooner or later, be invoked at a cost.

The Obstacles

  • Her family. Lord Cadoc wants alliances; Lady Nest wants prestige; both want Branwen married to a knight with land, lineage, and politics on the winning side. Paun is an unknighted rebel squire with a small encumbered holding he has not yet inherited. The match is improbable on every axis except hers and his.
  • His station. He cannot make a formal courtship until he is knighted. He cannot be knighted until his lord knights him. His lord is not yet established (Homage 0 on the sheet). The path is years long.
  • Her brother Hywel. Already notices, already wary, already protective. The first conversation between Paun and Hywel about Branwen is on the calendar; the temperature of it is not yet known.
  • The war. Saxon raids reach as far as Sarum. Paun goes on dangerous missions monthly. The Lay of Squire's Hill exists because the battle existed. He may not survive long enough to be knighted, and Branwen is fifteen, and time is the resource neither of them has any control over.
What Adoration 9 means at this stage

Below the typical trigger threshold for an automatic Inspiration roll, but high enough to be invoked deliberately in scenes where she is present or imperilled. If Branwen is in danger and Paun is on the field, the Wolves' Gamemaster has the ammunition to make him roll Adoration — and his Valorous 17 will cooperate cheerfully with whatever the roll demands. This is the single mechanical lever the campaign has on him that is likeliest to get him killed before knighthood. He does not know this. She certainly does not.

VIII. What He Carries Hidden

Paun is Honest 9 — balanced rather than dominant — and Modest 13 dominant. He does not lie outright, but there are matters he simply does not raise. A partial inventory of what the eschille and the court do not yet know, and may discover in play:

  • That Vengeful 13 dominates Forgiving 7 on his sheet. His public posture is the proper Christian young man; his private inheritance is rage. The first time a Saxon is at his mercy will reveal which is the deeper truth.
  • That Just 5 is his lowest virtue. He is the squire most likely to favour his own people in a dispute that calls for impartiality. His Honour 17 will hold him to oaths; his Just 5 will not hold him to fairness. The eschille has not yet had a situation that tested this.
  • The family fae curse. Hunting at −5. He has not mentioned it. The Wolves have noticed he does not hunt well and have attributed it to the courtly skill cluster taking most of his training. The truth, if it surfaces, will matter — British Christian Logres does not love being reminded that the fae are real and act.
  • What Sir Eamon is actually teaching him. Not swordwork; that is for show. The real curriculum is how to be looked at without making yourself a target — the Fox's specialty, applied to a younger and more beautiful version of the same problem. Paun is taking notes. The eschille has not yet realised he is.
  • The Lay of Squire's Hill verse he has not performed. The published version of the song is honourable to all parties. The third verse, which he composed but has not sung publicly, is about a specific Saxon he killed at the battle and the face the man made. He sings that verse only to himself, at dawn, in the practice yard.
  • That he has written a song for Branwen and has not yet sung it where she can hear. One of the lines is on this dossier. The rest he keeps. He is waiting for the right hall, the right night, the right plausible occasion. He may never have all three at once. He may sing it anyway when his Adoration 9 gets the better of his Prudent 13.
  • That he is not sure his father did right. Sir Geraint kept his head down through the bad years — through 447–456, through Constantine's murder, through Constans's murder, through Rowena's marriage to Vortigern, through the dispossession of the Cymri. He only joined the rebels in 456 when most of the moves were already lost. Paun has not said, even to Owain, that he is not certain the silence was right. Modest 13 will not let him judge his father out loud. The judgement is there.
  • The dragon-dream, if it is happening to him. The eschille's late-winter session indicates several of the squires are now sharing some version of the Red Dragon dream. Paun has not yet said whether he is among them. His Lay of Squire's Hill, composed earlier in 465, already contains the line the dragon of red shall fly — which either means the dream is older for him than for the others, or means he heard the image somewhere and felt it before he understood why. Either possibility matters.

IX. Life Timeline

447 A.D.
Born in the South Counties, son of Sir Geraint ap Llwyd
The same year Vortigern becomes High King; the same year Saxons are first given property in Britain; the same year the prelate from Rome heals the leper. His name Llwyd — "grey" or "holy" — is his grandfather's, in the Cymric custom.
450 A.D.
The fae curse on the family Hunting
Sir Geraint, hunting in the woodlands, offends the fae. The family Hunting takes a permanent −5. Paun is three. He will inherit the curse without ever being told what specifically his father did.
457 A.D.
Battle of Kent — Sir Geraint dies
Paun's father fights on the rebel side against Vortigern. He had joined the dissidents only the year before, after years of keeping his head down. He dies in the battle. Paun is nine. The family becomes formally Rebels; the political position is set for a generation.
458 A.D.
The uncle takes the stewardship; the Brittany cousins leave
Paun's paternal uncle takes over the lands. A portion of the extended family packs and departs for Brittany. Paun, ten, stays with his mother and siblings. The household contracts.
Circa 459 onward
Lady Heledd places Paun on the courtly track
His mother, recognising his gifts, arranges his pageship and eventual squireship through court favours and her better-family connections. The Courtesy, the Singing, the Folklore, the Orate — the cluster at 11 — are her project, laid down in his middle childhood. The Religion and the Devotion are hers too.
Midsummer 462 A.D.
First recorded interaction with Lady Branwen
Paun, fifteen, plays harp and sings at a midsummer feast. He betrays his ignorance of falconry to a knight and is treated kindly for it. Lady Branwen, thirteen, asks him to dance; he is so flummoxed he steps on her toes repeatedly and apologises; she teases him about it. +57 Glory for the feast. The toe-stepping is logged in family memory but not on the Glory roll.
Late 463 A.D.
Night of the Long Knives — the uncle slain at the Giant's Dance
Hengest's Saxons murder the great lords of Logres at Stonehenge. Paun's noble uncle is among the dead, alongside Sir Gorthyn's father. Paun is sixteen. The Hate (Saxons) passion is rolled at 15. The family's collective grievance against Vortigern's Saxons is now personal as well as political.
463 A.D.
Inherited Glory: 570
Paun, sixteen, inherits the formal Glory of his line at the cusp of his squireship. The bulk is his father's. The political position comes with it: rebel family, marked.
Autumn 464 A.D.
Squired to the Wolves of Vagon at Sarum
The squires of Sarum — Paun, Caradoc, Cináeda, Luthwellyn, Drust, Gorthyn — are placed under the eschille of the Wolves for training and field service. The Wolves are undefeated against the Saxons in skirmishes and have gathered all squires at Vagon for winter. Earl Robert is presumed dead; his heir Roderick is missing. Paun and Gorthyn talk about their fathers in November.
Winter Phase 464 A.D.
First winter at Vagon — passive Glory +80
The Wolves shelter the squires for the lean winter. Sir Elaid organises hunting in the King's Woods; salted meat is distributed to Bramblethorn and the other villages. The minor feast at Session 3: Paun harps and sings to admiration; discusses falconry with Sir Hiawell; is overwhelmed by Gorthyn in sparring; is knocked down by the quintain counterweight.
3 Jan 465 A.D.
The autumn hunt — the boar — Cináeda gored
A hunt for deer turns into an encounter with a boar that gores Cináeda gravely. Paun defeats Caradoc in the morning sparring; Sir Hywel ap Knook shares falconry insights with him at the forest camp; Sir Aemon advises him to keep counsel on his aspirations. The boar is killed by Sir Rhain after Luthwellyn wounds it. Cináeda lives.
10 Jan 465 A.D.
Pilgrimage to Glastonbury — Paun chosen by Sir Eamon
The Wolves walk Cináeda to Glastonbury as pilgrims, armed but on foot. During the long moral instruction the Wolves choose their squires. Sir Eamon teaches Paun "a lesson in vigilance during training" and claims him — promising mental and sword training both. Cináeda is healed miraculously by the abbey's waters. Paun returns to Sarum as Sir Eamon's man.
8 Feb 465 A.D.
Bramblethorn — the bandit fight — Merlin rescued
Escorting food to Bramblethorn in a snowstorm, the squires confront cloaked figures pursuing a young man named Merlin under claim of "King's Justice." Paun charges with his great mace and strikes a bandit; chases a fleeing one into the woods. The youth is taken in. +50 Glory for the rescue. Merlin's hints about important matters go unexamined.
21 Feb 465 A.D.
The Saxon engagement at the village — major wound
Returning to the village with prisoners, the squires hold while Saxon forces advance. Merlin reveals his prophecy: the Red Dragon Banner must be retrieved. Luthwellyn strikes down a Saxon; the Wolves' knights charge the flank and break the line. Paun takes a major wound — the first time his Valorous 17 cashes for serious tissue cost. He lives. The quest for the banner is agreed.
Through 465 A.D.
Squire's Hill, the Barghests, the Easter Feast, Bambury, the giants, Sir Roderick
A run of named achievements through the campaign year: Squire's Hill (+80), the Lay of Squire's Hill performed with Branwen present (+10), the Barghests killed (+100), the Easter Feast at Cameliard (+80), the charge at Bambury (+35), the giants and barghest (+200), and the rescue of Sir Roderick (+100). His Glory rises from 570 to 1,362. The unproven squire is no longer unproven.

XI. Possible Trajectories

Paun is further along in play than the eschille's youngest squires. Eighteen months of squireship, two named battles, one major wound, a Glory total approaching the threshold for early knighthood, an established passion for Branwen, an established mentor in Sir Eamon, and an unresolved political inheritance from his father. The campaign has more material to work with than it does for any other Sarum squire. What follows is not prediction but a sketch of the lanes his sheet and his situation make available.

The Quiet Threads

  • The voice becoming a tool. Singing 11 and Compose 5 are already producing Glory; the gap between them is the gap between performer and composer, and closing it is the most reliable arc on his sheet. By his second or third year of squireship he will have a body of compositions that travel ahead of him — the first proper bardic reputation in the eschille.
  • Owain and the holding. His brother is the heir-presumptive; their uncle the steward. If the Wolves clear the marches and the rebel taint settles, the holding becomes a question. Owain expects to inherit; Paun, as second son, expects nothing. The campaign may surface this. If Owain dies, the question changes shape entirely.
  • The Sir Eamon curriculum. The Fox's quiet instruction in how to be desired without lying about it is the most consequential ongoing apprenticeship in the eschille. By the time it finishes, Paun will be a different kind of man than his Modest 13 currently makes him.

The Loud Threads

  • Knighthood within two campaign years. His Glory trajectory makes early knighthood plausible. The unset bonds — Homage, Fealty, Loyalty — will be set in that ceremony, and the lord to whom he kneels will define the rest of his career. Sir Eamon does not have lands to give. The Earl, when Salisbury has one again, will. Which Earl — the rescued Roderick, an Aurelian appointee, a Cymric restoration — is the open question.
  • Branwen, formally. Knighthood opens formal courtship. Adoration 9 is already on the sheet; a successful courtship turns it into something the campaign can press into Love (Person), Honour, and Hospitality territory simultaneously. Her family is the obstacle. Her brother Hywel is the immediate one. Lord Cadoc is the structural one. Lady Nest is the implacable one.
  • The Red Dragon prophecy. Merlin's quest for the banner is on the table; the dragon-dream is moving through the eschille; the Lay of Squire's Hill already contains the line. Paun is positioned to be one of the carriers of the dragon thread, perhaps as a singer of it rather than a wielder of it. The role of the bard who names what is happening is open.
  • The Aurelian alignment. A rebel family's son, of the right age, with a strong voice, an aspirational sun-on-blue-and-green heraldry, and a developing reputation, is a useful figure for any rising claimant. The campaign may make him a small piece of the Aurelian story without him noticing for a long time.

The Tested Edges

  • A Saxon at his mercy. Vengeful 13 against Forgiving 7, Hate (Saxons) 8 still loaded. Modest 13 and Spiritual 12 on the other side. The first time he has the choice, the campaign will learn which Paun is the real one. His major wound at the late-winter engagement was the body teaching him something; the next Saxon encounter will test whether the lesson stuck.
  • Branwen in danger. Adoration 9 invoked in combat — Valorous 17 will not refuse it — is the likeliest mechanical route to a serious wound or death before knighthood. His Prudent 13 will help on the way in; nothing will help once Adoration is rolled.
  • Just 5 / Arbitrary 15 meets a real case. Sooner or later the eschille will face a moral question where the Cymric way and the right answer are not the same. Honour 17 will pull him one direction; Arbitrary 15 will pull him the other. His Honour is higher, but Honour 17 is not Tiphaine's 19 — it can be overridden. This is where his arc gets interesting.
  • The third verse of the Lay. The version of the song he sings only at dawn. The first time he sings it where anyone can hear is going to mean something he is not yet ready to mean.

What He Will Not Be

He will not be a pure courtly knight. The Valorous 17 is too high; the trait sheet is too thick with the soldier's vices (Vengeful, Arbitrary, Indulgent). He is going to be a fighting knight who sings beautifully, not a singing knight who occasionally fights.

He will not be a saint. His religious frame is real but is not Tiphaine's Modest 15 / Forgiving 13. He is Christian in the way of his mother and his country; he will not be martyred for clarity.

He will not be his father. Sir Geraint kept his head down for nine years and then died for a cause that was mostly lost when he joined it. Paun is younger, fiercer, more visible, and on the rebel side from the start. Whatever he dies for, it will not be silence.

He will not be untouched by what is coming. He is seventeen and Valorous 17 and in love and a rebel's son and the Peacock and the boy with the dulcet voice. The campaign is going to test all of those things. What is left at the end is what he becomes.
