I. The Man, As Recorded

Fenrir was an elderly cleric of the One True God, hooded and grey-bearded, who took up the dangerous pilgrimage to the Tomb of Sir Chide upon the Hill of the High Woald. He carried a torch and a carved staff, and he wore the dark robes of his calling. Of his earlier travels — whence he came, what parishes he had served, what saints he had walked between — the parchment is silent. He arrived in the woods already old.

What can be said with certainty is set down in the sections that follow. The rest is rumour, marginalia, and the smoke of an extinguished candle.

II. Confirmed Capabilities

Only those abilities he was witnessed to use are recorded here. The remainder of his clerical art — his prepared orisons, his higher rites, the full breadth of his learning — goes with him into the ground.

Witnessed In Use

  • Turn Undead. Invoked upon the slime-slick stag-skeleton at the stone circle. A green haze rose where it had stood.
  • Mace, wielded. Brought down with sufficient force to shatter the wooden cherub of the four blasphemous relics.
  • Light the Candle of Saint Sedge. A rite performed at the chapel altar above. A wind rushed about him; a distant neigh and a victorious battle-cry were heard, as though from a long-ago field. He laid wooden fruit upon the altar in thanks.

Of His Cloth

As a cleric in the proper tradition, Fenrir is assumed to have known the lesser orisons of his order — the laying-on of hands, the warding of evil, the speaking-of-blessings — though no spellcasting was observed in the record kept of his final day. Whether he had readied a prayer that morning and never had cause to speak it, or whether he had spent his grace before the descent, cannot now be known.

III. Goods & Carried Things

Recovered from his Person

Mace. Iron-headed, of the cleric's permitted arms. Used to shatter a cherub.
Torch. Surrendered to Lexel la Marque that she might attempt the burning of the cherub. Returned, presumably.
Wooden fruit. A pilgrim's offering. Painted. Left upon the altar of Saint Sedge.
Holy symbol of the One True God. Worn at the breast. The shape of it does not survive in the record.

Implied by Calling

Armour proper to the Cleric — the cut and weight not given.
A satchel; a prayer-book; oil for lamps; a flint & steel; the small habits of the road.
Coin enough for the road, but no record of his purse.
Whatever share of the five thousand gold he was promised, he did not live to spend.

IV. The Company

He went into the Wood with five others, gathered for the wizard's commission to recover a ring from the hand of a skeleton long buried.

  • Lexel la Marque — Elf, woman. Read the runes at the stone circle. Carried the ring to faerie at the end. (Jordan)
  • Legwen — Fighter, man. Cleaved the scolding spine of the holy book. Cut the silver crucifix in half. (Beef)
  • Sybil — Magic-user, non-binary. Slain by the stone hounds. (Beef)
  • Breagle — Dwarf, man. Banged on a metal owl to learn it was hollow. Slain by the stone hounds. (Frank)
  • Jilly — Thief, woman. Asked the Lady, at the end, that the fallen be returned. (Frank)

V. Chronicle of the Pilgrimage

The Commission

A wizard's coin — five thousand gold — for a ring upon the hand of a skeleton, buried in a mound within the Wood. The site had been a place of pilgrimage in older years. The church had since closed it off. The way was no longer safe.

It was said the Cold Prince once ruled Dolmenwood and kept it under deep cold; that mortals fought him nine hundred years past; that Sir Chide the fabled hero fell in love with the Lady of the Wood; that they exchanged rings of engagement; that Sir Chide was slain upon the Hill of the High Woald, asking only to be interred; and that the Lady was never seen again. Every year the magic weakens. Every year the winter creeps closer back.

The Stone Circle

The path led into a ring of mossy, rune-graven stones, etched in a magical script of summoning. Within their compass a stag's skeleton twitched in a coat of bright green slime. Fenrir spoke the rite of Turn Undead, and a green haze answered him.

Rusted owl-figures stood about the clearing, their wings spread, their eyes graven with pentagrams; Breagle struck one and found it hollow. A warty toad crept out of the trees and spoke one word: Betrayal.

The slab before the mound was levered loose with rope and pole. Stairs descended.

The Four Relics

In the first chamber stood four mouldering plinths, each bearing an object furred with mustard-coloured rot: a silver crucifix, a wooden cherub, a great candle, and an open holy book. When the company drew near, the relics rose, and a shrill voice scolded the party for their misdeeds.

Legwen cleaved the spine of the book in two. Fenrir seized the crucifix — which scolded him in turn, for not serving the one true god — and Legwen halved it. Lexel took the torch from Legwen and went to burn the cherub, but the torch would not catch it; Fenrir then shattered the cherub with his mace. Beneath the dust, a mosaic showed in colours.

The Dancing Dead

Behind the double doors two skeletons waltzed in the air above a fissure in the floor — drenched in a slime-vapour that fell in transparent sheets from a hairline crack in the ceiling. Five stone biers stood about them; a brass plaque named Lady Amaranda and another Lord Brigfolwith.

The dancing dead came forward to dance with Fenrir. One reached out a hand, and the slime-coating rushed over him in a thin film. Legwen prised open the eastmost coffin, and two other skeletons — one human, one feline — rushed forth crying that the company defiled their dead.

Return to the Chapel

Doubling back, the company climbed again to the small chapel above — the shrine of Saint Sedge, the Holy Crusader, who stood with a thick red candle held aloft. Fenrir lit the candle. A wind rushed about him; a distant horse's neigh and a victorious battle-cry sounded from across the years. He left wooden fruit upon the altar in offering. This was his rite.

The Stone Hounds

In the pillared chamber stood statues of war and, in a recess, two stone-hounds chained to the door. When the company touched the door the hounds woke. One bit Breagle and killed him. The party drew back; the hounds lunged. They opened the locked door themselves, and the company ran past them — Lexel through, then Jilly. Fenrir swung and missed. Sybil fell.

The Ghost and the Portrait

Beyond was a huge circular chamber, where a pale azure ghost — visor raised, forlorn — knelt before a grimed portrait of a fair maiden amid a stone circle. A stone coffer stood closed. The ghost was Sir Chide, who had been waiting these nine hundred years. He begged that his ring be taken to the lower level, that he might rejoin his beloved in Faerie.

Lexel slipped the ring from the ghost's hand. The party were shaken and hollowed out by the long descent. The mood was very much to be done with the job.

The Wintery Glade

Stairs led down to a pool, a marble statue of a maiden, dozens of ghostly candles floating in the air, and a vaulted ceiling that proved an illusion. Beyond the threshold lay two feet of snow, a frozen lake, a tower with smoke rising from it, and a purple crack in the sky from which sticky purple matter dripped to stain the snow. Mossy corpses hung in the trees.

Here Fenrir hung back. Of all the company it was he who would not cross fully into faerie. Whether out of doctrinal conscience — for the One True God has little patience with the realm beneath the hill — or simple weariness, the record does not say.

The Tower & the Ring

Lexel pressed on, parlayed her way past a hulking palanquin-bearer and its scrawny rider by accepting a small yellow mushroom that made her shine, and ascended the tower to the Lady of the Wood. The ring was given. The Lady gave the tale of herself and her late knight in full. At Jilly's request she gave a further gift: the resurrection of the fallen.

But the gift could not extend to all. The line between Faerie and the world of men is fading; in a few days it will be gone. The Lady and Sir Chide remain trapped in the other place, and shall remain so. Perhaps, in some campaign yet to come, the favour of the Winter King might unwork what cannot now be undone.
