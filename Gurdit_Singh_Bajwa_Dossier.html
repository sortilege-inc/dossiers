Service Record ਫ਼ੌਜੀ ਸੇਵਾ · 15th Ludhiana Sikhs

c. 1908 Enlistment at approximately age eighteen with the 15th Ludhiana Sikhs — a regiment raised 1846, with historical ties to China (Boxer Rebellion, 1900).
1914 — 1915 France & Flanders. Lahore Division, Indian Corps. First Battle of Ypres (Oct–Nov 1914); Neuve Chapelle (Mar 1915 — bayonet scar on left forearm; scar across left eyebrow); Second Battle of Ypres (Apr–May 1915 — the first major use of poison gas); Festubert; Loos.
1916 — 1918 Mesopotamia. Heat, disease, grinding combat against the Ottomans. Siege of Kut (a catastrophe); recapture of Kut; advance up the Tigris; capture of Baghdad (Mar 1917); push toward Mosul.
Nov 1918 — Dec 1918 The letter. Outside Mosul, weeks late, written in his elder brother's careful Gurmukhi: Harbans Kaur died first, then Jaswant three days later, then the baby girl Sukhjit four days after that. All three in the space of a week, in the influenza's second wave. He read it twice. Folded it. Saddled his horse and rode out alone for three hours. The night that followed he and his havildar sat together and did not speak at all.
Apr 1919 Jallianwala Bagh. Brigadier-General Dyer ordered British Indian troops to fire on an unarmed Baisakhi crowd in Amritsar. Between 379 and 1,000+ killed, most of them Sikhs. The massacre happened in his home district. This is the wound that does not heal.
May — Aug 1919 Third Anglo-Afghan War. Mobilized for the North-West Frontier; fought at Bagh in May, defending the Khyber Pass for the same Empire that had just massacred his people at Amritsar. He did his duty. He was good at it. His Sikh company mates shared his cold rage and did not speak of it to British officers.
1919 — 1921 Waziristan campaigns. Mahsud and Wazir tribes. Mountain warfare, night ambushes. Where he gained his Pashto and his knowledge of Pashtunwali.
1921 Retirement. Fourteen years served. Declines a commission extension and a position with the Indian Political Department. He tells his commanding officer quietly that he wants to see something other than war.
1921 — 1922 Shanghai Municipal Police, Sikh branch, International Settlement. He is good at it. He commands respect. He watches Chinese citizens beaten by Sikh policemen under British officers' command. He could not stomach being an instrument of it.
1922 — present Director of Expedition Security, Peking University. Recruited through a former 15th Ludhiana officer in Peking. Prior expeditions: Shansi (spring 1923, four months); Shantung (summer 1923, three weeks, near Qufu). Wang has been his client across both.

Faith & Practice ਧਰਮ · The structure that has held

He is a devout Amritdhari Sikh. Each morning before dawn he completes his nitnem — the five daily prayers: ਜਪੁਜੀ ਸਾਹਿਬ Japji Sahib, ਜਾਪੁ ਸਾਹਿਬ Jaap Sahib, Tav-Prasad Savaiye, ਚੌਪਈ ਸਾਹਿਬ Chaupai Sahib, ਅਨੰਦ ਸਾਹਿਬ Anand Sahib — said quietly in Punjabi into the still air of the room, the Gutka closed and set back on the table with both hands. At sundown, Rehras Sahib; before sleep, Kirtan Sohila.

He observes the Five Ks: kesh (uncut hair, kept beneath the turban), kangha (the wooden comb), kara (the steel bracelet), kachera (cotton undergarment), kirpan (always worn — religious article, not weapon of convenience). He keeps Sikh dietary observance — no halal meat (Sikh prohibition; halal slaughter is specifically Islamic ritual), prefers vegetarian when traveling, no beef or pork, no alcohol, no tobacco. This is why Marie's quiet question at their second meeting — whether he would prefer to take his meals separately during the expedition — registered as something other than the usual missionary obtuseness.

The deepening after 1918. For many men, the loss of wife and children to the influenza shatters faith. For him, it did the opposite. He has read the Guru Granth Sahib every morning since 1919 because it is the only structure that has held. He has found in scripture a specific vocabulary for what happened: Hukam, the divine will; Maya, the illusion of worldly attachment; the fundamental teaching that grief is real and also not ultimate. He is not a mystic. He is a man who has taken the consolations his tradition offers and shaped his days around them. Without this scaffolding he would not be functional.

In desperate moments he will be heard reciting Chaupai Sahib — the prayer of protection composed by Guru Gobind Singh himself, traditionally recited in battle. Any Punjabi-speaker in earshot would recognize it. He falls back on Chardi Kala — rising spirit, the Sikh virtue of maintaining high spirits in the face of adversity. He invokes Guru Gobind Singh's martial spirit in combat and Guru Nanak's contemplative peace in reflection. His religion is not a costume.

Lineage & Formation ਜੀਵਨ · The road here

Born around 1890 in Chak Sikandar, a village in Baba Bakala tehsil, Amritsar District — the Majha heartland of Sikhism, between the Ravi and Beas rivers. His family were Jat Sikh small landowners; his father owned perhaps ten to fifteen acres of canal-irrigated land. Not wealthy, not poor. Respectable peasant landowners with a long military tradition — grandfather possibly served with a Sikh regiment; father possibly served too. Military service was family vocation.

His name carries weight: Gurdit means given by the Guru — a child whose parents named him in gratitude, perhaps after a difficult pregnancy or the loss of an earlier infant. His life was understood from his birth as a gift received through the intercession of the Sikh Gurus. The wife and children he lost in 1918 were also given by the Guru, and given back. The Bajwa got — his clan — is a major Amritsar District Jat Sikh lineage, geographically close to Jallianwala Bagh. The land where his people would later be massacred is the land that named him.

His name shares one further weight he did not choose: in 1914, Baba Gurdit Singh chartered the Komagata Maru to challenge Canadian exclusion laws, bringing 376 Punjabi passengers to Vancouver harbor. Canada refused them entry; British authorities later fired on them at Budge Budge, killing twenty. Every Sikh in the diaspora knows the name. When he meets Marie — a Canadian — the subtext is in the room.

Village education: Punjabi literacy in Gurmukhi, some Urdu, the fundamentals of Sikh scripture, large portions of the Guru Granth Sahib memorized as a child. He visited the Harmandir Sahib — the Golden Temple — regularly. Enlistment at eighteen with the 15th Ludhiana Sikhs; France 1914–15, Mesopotamia 1916–18, the Third Anglo-Afghan War 1919, Waziristan 1919–21. Four theatres of combat. He has scars from Neuve Chapelle.

He took his pension in 1921. Declined a commission extension and a frontier political post. "I want to see something other than war," he told his commanding officer quietly. He served a year with the Shanghai Municipal Police Sikh branch on the International Settlement — and watched Chinese citizens beaten by Sikh policemen under British officers' command, parks with signs reading No Dogs or Chinese, opium profits sustaining the whole edifice. He recognized the same contempt he had seen turned on his own people at Amritsar. He could not stomach being an instrument of it. Peking University recruited him in early 1922 through a former 15th Ludhiana officer.

The bind. He cannot act on his hatred of the Empire without destroying the people he works to support — his parents on the ten acres in Chak Sikandar. He cannot confess it without risking his employment. So it sits. It does not go away. It goes deeper.

Prof. Wang. Wang specifically requests him on field expeditions. Gurdit has saved him from genuine danger — once from banditry in Shansi, once from a more complicated situation involving a warlord officer's grievance that Gurdit defused through careful negotiation. They take tea together once a week. Wang has asked him questions about Sikh theology, which Gurdit has answered carefully, honored to be asked. When Gurdit's birthday passed unremarked this year, Wang left a small package in his quarters: an edition of the Dhammapada in English, with a handwritten note: For reflection in quiet moments. Gurdit has read it twice. This is what earned loyalty looks like. He would kill for Wang. He would die for Wang. He would lie to foreigners for Wang.

The Party ਸਾਥੀ · Fellow travellers

Prof. Wang Enlai Department of Geology · Peking University · his client and his deepest living tie Their relationship is built across shared expedition ordeals — Shansi banditry, the Shantung warlord's grievance. Wang greets him with both hands and something warmer than the others get: "Gurdit Singh-ji. I am glad you are here. I am sorry the circumstances were not better." Gurdit's reply: "The circumstances are what they are, Professor. I have questions."
Marie Halvor Boucher Pao Su-t'ien · 包素恬 · medical officer · the relationship to watch He is used to Western women, missionaries especially, being kindly patronizing. He expects from her the usual mixture of goodwill and obtuseness. What he gets is different. She looks at him without flinching. She does not make the assumptions. She asks him, at their second meeting, whether he would prefer to take his meals separately during the expedition — a quietly informed question that shows she has thought about halal. He tells her he appreciates the question. They nod at each other. They recognize each other's silences.
Ch'ien Po-hsiang Chih-yüan · paleobotanist · Geological Survey of China Professional respect immediate. A Chinese scientist who speaks to him as an equal without condescension, who asks intelligent questions about Sikh religious practice without turning them into a colonial-curiosity interview, who treats Hélène as his full intellectual partner — Gurdit reads all of this quickly. Here is a man I can work with. Chih-yüan's political moderation may frustrate him, but he will not push. Not his place.
Hélène Éloïse Bergeron 貝樂然 · sociolinguist · married to Chih-yüan Complicated. He is wary of the colonial position even in its sympathetic forms. She will ask him interesting questions about Sikh theology; he will answer with measured politeness; over time, if she proves to take him seriously as a person rather than a case study, he may warm. He is more educated than she initially assumes — village literacy in Gurmukhi plus regimental English schooling plus fourteen years of reading. His thoughts on how the Guru Granth Sahib's poetry survives or fails in English translation might genuinely interest her.
Langdon Warner American · Fogg Museum, Harvard · Wagons-Lits Hotel He has no particular loyalty to Western archaeologists. The particular stillness of a man who has spent a career watching the machinery of empire process other people's patrimony, and has learned that his opinion of this was not part of the arrangement. He is still when Chih-yüan recites Pelliot's catalogue of losses.
His Parents Chak Sikandar · Punjab · the ten acres, the three graves at the village edge Living, still. His father showed him the three graves when he returned in mid-1919, seven months after the funerals — the graves were already sunken, already a year into their weathering by the time he stood at them. His mother held him without speaking. His brother — older, with his own family — manages the land. A cousin is an Akali volunteer.
Harbans Kaur · Jaswant · Sukhjit Wife · son (age 2) · daughter (infant) · 1918 influenza Wrapped in oilcloth in the locked trunk: a photograph taken in Amritsar on one of his leaves in 1916. Harbans Kaur in a pale sari, Jaswant standing stiffly beside her, her hand on his shoulder. He was not in the photograph himself; he had paid for the photographer and stood behind him. Field Journal ਰੋਜ਼ਨਾਮਚਾ · Notes from the road

26 August 1923 — approx. Peking University · the appointment
Wang contacts him ten days before departure. The original man — Mr. Fenton, a British ex-pat — has fallen ill (dysentery, six weeks at minimum). Fenton's notes have not been transferred. The handoff is abrupt. Gurdit has eight days with the briefing materials.
3 September 1923 — Before dawn University staff residences · his quarters
Awake since before dawn. The nitnem completed in the dark — five prayers said quietly in Punjabi into the still air, the Gutka closed and set back on the table with both hands. By the time the light came up, he was already at the desk. He writes his preliminary security assessment on plain university paper: English on the left, Punjabi annotations on the right. Five open questions for the meeting. He has read the briefing three times. He has underlined nothing. He never underlines. He reads until he remembers.
3 September 1923 — Mid-morning The map · his desk
Tracing the Gansu corridor route with his finger for the third morning running. Something about the distance between Anxi and the Mogao caves is not sitting right. Navigate roll · 60 · Extreme success. The briefing map's final leg has been compressed in the reduction. Real distance is a day and a half longer than shown — with laden camels, in autumn, that is not a rounding error. He underlines the note once. Beneath, in Punjabi: "Fenton should have seen this." What follows from that thought he does not write down.
3 September 1923 — 3:00 P.M. Wang Enlai's Meeting Room · Geology Building
The four investigators meet for the first time. Wang greets him with both hands and warmth. "The circumstances are what they are, Professor. I have questions." He reads from his prepared paper in careful Mandarin, moves into English without embarrassment when his vocabulary runs out, and Wang translates the English back into Mandarin for the room. Personnel. Route. Equipment. Firearms (raised carefully — there are sensitivities with the Ministry). And: "the briefing map has a scaling error. The final leg has been compressed by approximately one and a half days with laden camels." Wang stares at him, then at the chalk map. He writes for longer than the previous notes.
3 September 1923 — Later afternoon The Range · behind the groundskeeping buildings
Picks up the heaviest medical crate without asking and carries it without apparent effort. At the range he presents the Winchester Model 1906 .22 horizontally on his forearms — the way one presents a weapon to someone who knows what a weapon is. He watches Marie check the chamber before doing anything else; the motion automatic, before thought. He files the assessment without expression. She shifts to the 50-metre frame on the seventh shot, on uneven ground, and lands within an inch of center. He has already begun thinking about which rifle to acquire for her through his Legation contact.
5 September 1923 — Dawn The Range · Chih-yüan & Hélène's assessment
He takes the married couple to the range in the early morning. Chih-yüan's first shot into the berm, second wide, third clipped the frame's outer edge. He writes: "Ch'ien — non-combatant. Keep behind cover at all times." Hélène asks to be shown the grip once, replicates it imperfectly but not dangerously. Third shot inside the frame, low and left but inside. He writes: "Bergeron — can fire rifle if necessary. One target, close range, stationary. Do not rely on this." He closes the notebook. Marie's Lee-Enfield .303 is in the equipment cabinet, cleaned and zeroed. He will give it to her formally before departure. There is a right moment for this. The range on the fifth is not it.
7 September 1923 — Morning Hôtel des Wagons-Lits · Legation Quarter
Meeting with Warner. Still. The particular stillness of a man who has spent a career watching the machinery of empire process other people's patrimony and has learned that his opinion of this was not part of the arrangement. When Chih-yüan recites Pelliot's catalogue of losses — forty thousand manuscripts, two hundred and twenty pounds in silver, the signed receipt in Wang Yuanlu's hand — he does not editorialize. The facts editorialize themselves.
October 1923 Mogao Caves · Dunhuang
They arrive at the cliff face. Wang Yuanlu meets them in Taoist robes — perhaps seventy-five, unhurried. The old priest looks at Warner, away, with the quality of a man who has already taken that measurement and filed it. They are shown to a painted ground-level cave; the donor figures' eyes follow them through the space.
The cold before the light The base of the cliff · before dawn
Marie is already outside when the sky begins to pale, breath misting, the Lee-Enfield broken down on its cloth on a flat rock. He emerges a few minutes later with two cups of tea and sets one beside her without comment. The ravens watch from the trees.
