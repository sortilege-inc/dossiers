I. Identity & Role

Protocol Keshet is Zenith's architect. They do not make the unit's legal rulings — that is Vellpryte. They do not read the room — that is Nomos. They do not provide the force projection — that is Azimuth. Keshet builds the plan that makes the other three effective. They survey the operational environment, designate priorities, coordinate positioning, and ensure that when Vellpryte locks down a target and Azimuth executes on the perimeter, both of them are exactly where they need to be.

They are the most traditionally Axiomatic member of Zenith — the one who has most fully retained their species' epistemic priorities. They did not acculturate to the lower planes the way the others did. They approach emotion with the same methodology they apply to tactical problems: identify the variable, contain it, plan around it. Every contingency accounted for is one less variable that can make them feel something unexpected.

Their biography carries the most explicit wound in the unit. A group of heroes came to the territory Keshet was sworn to protect. They fought alongside them. The heroes fell. The wilderness was altered beyond recovery. Keshet left to ensure it would not happen again — and has been writing plans designed to prevent it ever since. They do not describe themselves as haunted. They describe themselves as thorough.

The contingency existed. I failed to execute it in time. That will not occur again. // Keshet, debrief following Case #ZEN-002 — partial formation failure

Cultural Background

Culture: Memonek — Nomadic, Communal, Academic. The most traditionally Axiomatic culture in Zenith. Nomadic means they understand movement and adaptation; communal means they think in terms of collective function; academic means they study before they act. This is the culture that produced a tactician who learned their craft in the field rather than a classroom, and who reads every battle as a problem that has a correct solution if you can think fast enough to find it.

Career: Warden. They protected a wild region from poachers and those who sought its destruction. They guided the lost, led rescue parties, and learned to read terrain the way other people read text. The wilderness they protected is gone now. They do not return to it. They redirect that skill set toward the missions that come through the Office, treating each case's operational environment as a new territory to read and protect.

Inciting Incident: Honor the Fallen. Heroes came to their land with trouble close behind. Keshet fought alongside them. It was not enough. The heroes died and the wilderness was changed forever. The decision to join the Office was a decision to be somewhere the loss could not recur — or, if it recurred, to ensure they were better prepared for it than they were that day.

III. Focus — The Heroic Resource

Keshet's Heroic Resource is Focus — the Tactician's measure of battlefield awareness, the currency of coordinated action. Unlike Drama (crisis-powered) or Clarity (metered and spillable), Focus is the most orderly resource in Zenith: a fixed 2 per turn with two conditional triggers, both of which reward executing the plan correctly. It does not spike. It does not overflow. It accumulates through competence.

  • 2 — at the start of your turn (baseline, every turn)
  • +1 — the first time each round that you or an ally deals damage to a creature you have marked
  • +1 — the first time in a round that an ally within 10 squares uses a heroic ability
◈ The Mark Loop

Mark is the engine at the center of Keshet's resource economy. They designate a target; any damage to that target gives them Focus; they spend Focus on abilities that amplify the team's damage to that target. The loop is self-reinforcing. A well-run engagement means Keshet has exactly the Focus they need at exactly the moment Vellpryte locks the target down and Azimuth executes the takedown. The plan, when it works, is a machine.

Where Azimuth chose them for operational efficiency, Keshet chose them because a tactician needs to be everywhere the plan requires at all times, and the dice cannot be allowed to produce unplanned outcomes at critical moments.

VI. Key Abilities

Core Mechanic — Constant Presence

Mark
Maneuver · Ranged 10 · One creature · No Focus cost
Keshet designates a target. That creature is marked until the encounter ends, until Keshet is dying, or until Keshet marks another. When Keshet or any ally deals rolled damage to the marked target and Keshet spends 1 Focus: the damage gains +2×Reason (4 extra), or the attacker can spend a Recovery, or the attacker shifts up to Reason squares. This is the engine of everything. Every encounter, there is a marked target. Everyone knows who it is. Everything converges on them.

Signature — Extraaction

"Strike Now!"
Main Action · Ranged 10 · One ally · No Focus cost (or 5 Focus for two allies)
Your foe left an opening. You point this out to an ally. The target uses a signature ability as a free triggered action — a full extra action granted to a teammate, cost zero. At 5 Focus: two allies instead of one. This is what makes Keshet a force multiplier rather than simply a combatant. On any turn Keshet calls this, the unit gets an extra action. Over a full encounter, this is decisive.

Subclass — Readiness

Overwatch
Triggered Action · Ranged 10 · Trigger: the target moves · No Focus cost (or 1 to slow)
Under Keshet's direction, an ally waits for exactly the right moment to strike. When a designated creature moves, one ally makes a free strike against them at any point during that movement. Spend 1 Focus: if the target has average Reason or lower, they are also slowed. Overwatch converts the enemy's movement into the unit's action economy. Movement is threat. Keshet turns it into opportunity.

Mobility Coordination

Squad! Forward!
Maneuver · Ranged 10 · Self and two allies · 3 Focus cost
On Keshet's command, three members of the unit move simultaneously up to their full speed. This is a repositioning tool — used when the formation needs to advance, when the perimeter needs to collapse on a target, when someone is out of position and the plan cannot execute until they aren't. Three people, coordinated movement, on a maneuver. This is Keshet's plan made physical.

Mark Trigger (Resource Engine)

Mark: Trigger (Advanced Tactics)
Free Triggered Action · Trigger: ally deals damage to marked target · 1 Focus cost
When any ability deals rolled damage to Keshet's marked target, Keshet spends 1 Focus for one of: +4 damage (2×Reason), the attacker spends a Recovery, or the attacker shifts up to 2 squares. The Mastermind subclass upgrades this: the target gains 2 surges for the triggering damage, and spending 1 additional Focus increases the potency by 1. The mark is not just targeting — it is a continuous buff engine for whoever is hitting the marked creature.

Melee Signature (Battlemind Kit)

Unmooring
Main Action · Psionic, Melee 1, Strike, Weapon · Kit: Battlemind
Keshet's weapon unleashes psionic energy that reduces the target's weight, making them significantly easier to force move. A melee strike that sets up the battlefield for force movement on subsequent turns — their own or Azimuth's. The Battlemind kit is psionic even though Keshet is not a psion; the weapon channels it. This is the self-taught approach: you use what works, regardless of whether it fits the theoretical framework.

VII. Mastermind — Subclass Features

The Mastermind subclass is characterized by encyclopedic knowledge of warfare — viewing the battlefield as a game board, seeking victory by thinking ahead of opponents. Two features define it at level 1.

Studied Commander

Keshet's encyclopedic knowledge of battle history has direct operational applications. While present, every hero treats war and battle lore projects as one category cheaper. If given 24 hours before a combat encounter or negotiation, and possessing at least one clue about it, Keshet can make a Reason test to pre-analyze the encounter and give the unit an advantage before it begins.

◈ Studied Commander in Play

The pre-encounter Reason test is one of the most significant downtime abilities in Zenith. Given preparation time, Keshet reduces the unknown. Given a clue, they build a model. Given a model, they find the contingency. This is not a separate tactical activity — it is Keshet's worldsickness coping strategy expressed as a game mechanic.

Overwatch

The second Mastermind feature — reactive fire coordination. When a designated creature moves, Keshet grants an ally a free strike at any point during that movement. This ability costs no Focus and activates on the enemy's action, not Keshet's. It converts the action economy equation: the enemy's turn is not safe. Every move is a potential strike.

◈ Overwatch + Keeper of Order

The combination is significant. Overwatch creates a free strike; Keeper of Order ensures the dice on that free strike don't produce an unplanned result. A bad roll on the Overwatch strike becomes normalized. The plan executes.

◈ Self-Taught Tension with Mastermind

Mastermind is an encyclopedic, knowledge-based subclass. Studied Commander specifically requires broad tactical education. The Self-Taught complication means Keshet's encyclopedic knowledge was not acquired through formal channels — it was built through observation, necessity, and a determination not to repeat the deaths they couldn't prevent. Their knowledge is real. The gaps in it are real. And because the knowledge was self-constructed, Keshet cannot always identify where the gaps are.

IX. Complication — Self-Taught

While their peers were learning their trades in schools, Keshet honed their capabilities in the field with nothing but instinct as a guide. What they lost in polish and theoretical grounding, they make up for in experiential power.

AspectMechanicSignificance
Self-Taught Benefit At the start of each turn during combat, Keshet can forgo gaining their Heroic Resource (Focus) until their next turn. If they do, all strikes gain a damage bonus equal to their highest characteristic score (+2) until then. A trade: delay the resource that powers the team for personal damage output. This is a solo decision that affects collective capability. Keshet choosing to forgo Focus in a turn when Vellpryte needs a Mark trigger is Keshet choosing individual power over coordination.
Self-Taught Drawback Tactical expertise is experiential and unorthodox — no formal doctrine, no institutional polish. Knowledge gaps exist that Keshet cannot always identify. The complication's mechanical benefit is straightforward; the narrative cost is structural. They know what they've seen. They have not necessarily seen everything. The Mastermind subclass creates tension here: encyclopedic knowledge built from field experience has survivorship bias.
◈ Self-Taught as Drama

The benefit is designed to tempt. Forgoing Focus for personal damage is individually optimal in the moment and collectively suboptimal when the team needs the resource economy. Every time Keshet takes this trade, they are choosing the self-taught response — raw force — over the coordination their class is designed for. It is a mechanic that creates the specific failure mode Keshet's arc is about.

X. Worldsickness — Their Method

Keshet's strategy is suppression through planning. Every contingency accounted for is one less variable that can make them feel something unexpected. They approach worldsickness as a tactical problem: identify the emotional trigger, develop a countermeasure, execute the countermeasure before the emotion can become operative.

This is the most methodologically coherent coping strategy in Zenith and possibly the least sustainable. You cannot plan for everything. The lower planes produce situations that resist anticipation. And the specific event that broke Keshet once before — the fallen heroes, the unrecoverable wilderness — was itself a failure of planning. Their current strategy is a response to a wound, not a solved problem.

XI. The Fallen Heroes

Keshet's inciting incident is the most legible wound in Zenith. Heroes came to the territory they were sworn to protect. Trouble followed them. Keshet fought alongside them. The heroes died. The wilderness was altered beyond recovery. Keshet left to ensure it would not happen again.

They do not describe this as grief. They describe it as a dataset. They have analyzed the engagement extensively: where the formation failed, what contingencies were not prepared, what additional preparation might have changed the outcome. The analysis is thorough and unending. The emotion underneath it is the part that never comes up in briefings.

XII. Position Within Zenith

Keshet is Zenith's operational backbone: the member whose planning makes the other three effective, whose resource generation rewards coordination, and whose chain-of-command position places them directly between Vellpryte's authority and Azimuth's execution. They are not the most dangerous member of the unit in direct engagement. They are the member whose absence makes the unit structurally incoherent.

◈ The Handler-Operator Pairing

Keshet and Azimuth form Zenith's perimeter pair. Keshet calls the play through Mark, "Strike Now!", and positional direction. Azimuth executes it with force projection and reactive defense. The partnership works because Azimuth trusts outcomes — she executes without second-guessing the call — and Keshet trusts Azimuth's capability even when her methods (luck, absorption) are alien to Keshet's planning philosophy.

XIII. Character Arc

The Central Question

The fallen heroes they couldn't save, the self-taught methods that work but lack polish, and the question of whether any plan can truly account for chaos. // Arc summary, Zenith.md

Keshet's arc is about the relationship between control and outcome. They built their entire professional identity around the belief that sufficient preparation prevents loss. The inciting incident was proof that this belief has limits. Their response was to prepare more comprehensively — to build more contingencies, study more battles, know more about the enemy before the engagement begins.

The arc resolves when Keshet encounters a situation that is genuinely unplannable — not failed planning, but a scenario where the outcome is structurally outside tactical control — and has to choose between accepting that and being present for it, or continuing to analyze in a way that prevents them from being there when the people around them need them.

XIV. Relations

Executor Vellpryte — Senior Field Agent Active
Commanding officer; the person who decides when Keshet's plan is executed and when it is set aside. The working relationship is functional. The professional tension is structural: Vellpryte decides, Keshet plans, and occasionally those functions are in conflict. Keshet executes regardless. Whether that compliance is loyalty or constraint is a question the arc will eventually require them to answer.
Secretary Nomos — Communications & Support Active
The unit's social and intelligence arm. Nomos provides information Keshet cannot gather — interpersonal data, cultural context, the things that cannot be extracted from a Reason test. Keshet values this functionally and finds it epistemically uncomfortable: you cannot build a contingency around what someone might say next in an interrogation. Nomos's improvisation is a variable Keshet has not found a way to fully integrate into their planning models.
Operator Azimuth — Psionic Specialist Handler
Keshet calls the play; Azimuth executes it. The partnership is operationally effective and philosophically alien. Azimuth trusts outcomes. Keshet trusts process. They have reached a working accommodation: Keshet names the objective and the approach, Azimuth delivers the result, and neither of them interrogates the other's underlying methodology too closely. Both are aware this is sustainable until it isn't.
The Fallen Heroes — Names Not Filed Memorial
They came to Keshet's territory. They died there. Keshet writes their names in no official document and does not discuss them in briefings. The 12-page operational review of Case #ZEN-002 — the one Nomos filed — is one of forty-seven similar documents in Keshet's personal archive. None of them address the original incident directly. All of them are about it.
The Warden Territory — Former Charge, Now Altered Antecedent
Gone. Keshet does not use the word "gone" — they use "operationally irrelevant to current deployment." The wilderness they spent years learning to read, protecting species whose names they knew, interpreting terrain in the way Nomos reads a face — all of it is beyond saving now. The Danger Sense perk still activates in natural environments. The instinct is intact. The place it was built for is not.
