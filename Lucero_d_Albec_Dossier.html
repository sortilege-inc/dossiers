I. Identity & Cover

Lucero d'Albec is a devil — one of the native ancestry of the Seven Cities of Hell. Purple-skinned, ram-horned, winged. He was a Watch Officer. He had a partner. He had a beat, an oath, and a record clean enough that the frame had to be a good one. The frame was a good one.

The Watch ruled the murder his. He didn't wait to find out what evidence had been planted, by whom, or why. He left. He picked up a whip — a thing no decent officer carried — and started doing the work outside the law that the law would no longer let him do inside it. He has not yet cleared his name. He may never. The campaign roster also describes him as "dogged by his own family," a powerful devil family that wants him ruined — though whether the family wanted him framed, helped frame him, or has merely benefited from the framing is undetermined.

In Alloy, the Free City of Brass, his cover is precarious. Someone has named him as collateral on a defaulted debt. Either the Saffron Seal does not know who he is, or it knows exactly who he is.

III. Ferocity — The Heroic Resource

The Fury's heroic resource is Ferocity. It accrues steadily at the top of the round and spikes when Lucero is hurt — when he becomes winded, when he is dying. The mechanic rewards staying engaged through pain rather than withdrawing from it.

TriggerGain
Start of your turn1d3
First time each combat round that you take damage+1
First time you become winded or are dying in an encounter+1d3
◈ The Logic of Rage

Ferocity is the inverse of caution. The longer Lucero fights, the more he has to spend; the worse he is doing, the more violently he can answer. The complication's drawback — he stays alive to set things right — fits the resource: he becomes most dangerous when he is closest to falling.

IV. Devil Traits

Devils are the native ancestry of the Seven Cities of Hell — humanoids with red or blue skin in a wide variety of hues, from bright crimson to deep purple. Each is born with some hellmark: horns, a tail, cloven hooves, a forked tongue, fanged incisors, or wings. Lucero has horns and wings both. He has chosen two further trait selections beyond the baseline.

⚠ The Wings Tradeoff

At level 1, flying makes Lucero five points more vulnerable to all damage. Two rounds in the air, and he comes down whether he wants to or not. The wings are not yet a free movement option — they are a tactical choice with a sharp cost. The weakness goes away at level 4; for now, the air is for emergencies, escapes, or a single decisive strike from above.

◈ Whip Geometry

The whip's reach (+1 melee distance) combined with Extension's pull means Lucero is dictating where the fight happens. He stands two squares away from the target, lashes them in, and now they are adjacent — to him, to where he wants them, in his Ferocity loop. The vertical pull is the elegant detail: an enemy flying or perched above gets yanked down to his level, and the wings make the inverse true (he goes up to theirs).

⚠ The Loadout's Logic

All three abilities are melee main actions; all three roll on Might; all three are about either dragging an opponent into position (Impaled grabs; Extension Of My Arm pulls; Tide of Death sweeps a line) or trading his own pain for theirs (Blood for Blood). Combined with Guilty Conscience's free-action Recovery at deep-negative Stamina, and Ferocity's spike when winded, this is a character built to escalate through injury. He fights harder the worse it gets.

XI. Complication — Guilty Conscience

From the Draw Steel complication: "The world is in trouble — and it's partly your fault. Maybe you helped a villain rise to power or inadvertently released a demon from imprisonment. Now it's your mission to repair the damage you caused."

AspectEffect
Guilty Conscience BenefitYou're determined to stay alive so you can set things right. When your Stamina reaches the negative of your winded value, you can use a free triggered action to spend a Recovery.
Guilty Conscience DrawbackMany people blame you for the evils you caused. They may be unfriendly or hostile to you — and you can understand their point of view. You take a bane on any test made to interact with those who know what you did, and on strikes made against such creatures.
⚠ The Open Question

Two slots in Lucero's backstory carry guilt-of-causality language: the Complication ("it's partly your fault... helped a villain rise to power") and the Inciting Incident ("the murder of your partner"). They are not the same event. Either Lucero is carrying two sources of guilt — the partner's death and something else he caused before or after — or the GM and player will want to reconcile them into a single, more complex backstory. The mechanical drawback (banes on tests against people who know what you did) implies there are people who know — which is a useful detail to track.

The family may have orchestrated the frame, benefited from it, or be unrelated.

XIII. Position in Collateral draft

◇ Provisional · Drawn from Campaign Concept

The following is drawn from the Collateral campaign summary, which is explicitly marked as draft / provisional. Details are subject to change in Session Zero.

The premise: the player characters wake in the same holding cell in Alloy and are told "The debt has defaulted. You belong to the Saffron Seal now." Lucero is by far the worst-positioned character to wake in a cell. He was last in a cell — or close to one — when his partner died and the Watch came for him. Waking captive in a new jurisdiction is exactly the situation he has spent months avoiding.

Role on the Team

Combat: frontline melee and lockdown. The whip's reach, Impaled's grab, and Extension Of My Arm's pull let him control where the enemy stands. Ferocity climbs as he takes hits, and Unearthly Reflexes converts incoming damage into shifts that keep him moving. He is the unit's primary damage threat and the person most willing to take a hit to land a counter.

Out of combat: investigation. The Intrigue suite (Alertness, Hide, Track, Pick Lock, Pick Pocket, Sabotage, Search) is the deepest of any character in the roster. He is the unit's instinctive detective — which is also the role he is most personally invested in, since his own case is unsolved. Silver Tongue gives him a credible secondary face role for negotiations.

Faction Connections

Per the campaign concept, three factions touch Lucero's situation: the Watch Officers of the Seven Cities of Hell (former employer, would arrest him on sight), Lucero's family (powerful, wants him ruined), and the Saffron Seal (now holds him as collateral). Any combination of these may turn out to be the same actor.

XIV. Character Arc

Two questions sit at the center of Lucero's arc.

The first is vindication: can he clear his own name, find his partner's killer, and survive what his family wants done to him? The Inciting Incident hands him this thread directly.

The second is the question the Complication makes mechanical: what else has he done, and to whom does he still owe a debt of conscience? The Guilty Conscience drawback presumes there are people who know — people who blame him, whose presence imposes banes. The Inciting Incident's frame job does not by itself produce those people. Something else does.

Underneath both: the cop and the criminal. He was the law; the law made him an outlaw. He carries a whip now — a weapon associated in his own culture with the sort of person he used to arrest. He is good at the work either way. The campaign asks whether he can stand to remain in the second life if the first life turns out to be uncleared, or whether some part of him will accept that the only way back is to stop running.
