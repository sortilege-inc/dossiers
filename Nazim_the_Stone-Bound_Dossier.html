On the Subject

Nazim was born in a felt-walled aul on the high steppe, the son of a herdsman named Yusuf and a mother who could read the cracks in a fire-heated shoulder-blade. He showed early the patience that the Stone Scribes look for — the kind of child who would sit for hours tracing the wind-carved grooves on a balbal stelae until he could close his eyes and feel the letter-shapes under his fingertips. A passing scholar of the Qara Bitig tradition — the ancient circle of arcanists who read the Old Turkic runic inscriptions left on kurgan-stones by the Göktürk and Uyghur khaganates — noticed him at thirteen and took him for an apprentice. He spent twenty years on the road since then, riding from one weathered stelae to the next: the Orkhon inscriptions, the Bilge Kagan stones, the half-buried Uyghur memorial pillars west of the Tien Shan. He has copied so many inscriptions into his spellbook that the book itself has become a kind of small kurgan — a stone in leather, carrying the voices of the dead.

In the cities along the Silk Road he hires himself out as an oracle — reading star-signs, casting bones, and now and then deciphering a real inscription for a merchant or governor who suspects their land has older bones than they thought. He keeps a thin Muslim veneer in Samarkand and Bukhara because it is easier than arguing, but his real loyalty is to Tengri's eternal blue sky and to the chain of qara bitigchi who came before him.

He is mid-career: old enough to have outlived his first master, young enough that the road still feels like an answer rather than a verdict.

School of the Stone Scribe — Empires of the Silk Road
Secrets of the Stone
Level 2 · Subclass core

When Nazim encounters an inscription bearing a spell that belongs to his arcane tradition, he can use his action to touch the inscription and memorize the spell. He can memorize spells this way a number of times equal to his Int mod (4 / long rest); each memorized spell can be cast once without expending a spell slot. Once such a memorized spell is expended, he cannot cast it that way again — but he may scribe it into his spellbook in half the normal time and at half the normal material cost. If it is a ritual, he can cast it as a ritual in half the given time (after which the memorized copy is spent).

Shared Memory
Level 2 · Subclass · bonus action · 20 ft

As a bonus action, Nazim can choose a bard, cleric, druid, sorcerer, or another wizard within 20 ft. and choose one of his own spell slots of 5th level or lower. That slot counts as expended for him, and for the next 10 minutes the chosen caster may use that slot to cast any appropriate spell of their own.

When he shares d10 — on a 10 the slot is not actually expended for him, and the ally still gets the benefit.

Free language utility: he also knows comprehend languages and can cast it without expending a slot 4 / long rest (= Int mod). He may also cast it normally with a slot, and it does not count against his prepared list.

Background — Âlim

Scholarly Tradition

Thanks to his studies, Nazim can recognize through which means to find a specific piece of knowledge, or how such knowledge could be lost to ages. In practice: if it can be known, he knows where to start looking — which library, which stelae, which memory-keeper to ask.

Event I · d20 = 11

Lost something or someone important.

His first master — dead and buried east of Samarkand under the kurgan whose inscription Nazim has not yet been strong enough to go and read. This is the bio's central bond.

Event II · d20 = 10 → d6 = 4

Met a great poet of his time.

During his travels, Nazim crossed paths with a known poet of the Silk Road era. Plausible: a Persian Sufi poet at a Samarkand court, or a Turkic ozan whose songs preserved old Tengrist inscriptions. The poet recognized what Nazim was reading on the stones and gave him a piece of language he hadn't seen before. GM-named.

Event III · d20 = 18

Throughout his life, he always felt he was being followed.

Some nights on the road between kurgans, he has heard a second set of footsteps in the snow that stop when he stops. He has never seen who. He has a strong suspicion — connected to the master's unread capstone — but he does not speak of it.

Event IV · Arcane · d20 = 4 → d8 = 2

Saw a mage conjure some kind of otherworldly entity.

He watched, did not speak of it, did not approach. He has read inscriptions about such bindings since.

Event V · d20 = 7

Made an enemy of an adventurer.

Some rival qara bitigchi who reads the same stones for a different patron and resents Nazim's access to the Qara Bitig fragment. GM-assigned.

Event VI · Forced Arcane · d8 = 6

Found a magical item, used it, lost it.

A minor scribe's tool he found in a half-collapsed kurgan early in his apprenticeship — a stylus that briefly let him read any inscription regardless of language. He used it for a year and then it broke. He thinks his master may have ensured it broke. He is not yet sure.

Profession

Silk Road PEX system · Apprentice rank

Oracle
Apprentice · Rank 1 PEX 0

Cold Reading (free) — Insight proficiency, usable for fortune-telling checks; at GM's discretion may be rolled with Intelligence as well as Wisdom.
Basic Astrology (free) — By learning a person's birthday and birth-hour, he can determine their star-signs (DC 10 Int (Nature) check; DC 15 without the hour) and learn the personality summary the GM provides.

Nazim earns PEX in play by reading hands, predicting weather, charting star-maps, and the like. Higher-rank Oracle abilities — deeper readings, true divinations, the binding of small omens — fit his trajectory but cost PEX he has not yet earned. His Oracle hangs over the Stone Scribe like a useful disguise: a man who can read your fortune is welcome at any caravanserai; a man who reads kurgan-stones is not.

Personality

Trait

"I measure a place by what is carved into its oldest stone, not by what its current rulers say about themselves. I will excuse myself mid-conversation to copy down an inscription on a doorpost."

Ideal — Continuity · Neutral Good

"The chain of those who can read the old stones must not be broken. Whatever else I do, I must one day teach what I was taught."

Bond

"Somewhere in the steppes east of Samarkand stands a kurgan I have not yet read — the burial-mound of the master who taught me, with his own final inscription carved on its capstone. I have not yet been strong enough, in any sense of the word, to go and read it."

Flaw

"I cannot leave a half-translated inscription alone. I have walked into more than one bad situation because there was one more glyph to puzzle out before I left."

Connections

  • Aymara Flameshaper — Two summers ago, Aymara's clan camped beside a half-buried kurgan-stelae two days west of Samarkand. Nazim, asking permission to read the inscription before the clan moved on, was let stay three nights. He traced rubbings by lamp-light, ate at their fire, and left Aymara's grandmother a folded paper covered in his careful Old Turkic glyphs — a copy of the line her grandmother had been worrying at. The clan that let him stay three nights was kinder than most, and the granddaughter — small for her age, then, with eyes that watched the fire too long — was the only one who asked him a question while he worked. She asked what the line said. He told her: "The road is older than the rider." She nodded as if she had known. He thought about that nod for months. When he came back the following spring to re-read the stelae alone, he hoped, foolishly, that the clan would still be camped there.
  • Farideh al-Saffar — They met at the House of Wisdom in Baghdad seven years ago, when she was nineteen and still her father's apprentice, and he was passing through to consult a manuscript on Old Turkic kurgan-inscriptions. She sat down in the reading-stall next to his; he looked up, nodded, and went back to his work. He does remember her. He has remembered her for seven years. He has not told anyone, and would not know how to begin telling her, and is increasingly aware that he has now also failed to tell her for seven years.
