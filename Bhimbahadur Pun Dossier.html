I. Identity & Station

Havildar Bhimbahadur Pun of the 5th Gurkha Rifles — Bhim to those who know him — is a thirty-four-year-old Magar from Baglung District in the western hills of Nepal, currently on detached duty as orderly to a distinguished English gentleman in London. He has been in London since 1884. He intends to remain. The English household sometimes confuses him for a valet; the correct word, and his own, is orderly, and he accepts the confusion with the patient, flat-eyed courtesy of a man who has decided that correcting English people on matters of nomenclature is below the useful expenditure of will.

He enlisted in the Gurkha Rifles at eighteen. He went up the frozen ridge at Peiwar Kotal with Roberts in December 1878 and came out with the Indian Order of Merit, Third Class. He served through the Second Afghan War, through the long winter occupation of Kabul, marched the 313 miles to relieve Kandahar in August 1880, and rose to Havildar in the quieter years that followed. He took the London posting in 1884 because it promised a life in which none of what happened in the winter of 1879–80 would matter any more.

The world is unlikely to honour that intention.

Physical Description

Short, densely built, clean-shaven, with the economical stillness of a career non-commissioned officer. He carries himself as men do who have learned to wait without fidgeting through long cold watches and longer marches. The kukri on his belt — worn as everyday regimental dress, on the same logic that a Sikh sepoy wears his kirpan, a cultural accommodation his officer sanctions and quietly deploys as a display of imperial bona fides — is maintained with the same impersonal care he brings to everything he owns. He wears his regiment's colours somewhere in every outfit. His hands are wide-fingered, unhurried, and extremely competent.

Manner of Presence

He speaks English haltingly and economically — far more halting than his comprehension — and has learned the significant advantage of being systematically underestimated in London drawing rooms. His silence reads as deference to those who want it to. He does not correct that impression. Among people he trusts, the silence is somewhat warmer but no more loquacious: he is a man of actions and small specific gestures rather than declarations. He does not perform emotions. He does not, as a rule, explain himself. The only exception he has made in five years in London is the iron nail under his shirt, which he will acknowledge, briefly, if someone who has earned the right asks about it.

Standing Personal Disposition

Yī mutu rākha. "Keep one heart" — the Magar phrase for internal consistency under pressure, which he has taken as personal doctrine since the winter of 1879–80
Havildar Pun distinguished himself throughout the advance by the calm and precision of his fire and the steadiness of his manner under conditions which disordered men of longer service. I commend him without reservation. Extract from a field officer's recommendation, December 1878 — Peiwar Kotal, Second Afghan War

Skill Pattern Worth Noting

His skill sheet has one conspicuous absence: almost nothing in the formal intellectual column. Library Use 20. No Archaeology, no Medicine, no Science, no Accounting beyond base. He is not the party's researcher or analyst. He is the party's scout, fighter, field medic, mountaineer, and the man who spots the thing in the dark that everyone else missed. He is also, very quietly, the man who has already seen something that most of the party has not.

The Occult 15 is worth noting: it is not high enough to be a deliberate investment, but it is higher than pure base, which means something happened between 1878 and now that pushed the number.

III. Operating Disposition

The Pulp Cthulhu archetype assigned to Bhim is Steadfast, which is accurate in the specific sense that archetypes can be accurate: it names the surface. What it does not capture is that the steadfastness is not temperamental stoicism. It is chosen. He is a man who has decided, with deliberate will, to be a man who holds. The distinction matters in play because chosen steadfastness is vulnerable in ways that natural stoicism is not: it can be reached by the right pressure in the right place, and once reached, it does not recalibrate gradually. It recalibrates in a single moment of either catastrophic collapse or confirmed resolve.

In a Fight

He is very good at this. The 75-skill weapons cluster — kukri, rifle, brawl — and the HP 27 / Tough Guy combination make him the most durable and among the most dangerous investigators in any standard party. His tactical instincts are twenty years of frontier service: he identifies terrain, chooses ground, waits for certainty before committing, and when he commits he commits entirely. He does not hesitate. He does not show off. The Beady Eye talent means he can shoot into melee and he knows it; he will not wait for clean shots when a difficult shot will end things faster.

In a Library or Study

He is not this. Library Use 20 and EDU 27 do not make him useless in research settings — they make him dependent on other investigators to translate and interpret, which means he is very useful for protecting the people who are doing the reading. He is also, specifically, the person in a research session who will notice the sound outside the window, the draft under the wrong door, the shadow that moves before anyone speaks to it.

In a Social Situation

In London society he is treated as a curiosity at best and as furniture at worst, and he has concluded that the furniture role is more operationally useful. He does not draw attention to himself. He does not assert himself in conversations that do not require him to. His English is halting enough that people who want to say things they would prefer the orderly not to understand will say them in front of him. He understands significantly more than he speaks, and he remembers everything.

With the Party

He is loyal to the people he has decided are worth loyalty, and his decisions on that question are slow, careful, and nearly irrevocable once made. The significant people on his sheet — Mehr Din Awan (dead) and Jaswant Singh (whose whereabouts are not recorded) — are both men he served with in Afghanistan. The speed at which he extends that same quality of loyalty to the new ensemble is a player decision, but the pattern is that he extends it to people who demonstrate competence under pressure and do not lie to him. He can tolerate a great deal from people in those categories. He cannot tolerate very much at all from people who are not in them.

IV. Interior Architecture

What He Believes

His religious and metaphysical frame is Magar animist layered under the Hindu structure his family observed, further complicated by twenty years of service alongside men of every confession in the Indian Army — Muslim sepoys, Sikh NCOs, Christian officers whose God he respects without joining. He is not syncretically confused: he has a clear and practical sense of the world's spiritual geography, which includes the understanding that spirits exist, that the boundary between the living and the dead is permeable, and that certain objects and practices carry protective power. His jhankri's nail is not superstition to him. It is engineering.

What the Kabul winter added to this framework is not disbelief in any of the above but a kind of upgraded threat assessment. He knew that things existed beyond the usual spiritual inventory. He did not know they could wear the faces of the dead. He knows that now. He has filed the information away in the part of his mind that handles frontline intelligence about enemy capabilities and tried not to think about it further. London makes this easier than Afghanistan did. He has not felt the nail grow cold since he left Nepal.

What He Wants

Genuinely, simply, persistently: a life in which what happened in the Kabul winter does not happen again. He wants the nail to stay warm. He wants to serve his officer competently, earn his wages honestly, and find a way to live in London until the memories age to the distance of other men's stories. He is not ambitious. He is not curious about the unnatural. He is the last person in any room who wants to be involved in a Cthulhu Mythos investigation. He will become involved anyway, because that is the shape of the campaign, and he will handle it the same way he handles everything that threatens what he has built: by holding on and doing the work and not showing how hard it costs him to hold.

What He Carries That Does Not Show

The Kabul winter of 1879–80 constitutes something that in CoC terms we might describe as a resolved Temporary Insanity that was never formally treated and has not fully resolved — the sanity cost is in the ledger already, taken in the field, managed through exactly the mechanisms a jhankri and a career soldier would bring to bear (iron, action, physical labour, the passage of time). The walls are good. They are not permanent. A second encounter — especially any encounter involving a body that should be dead walking around in the shape of itself, or any sensory trigger from that valley — is going to stress-test them in ways that the campaign is unlikely to be polite enough to avoid.

Sanity Pressure Point

His starting Sanity of 64 represents not a naive figure but an already-damaged one. He is not coming into the campaign fresh. He is coming in with a single very large Sanity cost that he has spent five years managing. The question is not whether he will encounter the Mythos — he already has — but whether the second encounter will compound the first into something his architecture cannot contain, or whether it will confirm his existing framework and allow him to function as a man who has already been tested and already knows what to do.

V. Languages & Literacy

His linguistic profile is the most complex in any likely party and is directly useful for a campaign rooted in empire. He is a man of the British Raj's frontier — Nepali, Hindi, Pashto, and the tribal languages of the western hills are in him at varying levels of fluency. English is his most recent acquisition and the only one he can be said to speak poorly. He reads and writes better than he speaks in most languages except English.

The other man who was there. The Sikh NCO who fought alongside Bhim against the thing that wore Mehr Din's face, who helped with the dismemberment and the separate burials, and who served with him through the relief of Kandahar. Last known posting was to a garrison in the Punjab. Bhim has not made an effort to locate him and does not know whether this is because he is afraid of what finding him might mean or simply because some witnesses are better left to their own silence. The unnamed distinguished English gentleman whose household Bhim serves as orderly. The nature of this officer is campaign-specific, but the relationship is the structural reason Bhim is in London rather than in Nepal or in a Gurkha cantonment. The officer sanctions the kukri on the belt. He uses Bhim's visible distinctiveness as a social marker. Bhim serves him loyally and without resentment and has not yet had occasion to test whether the loyalty runs in the other direction.

The man who prepared the iron nail in 1881, after Bhim returned from the Afghan campaigns and before the London posting. The relationship is one of the few on Bhim's sheet that is explicitly supernatural: the jhankri is the person who formally acknowledged what Bhim encountered, gave it a framework in Magar animist cosmology, and provided the single piece of protective technology Bhim carries against a recurrence. He is in Nepal. He cannot be consulted in London. His instruction — the nail grows cold when one of those things is near — is Bhim's entire existing protocol for detecting the Mythos.

VIII. The Iron Nail

He wears an iron nail under his shirt — drawn from the outer timberwork of a small Buddhist temple in the Magar hills and prepared by his village jhankri in 1881, on a black cotton cord against his breastbone. The jhankri told him it would grow cold if such a thing ever came near again. He has not felt it grow cold since he left Nepal. He checks it the way a man checks his watch. He does not find this abnormal.

The nail is listed as a Treasured Possession, which in CoC terms means it does what Treasured Possessions do: its presence provides a psychological anchor, and its loss or damage would constitute a Sanity cost. In this case the cost would be compound: the loss of the object and the loss of the only warning system he has.

The nail is also, in an operational sense, the campaign's most useful piece of Mythos detection equipment currently held by any investigator. It does not tell him what kind of thing is near, only that something is. He knows, from the one time the jhankri's instruction would have applied, that it got cold in the vicinity of the thing in the valley. He does not know whether it responds to all entities or only to the class of entity he encountered there. He does not know its range. He has never tested it and devoutly hopes he never will.

Mechanical Treatment

The nail's cold-warning function is not on the character sheet as a formal ability, which means its operational use is a GM determination. The backstory establishes the mechanism and the precedent; the campaign determines how it responds to the specific Mythos entities in Empire of Shadows. As a rule of thumb: if it worked on whatever was in that Afghan valley in 1879, it works on things of comparable or greater wrongness-of-being. Whether the entities Bhim encounters in this campaign meet that threshold is a dramatic question better left to the table.

IX. The Kabul Winter of 1879–80

In a high stone valley between Kabul and the Shutargardan Pass, during the long occupation of the Afghan capital in the winter of 1879–80, a patrol was ambushed. The details of the ambush are not on the character sheet and have never been spoken aloud. What is known, from the backstory: Mehr Din Awan, Punjabi Muslim Havildar, was killed and buried facing Mecca in frozen ground. And then, some days later, Mehr Din Awan came back.

He is listed in the backstory as a Significant Person. The thing that wore his face is listed under Encounters with Strange Entities as the entity that wore his friend's face in the winter of 1879–80 near Kabul. The action taken against it is explicit: Bhim and Jaswant Singh killed it, dismembered it, and buried the pieces in separate pits under piled stones.

This is the operational core of Bhimbahadur Pun's character. He is not a man encountering the Mythos for the first time. He is a man who encountered it once, managed it with the tools available to him — physical violence, folk protective practice, silence, time, geography — and moved to a city far enough away that the management might hold. He knows something. He has not articulated it in any framework that would make sense to a Western occultist or a Cthulhu Mythos investigator. He articulates it in the language of his own cosmology: there are things that can wear the dead. Iron helps. Separate pits. Piled stones.

What He Does Not Know

He does not know what it was. He has no Mythos framework. He has Occult 15 and an animist cosmology that has room for it but no specific name. He does not know if there is one of them or many. He does not know if what the campaign will show him is the same kind of thing or something different. He does not know if the methods that worked in the valley — dismemberment, iron, separate pits, piled stones — are generally applicable or specifically local. He filed it and closed the file and moved to London, and he will carry all of that unprocessed knowledge into Empire of Shadows as potential, not information.

The Sanity Ledger

His starting Sanity of 64 is not the base POW × 5 of an uninitiated investigator. The gap between POW 65 (base Sanity 65 before reductions) and 64 is small because five years' distance and careful management have allowed him to function, not because the wound was small. The winter of 1879–80 cost him something — possibly a single large loss taken in the field and partially recovered through the mechanisms available to him at the time, possibly a series of smaller costs over the weeks it took to manage the situation. Either way: the ledger is already open. He brings partial damage, partial resources, and one piece of specific prior knowledge into the campaign. He does not bring fresh eyes.

XI. Possible Trajectories

Bhim enters the campaign as a man who has already survived one encounter with the Mythos and is doing everything in his power to ensure there is not a second. He is also, by the mechanics of a Pulp Cthulhu investigation, going to encounter it again. The interesting question is not whether he encounters it — he will — but what happens to the architecture he has built to manage the first encounter when the second forces a confrontation.

The Quiet Threads

  • The nail grows cold. The first time the nail warns him is the campaign's most significant mechanical event for this character. It is not a Sanity roll. It is a choice: does he tell anyone, does he act immediately, does he pretend it did not happen? The nail is the instrument of his prior knowledge and his prior knowledge is, in the CoC tradition, both his greatest advantage and his deepest liability.
  • Jaswant Singh. The other witness is a thread the campaign may or may not pull. If he is findable — if the campaign takes the party into contexts where a Sikh ex-NCO from the Second Afghan War might surface — the reunion is structurally significant. The only person alive who knows what Bhim knows. The only person to whom Bhim does not have to explain the dismemberment and the pits and the stones.
  • His English, improving. EDU 27 and Language (English) 27 are the primary constraints on his social movement in London. As play continues and the campaign develops, the characteristic experience of immigrant language acquisition — understanding more than one can speak, the vocabulary of the specific domain one inhabits growing faster than general fluency — is available as a texture worth playing. He will not become an English gentleman. He may become a man who moves more freely through English London than his skill score currently allows.

The Loud Threads

  • Prior knowledge, formally deployed. At some point the party will encounter something Bhim recognises and the other investigators do not. He has experience they do not have. He has methods they have not considered — physical violence, iron, dismemberment, separated burial, piled stones — which are not in any Miskatonic University field manual and which may or may not work on the specific entities the campaign provides. The moment he offers that knowledge, and the moment the party has to decide whether to take it seriously, is one of the structurally interesting scenes available in this dossier.
  • The second Sanity crisis. His current Sanity of 64 represents a partially-repaired structure. A second large encounter — especially one that involves anything reminiscent of the Kabul winter, the specific wrongness of a dead thing wearing a live face — is likely to produce either a confirmed collapse or a confirmed resolution. Either is dramatically interesting. The collapse version is the obvious tragedy; the resolution version is more unusual: a man who has been carrying a wound that long, confirmed in his reading of it and given the tools to act, becomes something different from a traumatised survivor. He becomes a practitioner. The campaign gets to decide which version he is.
  • The Empire of Shadows context. A Nepali Gurkha ex-NCO in a campaign subtitled Empire of Shadows is not a decorative detail. The shadows are partly the shadows of empire; Bhim moves through both the imperial system (as soldier, as orderly, as the man the officer puts on display) and outside it (as a Magar in London, as a man whose cosmology does not require Western validation, as the person who cannot book a hotel room). His double position — insider enough to be useful to the investigation, outsider enough to move through spaces the English investigators cannot — is the structural advantage his Credit Rating 9 and his languages and his rank in the imperial military all converge to produce.

The Tested Edges

  • Loyalty against the campaign's demands. He is loyal to his officer; the officer is in some sense what keeps him in London. If the investigation compromises that relationship — if knowledge of the Mythos becomes a thing the officer cannot be shielded from, or if the officer is compromised by the campaign's antagonists — the structural ground he has built his London life on shifts. He will hold as long as holding is possible. The question is what happens when it is not.
  • The methodological collision. His Mythos toolkit is animist-practical: iron, dismemberment, piled stones, prepared objects, village jhankri's work. The other investigators may have Occult skills, Cthulhu Mythos points, library access, Western occult frameworks. These methodologies will not always agree, and the moments when they conflict — when his jhankri-derived protocol says one thing and a Miskatonic-trained investigator says another — are the moments that test both the intellectual architecture of the party and the social architecture of his position in it. He is not likely to defer on this subject. He has more data points than anyone in the room.
  • What the nail tells him that he does not tell the party. The nail is private. Its warning function is private. If it warms when the party approaches something and he does not speak — if he uses the warning to position himself advantageously without explaining why — the tactical advantage is real and the moral texture is complicated. The campaign will produce situations where telling the party about the nail is the right call and the hardest call simultaneously.

What He Will Not Be

He will not be the party's leader. CON 90 and Spot Hidden 70 and HP 27 make him the party's anchor and its scout and the last thing standing when the fighting is over. He is not the party's speaker, its theorist, its planner, or its social interface with London respectability. He is the thing between the party and the thing that wants to kill them, and he is very good at that role.

He will not be an enthusiastic convert to Mythos investigation. Every session he spends gathering Cthulhu Mythos points is a session he is spending in a direction he chose London specifically to avoid. His reluctance is not cowardice — nothing in his sheet resembles cowardice — it is the considered judgment of a man who assessed the cost-benefit ratio of knowing about this in 1879–80 and found it unfavourable. The campaign will require him to update that assessment. He will update it slowly and with visible cost.

He will not be unchanged by what the campaign shows him. A man who has seen what he has seen and managed it the way he managed it does not emerge from a Mythos investigation as the same person who entered it. He will acquire Cthulhu Mythos points against his will and his judgment. His Sanity will be tested at a level that engages the damage already in the ledger. His cosmology — which was already correct in its general shape, which already had room for things that should not be — will be revised, deepened, possibly broken and rebuilt. What he will be at the end of the campaign depends on what the campaign costs him. He entered it as a man who survived once by doing the work and saying nothing. Whether that is still sufficient is what the table gets to find out.

What Makes Him Interesting to Play

He is the investigator who already knows. Not fully, not in any framework that translates cleanly to Western Mythos scholarship, but in his body and his bones and his jhankri's cord against his breastbone. He is the investigator who took the posting to London specifically to avoid this. He is the investigator who carries HP 27 and Tough Guy and Spot Hidden 70 and Survival (Alpine) 60 and will use all of them, without drama, in the service of people he has decided are worth it. He does not perform heroism. He does the job.

He is also the investigator whose prior encounter with the Mythos is the most directly analogous to what Masks of Nyarlathotep contains — not in specific entity type, perhaps, but in fundamental wrongness: something wearing a face it should not have, something that required physical violence and practical folk magic and determined silence to manage, something that left a wound that has been managed rather than healed for nine years. He is the party's institutional memory of what it costs to encounter this and survive. He is also the party's warning that surviving is not the same as being all right.

The nail is warm. For now.
