I. Identity & Station

Havildar Bhimbahadur Pun of the 5th Gurkha Rifles — Bhim to those who know him — is a thirty-four-year-old Magar from Baglung District in the western hills of Nepal, currently on detached duty as orderly to a distinguished English gentleman in London. He took the posting in 1884. The English household sometimes confuses him for a valet; the correct word, and his own, is orderly.

He enlisted at eighteen. He went up the frozen ridge at Peiwar Kotal with Roberts in December 1878 and came out with the Indian Order of Merit, Third Class. He served through the Second Afghan War and the long winter occupation of Kabul, marched the 313 miles to relieve Kandahar in August 1880, and rose to Havildar in the quieter years that followed.

He took the London posting because it promised a life in which what happened in the winter of 1879–80 between Kabul and the Shutargardan Pass would not matter any more. He has every intention of keeping the past where he left it — an intention the world is unlikely to honour.

Physical Description

Short, densely built, clean-shaven. He carries himself with the economical stillness of a career non-commissioned officer. The sheet records his traits in two words — resolute, loyal — and offers no other physical detail, which is appropriate to him: he is a man whose surface is the whole of his social offer to people who have not earned more.

He wears his kukri on his belt as everyday regimental dress, on the same logic that a Sikh sepoy wears his turban and kirpan — a cultural accommodation his officer sanctions and quietly uses as a display of his own imperial bona fides.

Manner of Presence

He speaks halting English. He speaks Nepali, Kham Magar, and Hindustani fluently or well, and gets around in Pashto. His literacy in English and Nepali is very poor; he reads and writes professional Devanagari (the script he uses for Hindustani) and, to a lesser extent, Nastaliq. The gap between what he understands in English and what he can produce is wider than English people typically assume, and he has learned not to correct the assumption.

Standing Personal Disposition

The character sheet records his traits in two words — resolute, loyal — and assigns him the Pulp Cthulhu archetype Steadfast. The rest is in what he carries and what he does not say. Phobias and manias: none listed. Ideology and beliefs: none listed. Meaningful locations: none listed. Arcane tomes and spells: none. Magic Points: thirteen. Spells: none. Iron nail on a black cotton cord: one.

Skill Pattern Worth Noting

The high-skill cluster forms a coherent portrait of a frontier rifleman. The absences are equally coherent: Library Use 20, Persuade 10, Psychology 10, and almost everything in the formal intellectual or social-manipulation column at or near base. He is not the party’s researcher, talker, or analyst. He is the party’s scout, fighter, field medic, mountaineer, and the man who notices the thing in the dark that everyone else missed.

First Aid 60 is unusually high for a non-physician and is backed by the Burroughs Wellcome Tabloid Medical Chest and field surgical case he carries — he is the party’s field medic whether he intends to be or not.

III. Operating Disposition

The Pulp Cthulhu archetype assigned to him is Steadfast. The sheet’s only other personality data are the two trait words — resolute, loyal — and the absences in the other character-sheet fields (no phobias, no manias, no ideology, no meaningful locations). The portrait is built by negative space: he is what is left when the spaces for declared anxieties and declared beliefs are deliberately empty.

In a Fight

The 75-skill cluster — kukri, rifle, brawl — with HP 27, Build +1, and Tough Guy makes him a durable and effective combatant. Beady Eye lets him fire into melee and at small targets without the penalty die. He is the investigator most likely to still be standing at the end of a fight.

In a Library or Study

He is not this. Library Use 20 and EDU 27 do not make him useless in research settings — they make him a man best deployed elsewhere while research is happening. He is, however, specifically the person who will notice the sound outside the window, the draft under the wrong door, or the shadow that moves before anyone speaks to it. Spot Hidden 70 does not turn off because the party is reading.

In a Social Situation

In London society he is treated as a curiosity at best and as furniture at worst. His halting English and Credit Rating 9 mean he does not draw social attention even when he is in the room. His comprehension is wider than his production, and people who want to say things they would prefer the orderly not to understand will say them in front of him.

With the Party

The two Significant People on his sheet are Mehr Din Awan (dead) and Jaswant Singh (no current location on file). Both are men he served with in Afghanistan; both are connected to what happened in the Kabul winter. The pattern that emerges is not a wide network of friendships but a small one of bonds forged under specific pressure. How quickly he extends comparable loyalty to the new ensemble is a player decision, but the sheet’s pattern suggests that loyalty is something he gives, not loans.

IV. Interior Architecture

What He Has Observed

The character sheet leaves Ideology & Beliefs blank, which is honest to him: he is not a man whose interior architecture is a matter of declared creed. What he holds is closer to observation than belief. The boundary between the living and the dead is permeable; certain objects and practices carry protective power; the jhankri’s nail is not superstition to him but engineering. These are conclusions drawn from one specific experience and one specific village authority, not articles of a faith.

What the Kabul winter added to this framework was not a new metaphysical category but an upgraded threat assessment. He has filed the information away in the part of his mind that handles intelligence about enemy capabilities and tried not to think about it further. London has, until now, made this easier than Afghanistan did. The nail has not grown cold since he left Nepal.

What He Wants

The Notes section on the sheet states it plainly: he took the London posting in 1884 because it promised a life in which the Kabul winter would not matter any more, and he has every intention of keeping the past where he left it. He is not curious about the unnatural. He is the last person in any room who wants to be involved in a Cthulhu Mythos investigation. He will become involved anyway, because that is the shape of the campaign.

Sanity Note

His starting Sanity is 64 against a POW × 5 maximum of 65. The single point lost is from session one of play, not from anything historical. The Kabul winter of 1879–80 has not yet been paid for on the sheet — whether because the loss was managed in narrative time before play began, or because the cost is still notional and waiting, is a matter the campaign and the table will resolve. The point to note is mechanical and conservative: he comes into play almost full on Sanity, with one prior Mythos encounter on the record but no Cthulhu Mythos skill points yet.

VIII. The Iron Nail

He wears an iron nail under his shirt — drawn from the outer timberwork of a small Buddhist temple in the Magar hills and prepared by his village jhankri in 1881, on a black cotton cord against his breastbone. The jhankri told him it would grow cold if such a thing ever came near again. He has not felt it grow cold since he left Nepal.

The nail is one of two Treasured Possessions on the sheet (the other being the kukri). Its mechanical treatment is the standard Treasured Possession protocol: its presence is a psychological anchor and its loss or destruction would carry the usual costs of that loss. The cold-warning function is not on the sheet as a formal ability; the backstory establishes the mechanism and the precedent. How it interacts with the specific entities of Empire of Shadows is a question for the Keeper.

What is on the sheet is the single sentence: the jhankri told him it would grow cold. That is what he knows. He does not know its range, he does not know whether it responds to all classes of entity or only the one he encountered, and he has had no occasion to test it since 1881.

IX. The Kabul Winter of 1879–80

In a high stone valley between Kabul and the Shutargardan Pass, during the long winter occupation of the Afghan capital, the patrol was ambushed. Mehr Din Awan, Havildar, was killed, and Bhim buried him facing Mecca in frozen ground. Some days later, a thing wearing Mehr Din’s face came back. Bhim and Jaswant Singh, a Sikh Havildar, killed it, dismembered it, and buried the pieces in separate pits under piled stones.

That is what the character sheet records, in the Notes section and again under Encounters with Strange Entities. It is the operational core of the character. He is not an investigator encountering the Mythos for the first time. He is an investigator who encountered it once, dealt with it through the tools available to him — physical violence, folk protective practice, dismemberment, separated burial, the jhankri’s nail prepared after his return — and moved to a city far enough away that the management might hold.

What He Does Not Know

He has no Mythos framework. He has Occult 15 and Cthulhu Mythos 1, both at or near base, and the protective protocol his jhankri gave him. He does not know what it was. He does not know whether what the campaign will show him is the same kind of thing. He does not know whether the methods that worked in the valley — dismemberment, iron, separate pits, piled stones — are generally applicable. He brings prior experience without prior theory.

What the Sheet Shows, and What It Does Not

The mechanical residue of the Kabul winter is conspicuously light. Sanity 64 is essentially full. Cthulhu Mythos is 1. There is no phobia, no mania, no ideology recorded. The encounter is in the Notes and in the Significant People; it is not in the numbers. The campaign may treat that as a feature (he managed it well) or as a delayed bill (the cost is still owed). The dossier neither asserts nor invents a story here; the table will tell which.

XI. Possible Trajectories

Bhim enters the campaign as a man who has had one prior Mythos encounter on his record and is doing what he can to keep the past where he left it. He will encounter the Mythos again because that is the shape of the campaign; what follows is not prediction but a sketch of the lanes his sheet and his stated intentions make available.

The Quiet Threads

  • The nail grows cold. The first time the jhankri’s warning applies in play is the most significant single event the dossier can predict for this character. It is not a Sanity roll. It is a choice: does he tell anyone, does he act on it alone, does he say nothing?
  • Jaswant Singh. Listed as a Significant Person with no current location. Whether the campaign brings him back into Bhim’s reach is a thread the table may or may not pull. He is the only other living witness.
  • His English, slowly improving. EDU 27 and Language (English) 27 are the primary constraints on his social movement in London. The familiar shape of immigrant language acquisition — comprehension widening faster than production, the vocabulary of one’s actual domain growing first — is available as a texture as play continues.

The Loud Threads

  • Prior experience deployed. At some point the party will encounter something Bhim recognises and the other investigators do not. He has methods that are not in any Western occult manual: physical violence, iron, dismemberment, separated burial under piled stones, an object prepared by a village jhankri. The moment he offers that experience, and the moment the party has to decide whether to take it seriously, is one of the structurally interesting scenes available in this dossier.
  • The Sanity ledger opens further. Starting Sanity 64 is essentially intact. A serious Mythos encounter is likely to start drawing on the ledger in earnest, and how it draws — whether it confirms the existing framework (he has been here before; the methods still work) or breaches it (this is not the same kind of thing; iron does not help) — is one of the more interesting dramatic axes the campaign can move along.
  • The Empire of Shadows context. A Nepali Gurkha ex-NCO is not a decorative figure in a campaign with that subtitle. Bhim moves through the imperial system (soldier, orderly, his officer’s visible accommodation) and outside it simultaneously (a Magar in London, Credit Rating 9, a cosmology that does not need Western validation). His double position is the structural advantage of the sheet.

The Tested Edges

  • Loyalty against the campaign’s demands. The sheet trait is loyal, and the structural ground he has built his London life on is the relationship with his officer. If the investigation compromises that relationship, the ground shifts. He will hold as long as holding is possible.
  • The methodological collision. His protocol is jhankri-derived and concrete: iron, dismemberment, separated pits, piled stones, a prepared object on a cotton cord. Other investigators may bring Western occult frameworks, library research, Cthulhu Mythos points. The moments when those methodologies conflict are moments that test both the party and his position in it.
  • What the nail tells him that he does not tell the party. The nail is private. Its warning function is private. If it warms when the party approaches something and he does not speak — if he uses the warning to position himself advantageously — the tactical advantage is real and the moral texture is complicated.

What He Will Not Be

He will not be the party’s leader, speaker, theorist, or social interface with London respectability. CON 90, Spot Hidden 70, HP 27, Tough Guy, and the 75-skill weapons cluster make him an anchor and a scout. He is the man between the party and the thing that wants to kill them.

He will not be an enthusiastic convert to Mythos investigation. Every session he spends acquiring Cthulhu Mythos points is a session he is spending in a direction he chose London specifically to avoid. His reluctance is not cowardice — the sheet has no room for that — it is the considered judgment of a man who already assessed the cost-benefit of knowing about this and concluded against. The campaign will require him to update the assessment. He will update it with visible cost.

The nail is warm. For now.
