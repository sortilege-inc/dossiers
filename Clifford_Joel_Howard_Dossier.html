V. Background & Origins

Clifford Joel Howard was born in Boston, Massachusetts, to parents who had relocated from Eastern Europe. In that earlier life they had been Jewish socialists — participants in a tradition of radical political thought rooted in a specific community, a specific language, a specific set of convictions. That tradition ended when they came to America.

The name change — Horowitz to Howard — was part of a deliberate erasure. The family downplayed their Jewish heritage and their socialist politics in order to integrate into the east coast middle class. Clifford grew up largely ignorant of this history. He was raised in a household that had performed assimilation thoroughly enough that the seams were invisible to him.

From the Character Journal (Foundry VTT)

"Parents relocated from Eastern Europe. They were Jewish socialists in Poland, but have since tried to put all that behind them, changing Horowitz to Howard, downplaying their Jewish heritage while celebrating Judeo-Christian values and American exceptionalism, integrating into the east coast middle class in Boston."

He came to federal investigative work — and to the Radiance — through routes the character sheet does not specify. What is established is that Investigation and Research are his primary trained competencies, with Courtesy and Bureaucracy also deliberately developed. He navigates institutions and finds things out. The Radiance found that useful enough to assign him to Nashoba.

What he is beginning to unravel there is the history he was never given. The community he has been sent to investigate has maintained exactly the kind of continuity — syncretic, stubborn, centuries-deep — that his own family voluntarily surrendered. The comparison has begun to register, even if he cannot yet fully articulate it.

VI. The Nashoba Situation

Howard has been in Nashoba — marked on official Massachusetts maps as "Crossfield," a designation the residents do not use — for several months. He was sent to investigate a group called the Called, which the Radiance designated as a dangerous cult. He is increasingly sceptical of that designation.

The Community

The people of Nashoba are in fact several peoples, assimilating syncretically over hundreds and thousands of years. Their culture extends a particular welcome to children with all manner of mental and physical differences. Howard has been getting to know Maude, who runs the local diner and organises a knitting circle that benefits the local orphanage. Overall the community seems quite healthy, if idiosyncratic.

The continued influx of "undesired" children from outside also serves a practical function: it helps keep an otherwise insular community from the dangers of inbreeding. The orphanage is not only charitable — it is structural to the community's survival across centuries.

The Called

The Called are, from Howard's perspective, a cult of sorts — but not in the sense his briefing suggested. They are a celebration of fecundity and, indirectly, a tribute to Shub-Niggurath, though Howard does not yet have that name. Their nominally Presbyterian church is remarkably light on references to Christ or his Father, and focuses much more on the wonders and varieties of creation.

The Shonokin

The ancient Shonokin predated the indigenous peoples of this area. Some members of the community still exhibit the slitted eyes and other, stranger abilities that mark Shonokin descent. Howard is encountering people and phenomena for which his Radiance training has no category.

Setting Context — Weird of Hali

The Weird of Hali novels by John Michael Greer invert the standard Lovecraftian framing: the eldritch entities are not the threat. The threat is the rational, scientific, imperialist forces that seek to suppress or destroy them. Howard is an operative of exactly those forces, stationed in a community that has coexisted with the non-human for centuries. His growing scepticism about his assignment is the character's central dramatic question.

VII. The Radiance & The Handler

Howard works for the Radiance — but what the Radiance is precisely, he is not sure. What he does know is that his handler has been responding to his field reports from Nashoba with orders that are growing darker and stranger, and that he finds increasingly difficult to carry out.

The Distrust His Handler passion at 57% is the highest score on the sheet — higher than his drive toward community belonging, and higher than his personal feeling toward Maude. The mechanical ordering is significant: Howard's most acute tension is institutional. He has not yet refused his handler directly. He is getting there.

The character is a deliberate inversion of the investigator-in-a-strange-town template drawn from Twin Peaks and True Detective. The detective arrives expecting to find something wrong. He finds instead that the strangeness is not the danger — and that his own presence, and the orders he is carrying, may be the most threatening element in the situation.

VIII. Psychological Profile

The three passions read together tell a coherent story: a man whose institutional loyalty is eroding (Distrust His Handler, 57%), who is drawn toward a kind of belonging he was never given (Seek Acceptance in a Community, 48%), complicated by a specific person who embodies the community he is investigating (Desire Maude, 37%). The character was built to be caught between these pulls.

POW 6 and zero Voorish Points establish Howard as someone who has not yet encountered the genuinely uncanny. Six Rationality Points confirm the same: he is operating entirely within the rational-materialist framework his career and upbringing have provided. Both are about to be tested by Nashoba.

The comparison Howard is beginning to make — between Nashoba's centuries-deep communal continuity and what his own family surrendered in a single generation — is the personal stake underneath the plot stake. He is starting to unravel the family history he was not given, by watching what it looks like to have kept it.

Player's Design Note (verbatim)

"Clifford is a federal investigator, but also works for the Radiance, although what that is precisely he isn't sure about. He's been in Nashoba for a few months now, working an investigation into a mysterious cult that he's increasingly skeptical about the dangers of... Clifford is increasingly unsure how to navigate their hospitality, or the darker and stranger orders that keep coming in from his handler in response to his reports. In a way, what he is seeing is a continuity of community that he never had."

IX. Open Questions — Unresolved at Campaign Start

  • What precisely is the Radiance, and what does it want from Nashoba?
  • What are the "darker and stranger orders" Howard's handler is sending — and when will Howard have to refuse them directly?
  • What is the nature of Maude's relationship to the Called and to the community's deeper traditions?
  • The Shonokin: some community members exhibit slitted eyes and stranger abilities. What do those abilities amount to in play?
  • How much of his family history is Howard actually aware of, and how much remains to be uncovered through the comparison Nashoba is forcing him to make?
  • Howard has zero Voorish Points and six Rationality Points. When the genuinely uncanny presents itself directly — what breaks first, and what replaces it?
  • Shub-Niggurath is the indirect object of the Called's veneration. Howard does not yet have that name. When he does, what follows?
