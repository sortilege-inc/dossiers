I. Standing & Bearing

Samuel Hornton owns and works The Occident, the principal saloon of Fallstaff. He pours, he listens, he remembers. Most things in town pass through his bar before they pass into the street, and Sam is the kind of proprietor who makes sure of it — not by demand but by attention. He keeps Hilary Duval in booze and women. He keeps Robards' Gang in whiskey and women. He keeps Phebe Souder paid to watch and listen. He kept the jailhouse standing after the fire. He keeps a soft spot for Finneus Varner, the old prospector. He keeps Ota Bouvier closer than anyone else, and he keeps Chicago Toluth alive in trade for a debt that hasn't run out.

By manner Sam is courteous, watchful, and unhurried — an Al Swearengen-coded operator, dressed in dark pinstriped vest and shirtsleeves, hair swept back, mustache and small beard kept exact. He runs the room from his end of the bar with a shot glass between two fingers and an eye on every door. He smiles when he means to. He keeps his voice low when he means it most. He has a gift for naming what a person needs before they have admitted it to themselves, and a corresponding gift for making them feel they have chosen freely.

Manner of Presence

The civility is not a mask — it is the trade. Sam genuinely likes most of his clientele, in the way one likes a profitable seam. He does not raise his voice and does not need to; the room watches him for cues. When matters turn hard he becomes quieter, not louder. The hardness is in the eyes.

II. Values

In this conversion of Cortex Prime / Smallville to the frontier, the six Values are Grit, Authority, Grace, Justice, Truth, and Love. Each carries a personal statement — the lie or truth Sam tells himself about it.

Reading the Sheet

Grace at d10 is the loudest number on Sam's values, and his statement is unmistakably operational: people who love him are useful, and he knows it. Justice at d4 is the floor — Sam does not believe the universe is keeping score, which is precisely what frees him to keep his own. Truth at d8 with the "fool's gold" statement is the engine of every con: he doesn't lie about whether things are true, he lies about whether they matter.

The Shape of the Pool

Connected at d10 plus Not Born Yesterday at d8 is the working hand: Sam is hard to swindle, and he knows the man who knows the man. Popular d8 and Clever d6 round him out as a public figure rather than a back-room operator. Nothing on this list is martial. He fights with the room.

VII. What Happened in Play

The campaign — "Western" — ran two to three sessions and ended cleanly. The detailed beats are no longer recoverable; what follows is the shape of the thing as it can be read off the sheets, the relationship map, and the final positions of the pieces on the board.

The Standing Situation
Two parcels, two ambitions, one saloon in the middle.
Zephaniah Delmar — the Robber Baron, holder of Shady Valley Ranch — was openly looking to expand onto Lot 295, the Homesteader Averin Dale's parcel. The Sheriff was minded to protect the homesteader. The Thief was Kitty Clayton's family. Sam Hornton, from the Occident, was tied to all four men and played each one at a different angle of the same triangle.
Sam's Position Going In
"To be treated with care."
Sam's read on Delmar at start was a d6 — not an enemy. Delmar's read on Sam was a d8: useful if he's on my side. Both men entered the table believing they could use the other. Only one of them was correct.
In the Middle
The town's quieter machinery.
Ota Bouvier did the listening. Phebe Souder kept the tabs. Hilary Duval drank what Sam poured. Chicago Toluth stayed on the leash of an old debt. Marian Krick ran the rougher half of Fallstaff in parallel. Sam ran the bar, the room, and the conversation — and somewhere along the way arranged matters so that the question of who would end up holding the deed to Lot 295 had a third, unexpected answer.
The Resolution
A shootout, a transferred deed, and a saloon still standing.
By the time the final guns came out, Sam was not in front of them. Bodies fell. Sam was not among them. When the smoke cleared, the Occident was still in his name — and Lot 295 had quietly changed hands. The Homesteader's parcel, the one the Robber Baron had been trying to swallow, ended up Sam Hornton's. A deal's a deal.
The Mechanical Throughline

The result is exactly what the sheet predicts. Sam's working hand is Connected d10 + Not Born Yesterday d8 + Truth d8: a man hard to swindle, a man who knows the right men, and a man who deploys truth as currency rather than principle. Grace at d10 ("when people love you, they'll go through hell for you") supplied the levers; Justice at d4 ("if God's in his heaven, He ain't looking down here") supplied the permission. Nothing on his sheet was set up to win a shootout, and so the resolution — on his end — was not one.

VIII. The Ledger Closed

Sam Hornton walked out of "Western" with the things he started with and one thing he did not: the parcel the powerful man had wanted. The Robber Baron is dead or done. The Sheriff has his town back, more or less, and is probably no longer Sam's d4 relationship. The Thief, against the odds, lived. The Homesteader's lot is on the Occident's books.

What the Sheet Was Always Pointed At

  • The triangle of Grace d10, Truth d8, and Love d8 — Sam never lied about what was true; he made people want what he wanted. The d10 in Grace was always going to be where the win came from.
  • Connected d10 as the spine — every meaningful turn in this game ran through a person Sam already knew or could borrow from Marian. The d12 trigger ("gain a 2d8 Location you'd normally not have access to") is, in retrospect, almost on the nose.
  • Justice d4 as the floor — the lowest number on the sheet is the one that made the ending possible. A man who scored the universe differently would not have walked out of the same room.

If There Were Another Session

The d4 Sam carried for Otto McVey ("a pain in the ass; should have died years ago") survived the campaign. Otto did not die. That is an unresolved bill, and Otto's own d8 on Sam reads playing too many sides. The Thief is one of the two people in town who has Sam's measure. The other is Ota Bouvier, and her account is squared. Otto's is not.

And there is the matter of Audrey Warris, who is still not happy that her brother Chicago works for Sam. Lot 295 raises the rent on that particular grievance.
