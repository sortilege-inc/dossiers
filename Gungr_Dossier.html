I. Identity & Calling

Gungr is Hakaan — descended from a tribe of giants in upper Vanigar, whose first ancestors traded some of their gigantic size and strength to Holkatja the trickster god in exchange for the ability to see the future. The Hakaan are, in spite of this lineage, a friendly and outgoing people. Their rare presence in human society is taken as a harbinger of dark times, which is sometimes accurate and sometimes the prejudice of people who confuse large with ominous.

Gungr's class is Talent — born with the potential to harness psionic power, and woken to it by some defining event. His discipline is Chronopathy, the psionic practice of viewing future and past events and manipulating time. The match between his ancestry's bargain (future-sight) and his class's subclass (time manipulation) is not coincidence: he is who the gift made.

He spent his adult life as a Warden, protecting a wild Vanigar region from those who would harm it. The Portents — a sequence of signs culminating in a great beast dying at his feet — told him to leave that work and fight a battle for the fate of all lands. He gave up the only life he had ever known. He has not yet arrived at the battle. He has, instead, arrived at a holding cell in Alloy, where someone has named him as collateral on a debt he did not sign.

A Note on the Spelling

The .ds-hero source spells the ancestry Hakaan. The Collateral campaign summary uses the variant Haaken. Both refer to the same Vanigar giant-descended people. This dossier follows the character sheet's spelling.

III. Clarity — The Heroic Resource

The Talent's heroic resource is Clarity: the disciplined attention that lets him hold the world's strands long enough to bend one. Unlike most heroic resources, Clarity can be spent below zero — pushed into the red at the cost of strain, which has lingering mechanical consequences.

How Clarity Builds

TriggerGain
Start of your turn1d3
First time each combat round that a creature is force moved+1

Spending Below Zero (Strain)

Gungr can spend clarity he does not have, pushing the resource into negative numbers to a maximum negative value equal to 1 + his Reason score (so −3 at L1). At the end of each of his turns, he takes 1 damage for each negative point of clarity. Whenever he has clarity below 0, he is strained. Some psionic abilities have additional effects if he is already strained, or become strained when he uses them. Strained effects can still impact him even after he is no longer strained.

Outside of combat: Whenever he uses an ability with a strain effect outside combat, he can take 1d6 damage and incur the effect if he doesn't incur it for other reasons.

◆ The Bargain Made Mechanical

Clarity's negative-spend is the Hakaan ancestral deal in miniature. The trickster gave the giants future-sight in exchange for some of their size and strength; Gungr can have more psionic effect in exchange for hurting himself, and for becoming subject to compounding strained effects. He pays in advance. The character is built to pay.

V. Doomsight — Special

⟢ Trait Text · Verbatim

Working with your Director, you can predetermine an encounter in which you will die. When that encounter begins, you become doomed. While doomed, you automatically obtain a tier 3 outcome on tests and ability rolls, and you don't die no matter how low your Stamina falls. You then die immediately at the end of the encounter, and can't be returned to life by any means.

If you don't predetermine your death encounter, you can choose to become doomed while you are dying with the Director's approval (no action required). Doing so should be reserved for encounters in which you are dying as a result of suitable heroism, such as making a last stand against a boss or saving civilians.

◆ How This Reads in Play

Doomsight is the most powerful narrative trait on any sheet in the cell, and it can only be spent once. The character has been told, by signs that produced a literal great-beast omen, that he is meant to fight a battle for the fate of all lands. Doomsight is the mechanical permission to make that battle happen — to call the moment, walk in already-dying, and obtain tier-3 outcomes on every roll until the encounter ends. He will then be dead. No resurrection. The trait converts the Portents into a single redeemable promise.

◆ The Loadout's Shape

The two signatures cover offence and control: Hoarfrost for damage with a slow effect (or a restrain if he eats the strain), Kinetic Grip for pure displacement. The 3-Clarity ability protects an ally and converts incoming damage into free counterstrikes — a strong fit for a Talent fighting alongside the cell's melee characters. The 5-Clarity is the rescue button: an ally gets 10 temporary Stamina and a stability buff for one fight, and Gungr accepts (potentially) losing maneuvers until he saves. Every selection is built to be spent strained, which is the point of choosing a Talent at all.

XI. Complication — Primordial Sickness

From the Draw Steel complication: "You once contracted a terrible illness for which no one could find a cure. You sought out a primordial swamp said to be either incredibly poisonous or miraculously salubrious. It turned out to be both, keeping your illness at bay while corrupting your body with its unnatural energy."

AspectEffect
Primordial Sickness BenefitDamage Immunity: Poison 5, Corruption 5. The swamp's energy makes him resistant to the kinds of harm that produced the illness in the first place.
Primordial Sickness DrawbackHis Recoveries are reduced by 1 (8 → 7). His body keeps the illness at bay but cannot fully mend itself.
⚠ The Body's Long Negotiation

The complication is mechanically lean — one immunity, one penalty — but narratively heavy. Gungr is sustaining the illness, not curing it; the swamp's bargain works only as long as the corruption holds. Combined with Doomsight (a predetermined death) and Clarity's strain economy (self-damage as an option), the character carries three separate self-cost mechanics. He is built to pay.

XII. Inciting Incident — Portents

From the Draw Steel inciting incident: "There were signs. You tried to ignore them, but when a great beast died at your feet, you had to recognize the truth. You were meant to leave your home territory, meant to fight a battle for the fate of all lands — and so you gave up the only life you've ever known."

◆ What's Established
  • The signs came over a period of time. Gungr tried to ignore them.
  • A great beast died at his feet. This was the moment he could not ignore.
  • He was meant to leave his home territory in Vanigar.
  • He is meant to fight a battle for the fate of all lands.
  • He gave up his life as Warden — the only life he had ever known — to do this.
  • He has not yet arrived at the battle.
⟢ The Sign and the Trait

Portents and Doomsight are the same idea in two registers. The Inciting Incident says he was sent to fight a battle for the fate of all lands. The ancestry trait says he can predetermine an encounter in which he will die. Mechanically, Doomsight is what allows the Portents to be redeemed: when the right battle comes, he can call it and become unstoppable for exactly that fight. The character is built around a single arc with a single ending. The campaign's job is to make that ending earn its setup.

XIII. Position in Collateral draft

Provisional · Drawn from Campaign Concept

The following is drawn from the Collateral campaign summary, which is explicitly marked as draft / provisional. Details are subject to change in Session Zero.

The premise: the player characters wake in the same holding cell in Alloy, the Free City of Brass, and are told "The debt has defaulted. You belong to the Saffron Seal now." Of the cell's prisoners, Gungr is the one most explicitly in a between-state: he was sent on a journey, has not arrived, and has now been intercepted on the way. The frame of the campaign — being held by a creditor he never signed a contract with — reads to him through the lens of the Portents: is this on the path, or is this a delay?

Role on the Team

Combat: ranged controller and ally booster. Mind Spike at 12 squares; Hoarfrost for cold damage and slows; Kinetic Grip for pure displacement; Iron for ally protection. The character is built to fight at distance, redirect enemy movement, and keep front-liners alive — and to spend his own Stamina (via strain) when the rolls don't go his way.

Out of combat: wilderness scout, behavioral read, and supernatural diagnostic. Track, Navigate, Climb, Handle Animals, Read Person, plus Psionics and Monsters for identifying what's actually being done with magic. The campaign hook ("an ancient chronopathic rite is being exploited by an unknown hand") is in his specific area of expertise.

Faction Connections

Per the campaign concept, the relevant faction-shape for Gungr is the orchestrator of the chronopathic rite — described as "an unknown hand" exploiting an ancient practice. That orchestrator is a candidate for who named him as collateral. No other listed faction is explicitly tied to his backstory.

XIV. Character Arc

Three threads pull through Gungr's sheet.

The first is the Portents: he was sent to fight a battle for the fate of all lands. He has not arrived. The cell, the Saffron Seal, the chronopathic rite — the campaign will have to decide whether these are obstacles to the battle or are the battle. Doomsight is the redeemable promise either way.

The second is self-cost. Three separate mechanics — Primordial Sickness (−1 Recovery), Clarity strain (−damage for over-spend), Doomsight (predetermined death) — all model a body that gives more than it has. Whether the costs build toward a single climactic spend, or wear him down across the campaign's arcs, is a play question. The mechanics support both.

The third is the warden in a city. He was the protector of a wilderness; he is now in the densest urban node in the timescape. The career perk doesn't work here. Two of his cultural skills don't quite apply. The campaign asks him to be useful in a context his life did not prepare him for — and the answer the sheet gives is at distance, with foresight, and at cost.
