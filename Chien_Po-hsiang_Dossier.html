Lineage & Formation 家世

The 錢 surname traces to the kings of Wuyue in the tenth century — an unbroken thousand-year lineage of scholars, and the weight of expectation that comes with it. A Qian man is expected to be learned. He was born around 1890 in a traditional Jiangnan courtyard compound in the Suzhou old city: walled, axial, a studio (書房) where his father worked, a garden with a single taihu stone and a plum tree that bloomed in late winter.

His given name, Po-hsiang (伯驤), pairs the generational marker for an eldest son with a rare literary character meaning a horse galloping with head held high — by extension, to soar, to rise nobly. His courtesy name, Chih-yüan (志遠), is drawn from Zhuge Liang's Admonition to His Son and turns the outward brilliance of 驤 inward, into the discipline of the long view.

He did his Shanghai schooling at St. John's, taking the train from Suzhou. Around 1910–1912 he sailed for France. He weathered the Great War there as a non-combatant student and served as interpreter for the Chinese Labour Corps in 1917–18 — and saw his countrymen treated as disposable labour by Europeans who called themselves civilized. He completed a doctorat ès sciences at the Muséum national d'histoire naturelle, working on Cretaceous and Jurassic plant fossils. He met Hélène in Paris in 1918, married her around 1919–1920, and brought her home to China in 1921.

He returned to Peking and a position under V. K. Ting at the Geological Survey of China, with an adjunct seminar at Beida's old Red Building. He runs a small paleobotanical unit. His office is near Fengsheng Hutong. He publishes in the Bulletin of the Geological Survey of China. His income is modest middle-class Chinese professional — enough for the courtyard rent, a cook, a housekeeper, French wine occasionally and good Chinese books constantly; not enough to travel luxuriously. He and Hélène take the train second class.

In Paris he knew the future Chinese Communist leadership — Zhou Enlai, Deng Xiaoping, Cai Hesen, the Li Shizeng anarchists — without joining them. His position is that of V. K. Ting and Hu Shih: China must be saved, but by science, education, and slow institutional reform, not by violence or imported dogma. He finds the Communists earnest and often likable; he finds their certainty unnerving. He is, in his own phrase, a patriot without an -ism.

In a locked drawer of his study he keeps a slim notebook of poems written in Paris — some in Classical Chinese, some in clumsy French. Several were written in 1919 when Versailles handed Germany's Shandong concessions to Japan and he was angrier than he has ever been in his life. He has not shown them to anyone, including his wife. He is not sure he recognizes the young man who wrote them.

The Party 同行者

Hélène Éloïse Bergeron Wife · Sociolinguist · Burgundy → Paris → Peking Deep love; the marriage carries political tension — her radicalism against his moderation — lovingly. They argue well; it is their favorite Sunday afternoon. She calls him Michel daily, Chih-yüan when she is serious. He has learned to hear what his wife is actually asking, not what she has said aloud.
Marie Halvor Boucher Pao Su-t'ien · 包素恬 · Medical Officer · Vancouver Mutual respect, careful navigation. He knows what missionaries can be — and what they can also be. Two serious people in a not-very-serious world; an unspoken solidarity is possible.
Subedar Gurdit Singh Bajwa Director of Expedition Security · Amritsar Professional respect, cautious warmth. Another man of dignity who has crossed cultures. Chih-yüan's political moderation may frustrate Gurdit — he will sense the paleobotanist sees the problem clearly and declines to act — but he will not push.
Prof. Wang Enlai Department of Geology · Peking University Senior colleague and the expedition's organizer. The man who countersigned the briefing and named Chih-yüan as lead Chinese scientific researcher in his note to Warner.
Langdon Warner American · Fogg Museum, Harvard · Wagons-Lits Hotel Frosty professional acquaintance at best. Chih-yüan knows precisely what men of Warner's profession have done to China's patrimony, and the knowledge sits in the room with them whenever they speak.
V. K. Ting · Ding Wenjiang 丁文江 · Founder, Geological Survey of China Mentor. Cambridge-trained geologist; the founding figure of Chinese earth sciences. The intellectual father of his political moderation.

Field Journal 行旅誌

3 September 1923 — Morning The Courtyard off Dongsi
Reading the briefing with Hélène. Both fail their History rolls; Pelliot's 1908 looting remains unknown to them. Hélène marks W-7, W-8, W-11 with a question mark — three coordinates deliberately unlabelled.
3 September 1923 — 3:00 P.M. Wang Enlai's Meeting Room · Geology Building
The four investigators meet for the first time. He listens. He asks one question: "This collector — is he still living?" Wang does not know. Warner did not say.
4 September 1923 — Afternoon His Office, Survey Building
Fenton's annotated Survey maps arrive smelling faintly of Scotch. East of Anxi, in the lower-right quadrant: a symbol partially erased — closed circular form with radiating lines, three characters beneath in no script he knows. He traces it exactly and folds it into his pocket. He has not shown it to anyone.
5 September 1923 — Dawn The Range
Marksmanship trial with Bajwa. First shot into the berm. Second wide. Third clipped the frame's outer edge. He sets the rifle down with the relief of a man completing an obligation.
6 September 1923 — Evening The Study, off Dongsi
Packing the specimen cabinets alone while Hélène is at the library. The notebook of poems stays in the locked drawer. The rubbing of his father's calligraphy stays on the wall. He looks at it once before deciding.
7 September 1923 — Morning Hôtel des Wagons-Lits · Legation Quarter
Meeting with Warner. Asked about the Library Cave, Chih-yüan speaks for ninety seconds — dates, names, quantities, level and unornamented. Forty thousand manuscripts. Two hundred and twenty pounds in silver. The facts editorialize themselves.
October 1923 Mogao Caves · Dunhuang
Wang Yuanlu meets them at the cliff face. Chih-yüan introduces the party without mentioning Warner. The old priest says: "你的老師是個好人." — your teacher is a good man. Later, at the painted quarters: "來找我. 不是他." — come to me. Not him.

The priest confirms the Russian soldiers' phrase predates the Russians by very much longer. "有些門, 不應該被打開." — there are gates that should not be opened. Chih-yüan asks if he stands watch at them. The reply: "我們都是. 有些人不知道自己在守護." — we all do. Some do not know they are guarding.
After lamps out Their painted quarters · ground level
He lies awake. He thinks about the phrase some do not know they are guarding. He thinks about the tracing folded in his field journal. He does not take it out.
